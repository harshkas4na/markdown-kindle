# Start Here

Two PDFs sit in the same folder, both describing the same ten months at the same employer, and they tell two different stories. One — call it the CV — leads with "advanced UK taxation expertise... covering VAT, Capital Gains Tax, Inheritance Tax, Income Tax, and Corporation Tax" and describes the job at Mercurius Advisory as IFRS-compliant reporting, multi-currency bookkeeping, and cross-border regulatory compliance for international clients. The other — the Resume — leads with "US tax compliance, ITIN processing, and multi-state registrations" and describes the same ten months as processing 50+ ITIN applications, registering LLCs and corporations across seven US states, coordinating EFTPS enrollment, and liaising directly with the IRS. Both are true. Both are the same job, at the same company, in the same ten months. The fact that there are two documents, each built to quietly delete the half of the story that doesn't fit the audience being pitched, is the actual problem this book exists to solve — not "which one is right," because neither is. The honest resume is the one that stops choosing.

## What this book is not

It isn't a generic "how to become ACCA qualified" guide, and it isn't a pep talk about picking a lane. It's an answer to a narrower, more specific question: given eleven of twelve ACCA papers passed, real UK tax and IFRS exam depth (ATX-UK and SBR — two of the four hardest Strategic Professional papers), and one job's worth of hands-on experience that happens to be almost entirely US-side, what is the actual, defensible story to tell UK-facing employers in 2026 — and what has to happen structurally, not just rhetorically, before that story lands.

## Your actual starting position, stated plainly

Strip the confusion and here's what's true on paper: passing ATX-UK and SBR isn't a small thing. They're two of the four hardest Strategic Professional options in the whole ACCA syllabus, and clearing both puts you ahead of a large share of people who call themselves "ACCA part-qualified." Ten months at Mercurius Advisory wasn't generic data entry — it was ITIN processing (a genuinely technical, IRS-regulated process most junior accountants never touch), multi-state US entity registration across seven states, EFTPS enrollment, and direct authority liaison, alongside IFRS-based reporting and multi-currency bookkeeping for international clients. That's real, specific, checkable work. The gap isn't competence. The gap is three separate problems that have been tangled into one, and only one of them is the framing question you've been circling:

1. **A framing problem** — one job, artificially split into two competing, incomplete resumes, when the actual differentiator on the market is the combination, not either half alone.
2. **A membership problem** — passing all thirteen papers (once SBL is done) makes you an ACCA *affiliate*, not an ACCA *member*. Full membership needs 36 months of supervised, signed-off practical experience (PER) on top of the exams. Right now, ten months in, it's worth finding out this week whether that clock has even started counting — and it depends on one specific fact about the old job that neither resume mentions checking.
3. **A market-timing problem** — the traditional path into UK-facing accounting (a graduate trainee seat at a UK practice, PER ticking away quietly in the background) is being visibly compressed by AI-driven hiring cuts at exactly the entry level you'd be competing at, at exactly the moment you're trying to enter it — and the UK visa math makes a fast physical relocation as a part-qualified candidate close to impossible regardless of how good the resume is.

## How to use this book

Chapters 1–3 build the diagnosis: the real decision underneath the resume question, the PER mechanics nobody explained clearly, and why the current UK entry-level market and visa math both argue against the obvious path. Chapters 4–6 build the alternative: the India-based UK-process channel that gives real UK-client experience without a visa, the specific US-UK cross-border tax niche your resume already accidentally half-qualifies you for, and the one additional credential that turns "I did ITIN processing" into a recognized qualification instead of just a resume line. Chapter 7 collects real people's accounts of nearly this exact confusion — PER-supervisor dead ends, "affiliate means nothing to most employers" threads, people weighing this exact dual background out loud. Chapters 8–10 are the execution layer: one resume instead of two, outreach scripts, and a 90-day plan that ties PER logging, exam prep, and applications together instead of treating them as three separate, competing projects.

One honest note on sourcing: this book draws on ACCA's own official pages on the Practical Experience Requirement, live UK job postings (including current Skilled Worker visa salary thresholds pulled directly from gov.uk), real Reddit and OpenTuition forum threads from ACCA candidates in comparable situations (named and linked where a thread exists), and named firms in the US-UK cross-border tax space. Some leads — specific outsourcing companies' actual ACCA-hiring practices in India, in particular — could only be partially verified in the research pass behind this book, and are flagged as leads worth checking directly rather than stated as settled fact. Career advice about "what UK firms really want" is one of the most confidently-asserted, thinly-sourced genres on the internet; where a claim here is solid, this book says why, and where it's a reasonable inference rather than a confirmed fact, it says that too.
