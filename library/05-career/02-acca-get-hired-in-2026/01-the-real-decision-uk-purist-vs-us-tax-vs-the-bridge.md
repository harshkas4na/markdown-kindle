# The Real Decision: UK Purist, US Tax Story, or the Bridge

The question underneath the two-resume problem isn't "which document do I send" — it's "which identity am I actually building toward." Three real options sit on the table, and you've already ruled one of them out by deciding the goal is the UK route, not a US accounting career. Worth walking through all three anyway, because the reasoning for ruling out Option B is exactly the reasoning that makes Option C the strongest read of "going UK" rather than a compromise on it.

## Option A: Go UK-purist — lead entirely with ATX-UK, SBR, and IFRS

This is what the CV version of the resume already attempts: bury the US-side work, present as a straightforwardly UK-focused ACCA candidate. The appeal is obvious — ATX-UK and SBR are real, hard-won technical depth, and ACCA is a UK-founded body, so this reads as the "natural" story. The problem is that it's theoretical strength with no experience receipt behind it. You've never actually filed a UK VAT return, prepared a CT600, or run a UK self-assessment for a real client — the UK tax knowledge lives in exam papers, not in ten months of client work. A UK hiring manager reading "ACCA part-qualified, ATX-UK and SBR passed" sees exam competence; a UK hiring manager reading "prepared VAT returns for 12 UK clients" sees experience. Those are different signals, and right now you can only offer the first one. Worse, this option means competing head-on for exactly the segment of the UK market that's contracting hardest right now — entry-level, generalist, UK-based trainee seats — a market chapter 3 will show is shrinking sharply under AI-driven hiring cuts at the Big Four and beyond. Leading with pure UK-theory-only, with no UK-client proof behind it, is the weakest version of the story you could tell into the toughest part of the market to break into.

## Option B: Go all-in on US tax — lead entirely with ITIN, EIN, multi-state, IRS liaison

This is what the Resume version does, and it's tempting for the opposite reason: it's your strongest *provable* experience. Fifty-plus ITIN applications is a specific, verifiable number. Seven-state entity registration is concrete. IRS liaison is a real, checkable claim. If the goal were "get any accounting job fastest," this might be the better lead. But it doesn't connect to the actual decision already made — ACCA, the UK tax paper, the UK-facing goal — and a resume that leads entirely with US tax work invites the obvious question "why did you study UK tax at all?" with no good answer inside that framing. This option is worth naming honestly, because it's the one that's easiest to drift into by default (it's simply the stronger story on paper), but it's off the table given where you've already decided to point this.

## Option C: Position the bridge — US-UK cross-border tax, on purpose

The strongest version of this candidacy on paper isn't "UK tax candidate" or "US tax candidate" — it's the specific, much rarer combination you already have real, provable proof of on both sides: **UK tax and IFRS depth from ACCA, paired with hands-on US federal and multi-state tax compliance experience.** This isn't a hedge between the two — it's a real, named market segment. Firms specializing in US-UK cross-border and expatriate tax exist precisely because this combination is scarce: USTAXFS describes itself as "the largest independent boutique tax accounting firm in the UK and Europe specializing in US tax," serving private clients, trusts, and corporations across US-UK-Switzerland cross-border work. Vialto Partners — PwC's former Global Mobility practice, now independent — does global tax and immigration work for people and companies operating across borders. Buzzacott and Frank Hirth are established UK firms built specifically around US/UK dual tax expertise. Greenback Expat Tax Services runs a fully remote, globally distributed team by design, because its client base is scattered exactly like yours would need to be. None of these are US firms asking you to abandon the UK route — they're UK-headquartered or UK-serving firms whose entire reason for existing is that most candidates only have one side of what you have on both.

There's a catch worth naming plainly rather than glossing over: several of these firms, on their actual job postings, gate on a US credential — CPA, Enrolled Agent, or a JD — not on ACCA by itself. ACCA plus real ITIN/EIN/multi-state/IRS-liaison experience gets a resume read with genuine interest in this space, but on its own it isn't yet the exact credential match their filters are built around. That's not a reason to abandon Option C — it's the reason chapter 6 exists: one additional, fast, exam-only credential closes that specific gap without requiring you to give up ACCA or the UK-facing goal.

## What this means for how you apply

Don't pick one identity and bury the rest — pick which half leads, per target, from the same true body of work:

- **Applying to a UK-process outsourcing firm or an India-based Big Four/mid-tier Global Delivery Centre working UK clients:** lead with IFRS, ATX-UK, SBR, and the multi-currency/cross-border compliance work. The US-tax bullets stay in, lower down, as evidence you can handle regulator-facing complexity — not as the headline.
- **Applying to a US-UK cross-border or expatriate tax specialist firm:** lead with the full combination, ITIN/EIN/multi-state/IRS-liaison bullets first, ATX-UK and SBR immediately behind them as the credential half of the same pitch. This is the target where the combination is the whole point, not a footnote.
- **Applying anywhere doing global mobility, international tax, or cross-border advisory more broadly:** same as above — this is the segment where "I've actually done US tax compliance work AND hold UK tax and IFRS credentials" is a genuinely rare sentence, and it's worth building the target list in chapter 5 around firms in exactly this seam rather than assuming there are only two or three of them.

The rest of this book is built around making Option C real rather than aspirational: fixing the PER clock (chapter 2), understanding why the market and visa math argue against Option A specifically (chapter 3), finding the India-based channel that builds real UK-client proof without a visa (chapter 4), mapping the cross-border niche in more depth (chapter 5), and closing the credential gap the niche's own job postings reveal (chapter 6).
