# The Enrolled Agent Unlock

Chapter 5 ended on a specific, unglamorous problem: the cross-border tax firms that would genuinely value the US-UK combination mostly screen applications on a US credential — CPA, Enrolled Agent, or JD — not on ACCA alone. Of those three, one is realistically reachable on a timeline measured in months, not years, doesn't require a US degree or residency, and directly converts the ITIN and IRS-liaison work already done into a credential these firms' own job postings are built around: the **IRS Enrolled Agent (EA)** designation, the federally-licensed status that grants unlimited rights to represent taxpayers before the IRS.

## The mechanics, confirmed directly from IRS.gov

- **Three exam parts**, each independent: Individuals; Businesses; Representation, Practices and Procedures. 100 questions per part (85 scored), 3.5 hours each.
- **Cost**: $317 per part — $951 total exam fees — plus a $140 enrollment application, roughly **$1,091 all-in**.
- Parts can be taken **in any order**, up to four attempts per part per testing window, and a passed part **carries over for three years** — there's no requirement to clear all three in one sitting or one year.
- **International candidates can sit remotely** via Prometric; the international testing window runs **September 1 through February 28** each year.
- No US residency or citizenship requirement was found anywhere in the official material.
- No evidence was found of an exemption or fast-track for prior tax-preparation or ITIN experience — treat it as the full three-part exam, not a shortcut, even with real hands-on background already in place.

## Why this is the right unlock, specifically for this situation

Set this directly against the PER math from chapter 2: EA is purely exam-based, with no supervisor to find, no employer's sign-off to chase, no 36-month clock, and no dependency on anyone else's willingness to vouch for the work. It's the one credential in this entire plan that converts real, already-lived experience — the actual ITIN applications, the actual IRS calls, the actual multi-state filings — directly into a recognized qualification on a self-directed timeline, rather than depending on finding the right employer or the right supervisor first. For someone who has already done the underlying work for ten months, that's a genuinely fast path to turning "did this at a job" into "licensed to do this."

It's also, specifically, not a pivot away from the UK. EA is a US credential, but it's aimed squarely at UK-headquartered and UK-serving firms — USTAXFS, Buzzacott, Frank Hirth, Vialto, Greenback are all either based in the UK or explicitly serve the UK/Europe corridor. Pairing ACCA (UK tax and IFRS depth) with EA (US tax practice rights) and the real ITIN/EIN/multi-state experience already on the resume is the credential version of the Option C bridge from chapter 1 — a specific, rare combination that speaks directly to what those firms' own job descriptions ask for, without requiring a choice between US and UK at all.

## Sequencing against SBL

SBL — the one ACCA paper still outstanding — and EA prep can genuinely run in parallel, since the two test almost entirely non-overlapping material: SBL is strategic, governance, and leadership case-study work; EA is US tax code, procedure, and representation rules. The risk isn't that they conflict — it's that SBL feels more urgent (it's the one paper standing between here and "affiliate"), and EA registration quietly slips as a result. The EA international testing window is time-boxed, September through February — worth locking in a study and booking plan for that specific window now, rather than discovering later that it closed while SBL revision took priority. Given where things stand, the next international window is close enough to treat this as an immediate planning item, not a someday one.
