# The US-UK Cross-Border Tax Niche

Chapter 1 named this as the bridge option — the identity that makes the US-side work an asset instead of something to hide from a UK-facing application. Worth going deeper on what this niche actually is, who's really hiring in it, and what it genuinely wants from a candidate, because the honest answer isn't uncomplicated enthusiasm — it's a real, valuable niche with one specific gate this book hasn't glossed over.

## The firms

**USTAXFS (US Tax & Financial Services)** describes itself, on its own careers page, as "the largest independent boutique tax accounting firm in the UK and Europe specializing in US tax" — a real, currently active firm serving private clients, trusts and estates, investment funds, and corporations across explicit US-UK-Switzerland cross-border work. It runs an "Early Career Program" aimed at people new to the profession, meaning it's built to bring people in before they arrive fully credentialed — a genuine on-ramp rather than a senior-only door. Its currently listed roles sit in London, Zurich, Geneva, and the Nordics, not India — worth noting because it implies this specific firm expects an eventual UK/Europe presence rather than offering a remote-from-India starting point.

**Vialto Partners** is PwC's former Global Mobility practice, spun out as an independent firm, doing global tax, immigration, and rewards mobility work for people and companies operating across borders — real, and hiring broadly ("from Adelaide to Zurich," per its own careers messaging), though its careers page doesn't spell out an India-specific or dual-US-UK-experience requirement in what this research could pull.

**Greenback Expat Tax Services** is genuinely useful as proof-of-concept: a fully remote, globally distributed team by design, because its client base — American expatriates scattered across the world — is distributed the same way. That's concrete evidence this niche does hire location-agnostic. The catch: its currently listed opening asks for a US CPA, IRS Enrolled Agent, or JD, with three-plus years already in expat tax specifically. ACCA alone doesn't clear that particular bar.

**Buzzacott** and **Frank Hirth** are both well-established, real UK-based firms built specifically around US/UK dual-tax expertise — this book's research pass couldn't freshly verify their current hiring specifics (site access issues), so treat them as firms worth checking directly rather than firms with confirmed open roles right now.

## The pattern, stated honestly

Across every firm that could be verified, the same shape repeats: **the niche genuinely values deep US tax compliance experience paired with UK or international credentials** — but the specific credential most of these firms actually screen applications on is a US one: CPA, Enrolled Agent, or JD, not ACCA by itself. ACCA plus real ITIN processing, multi-state entity registration, and IRS-liaison experience is a genuinely rare, differentiated story that will get a resume read with real interest here — recruiters in a niche this specific notice when someone shows up who's actually done the work rather than just studied around it. But on its own, right now, it's a story that opens the conversation rather than clearing the credential filter. That's precisely the gap chapter 6 exists to close — not by abandoning ACCA, but by adding the one credential these job postings are actually built around.

## The wider category, and an honest counter-voice

The niche is bigger than the boutique US/UK specialist firms above. ACCA's own careers board lists roles like Manager, Global Mobility Tax across London, Reading, Bristol, and Manchester, and a search of India-based "global mobility tax" listings on LinkedIn turns up roughly a hundred current postings — real, active hiring in the broader category, not a handful of niche firms. Worth pairing that with an honest counter-voice rather than pure enthusiasm: on r/Accounting, someone weighing a Deloitte Global Mobility Services offer describes worrying it's "a very niche process with limited exit opportunities." That's a fair concern to sit with, not dismiss — global mobility and cross-border tax work can become its own specialized track that's harder to pivot out of later, the same way any narrow specialism can. The honest framing isn't "this niche is obviously the answer" — it's "this niche is real, actively hiring, and rewards a combination almost nobody else applying has, and it's worth going in with eyes open about how specialized a long-term bet on it would be," which is a decision worth revisiting periodically rather than committing to blindly from chapter 1 onward.
