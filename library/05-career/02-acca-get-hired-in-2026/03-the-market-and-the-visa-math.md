# The Market and the Visa Math

Even with a perfect resume and a clean PER record, two structural forces work against the most obvious version of the plan — apply directly to UK trainee or part-qualified roles, hope for sponsorship, move to the UK. Neither of these is about how good the application is. Both are worth understanding in hard numbers before spending months pushing against them.

## The UK entry-level accounting market is contracting, right now, at exactly this level

This isn't a vague "AI is changing accounting" observation — it's showing up in hard hiring numbers at the exact rung of the ladder a part-qualified candidate would be competing for. KPMG cut its UK graduate intake from 1,399 to 942 (Accountancy Age, June 2025). PwC has said it plans 661 fewer entry-level hires by FY2028 — a 39% cut from current levels (Business Insider, August 2025). Entry-level openings across the Big Four overall are down 44% (filed.com's analysis of the shift). Adzuna data cited by The Finance Story shows UK graduate accounting job listings down 44% year-on-year. And a Financial Times analysis, cited in the same reporting, found that for the first time the Big Four are now posting more AI-related roles than audit roles. None of this means ACCA is losing value, and none of it means the UK accounting market is disappearing — it means the specific doorway Option A (chapter 1) would walk through — a generalist, entry-level, UK-based trainee seat, competed for as an overseas part-qualified candidate with no UK-client proof yet — is measurably the most contested, most AI-compressed part of the entire market to be entering right now. That's a reason to route around it, not a reason to give up on the profession.

## The visa math doesn't care how good the resume is

This is the harder, less negotiable constraint. UK Skilled Worker visa rules set a "going rate" salary a sponsoring employer must pay for a given occupation code, published on gov.uk's Immigration Rules Appendix Skilled Occupations. For SOC 2421 (chartered and certified accountants), the going rate is £49,200 a year. For SOC 2423 (taxation experts), it's £48,500. Neither code carries a shortage-occupation discount, so there's no reduced threshold to fall back on. Meanwhile, realistic UK salaries for a genuinely part-qualified candidate run roughly £20,000–£34,000 depending on exam progress and location, rising to about £25,000–£42,000 for someone further along — nowhere near the going rate a sponsor would legally need to clear. That gap is exactly why UK part-qualified job postings so often carry the line "no sponsorship available" — it isn't a judgment on the candidate, it's arithmetic a UK employer can't get around: sponsoring a part-qualified hire at typical part-qualified pay would mean paying £10,000–£29,000 above what the role normally pays, just to clear the government's floor.

This changes what "decided to go the UK route" should actually mean right now. It doesn't mean "apply for UK jobs and hope for a visa" — the going-rate math makes that close to structurally impossible at this stage, independent of resume quality. It means separating two decisions that have been fused into one:

1. **Building a UK-facing career** — a UK-relevant credential, a UK or cross-border client base, a specialism UK firms value — which is entirely achievable from India right now, with no visa required.
2. **Physically relocating to the UK** — realistic later, after full ACCA membership, when salary naturally crosses into a band closer to the going rate (newly qualified UK salaries run roughly £35,000–£60,000), and worth revisiting seriously at that point, not this year.

A real example of attempting the reverse order is worth reading, not as a template but as a live data point: on r/ACCA, one poster describes planning to move to the UK first and finish their remaining papers there via part-qualified-level jobs. That path exists and people do try it — but it means clearing the exact visa-sponsorship gap described above before a single exam-related advantage comes into play, which is a harder order of operations than building UK-facing credibility from India first and relocating once the going-rate math is no longer working against you.

Worth naming plainly: this isn't "no UK jobs exist." A search of ACCA's own careers board (jobs.accaglobal.com) turned up 628 open part-qualified roles in the UK at the time of this research — real volume, genuinely open. The issue is that a large share of that volume explicitly requires "the unrestricted, ongoing right to work in the UK," with sponsorship unavailable — confirmed directly on live UK job postings during this research. That's not a wall against the profession. It's a wall against the specific move of applying from India, unsponsored, this year. The next three chapters are about the moves that don't run into it.
