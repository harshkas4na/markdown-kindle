# Templates and Scripts

Every template used earlier in the book, collected here so you're not hunting back through chapters when you actually sit down to write outreach. Treat none of these as copy-paste — the specific detail in the brackets is the entire point; a filled-in template with the brackets left generic converts at the bad end of every range cited in this book, not the good end.

## Cold email to a startup founder (Chapter 3)

> Subject: [specific thing you noticed] — question
>
> Hi [Name] — saw you just shipped [X]. I built something similar for [Y project] and ran into [specific non-obvious problem]; here's how I solved it: [one sentence]. I've been [doing relevant thing — e.g., "shipping Solidity + Reactive Network automations solo, most recently REACTOR which got a $9K grant from the network"]. Would 15 minutes be useful before you fill your next engineering seat? Happy to send code either way.
>
> [Name] / [one-line portfolio link]

**Use when:** reaching a founder directly, via email or a Twitter/X DM, about a specific YC or early-stage company you've actually used or studied.

## Hiring-manager cold email (Chapter 5)

Structure lifted from Nick Singh's breakdown of cold emails that actually landed interviews (Periscope Data, Airbnb, Reddit): a specific accomplishment, a genuine personal connection if one exists, one explicit ask, 50–125 words total.

> Hi [Name] — [common ground sentence, e.g. "read your post on X" / a real shared connection]. I've been [proof point, e.g. "building production systems that wire LLMs into on-chain transactions — most recently a service handling real financial value with rate limiting and idempotency baked in"]. Would a short call make sense before [Company]'s [role] closes? Happy to share code or a walkthrough either way.
>
> [Name]

**Use when:** you've identified the actual hiring manager (not the recruiter) for a specific open role, via email rather than LinkedIn where possible. Follow up 2–3 times over the next two weeks — Singh's verified cases all involved more than one message.

## Sohan Sethi's referral-request template (Chapter 4)

> Hello [Name], I hope you are doing well. I am reaching out to request a referral and possible guidance to become a part of [Company]. I have [X]+ years of experience in [field] and specialize in [skill 1], [skill 2], [skill 3]. If someone who has already walked this path could guide me, it would help a lot. I am interested in the [job title] role at [Company]. Job ID: #[123456]. Let me know if you would be open to assisting with the referral and any additional information you'd need from my end. Thank you — it means a lot.

**Use when:** targeting a manager or senior IC (not an executive) with a couple of years of tenure at the company — Sethi's own targeting rule. Pair with a concrete proof point from your own work rather than a generic skills list.

## The Value Validation pitch (Chapter 4, Austin Belcak's method)

Not a message template — a different entry point entirely. Instead of asking for a referral or an interview, identify one real, specific problem or opportunity at the target company and send a short, concrete writeup of how you'd solve it, addressed to whoever could actually act on it. The ask (a referral, a call, an interview) becomes a natural side effect of the pitch being useful, not the thing you're requesting up front.

**Use when:** you have enough signal on a target company's actual product to identify a real gap — the higher-effort, higher-payoff move for your top 3–5 companies on the target list (chapter 5), not something to do for all 20–40.

## Informational-chat request (Chapter 4)

> Hi [Name] — I've been [specific true thing you're doing] and noticed you work on [their specific team/project] at [Company]. I'm exploring a move into [role type] and would genuinely value 15 minutes on how your team thinks about [specific, real technical question about their work] — happy to work around your schedule.

**Use when:** first contact with someone at a target company you don't know at all — lowest pressure, sets up the next message.

## Direct-but-earned referral ask (Chapter 4)

> Hi [Name] — following up on [previous context]. [Company] has an opening for [role] that lines up well with what I've been building — [one concrete proof point]. Would you be open to a referral, or pointing me to whoever owns that hire? Happy to send anything that makes it an easy yes — resume, code, a walkthrough of the project.

**Use when:** you already have some reply or common ground from the informational-chat message, or a warm-ish connection — never as a first message to a stranger.

## Rules that apply to every template above

- **One filled-in specific beats three generic sentences.** If you can't fill in the bracket with something true and specific, you haven't done the research yet — go do it before sending.
- **Lead with a checkable artifact, not a claim.** "Here's a live product with a $9K grant" resolves itself when clicked; "I'm a skilled full-stack engineer" doesn't.
- **One follow-up after 5–7 days is normal.** More than that reads as pressure, per chapter 4's etiquette notes.
- **No template survives being copy-pasted to twenty people.** The response-rate numbers cited throughout this book are for *personalized* versions of these structures — a mail-merged version of the same words converts at a fraction of the rate, and risks the reputational backfire chapter 4 describes if two recipients compare notes.
