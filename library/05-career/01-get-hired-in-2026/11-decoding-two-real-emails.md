# Decoding Two Real Emails

The two emails in this chapter are real, and they're a genuinely useful teaching artifact — but the person who wrote them is a cautionary tale, not a role model, and that needs to be said plainly before anything else. Both were sent by Soham Parekh, an engineer who became publicly notorious in July 2025 after Suhail Doshi, CEO of Playground AI, posted that Parekh had been secretly working three to four full-time jobs simultaneously across multiple startups — including one Doshi said he'd already fired Parekh from a year earlier for the exact same reason. Reporting from TechCrunch, Fortune, and CNBC in the days after found at least ten companies had hired (and mostly fired) him under the same pattern, and Parekh admitted to it on the *TBPN* podcast, citing financial pressure and describing roughly 140-hour work weeks split across employers who each believed they had him full time.

None of that is a reason to ignore what's in these two emails. It's a reason to read them the way you'd read a magic trick: study the mechanics, and separately notice that the person performing it was lying about what happened after the curtain closed. The craft and the character are two different axes, and this chapter is only about the first one.

## Email 1: the one that actually works

> tldr;
>
> I love everything about what Happenstance is doing. I don't have many hobbies outside coding. I am not athletic, bad at singing, don't drink, can't dance. Building is the only thing I am good at. At this point, I want to be a part of taking something from 0 -> 1 or 1 -> 100. I just want to be heads down chasing that goal

This opening does something most cold emails never attempt: it leads with **identity**, not qualifications. Every sentence in that paragraph is a small act of self-narrowing — "I don't have many hobbies," "I am not athletic, bad at singing, don't drink, can't dance" — which is a deliberate rhetorical move, not padding. By listing everything he *isn't*, the only thing left standing at the end of the paragraph is the one thing he claims to be: someone for whom building is the whole identity. This is the "unity" principle from the previous chapter in its purest form — he isn't saying "I'd be a good fit for your culture," he's constructing a self-portrait that only makes sense as a person who already belongs inside an early-stage, all-in company, and letting the founder draw that conclusion themselves rather than asserting it. It also reads, correctly, as **worldbuilding**: he's not asking to join Happenstance, he's describing a life that has no shape *except* inside a company like Happenstance, and inviting the reader to recognize that his story and their story are the same story.

The tldr placement itself is a second, separate technique: it respects the founder's actual constraint (eight seconds, per chapter 6) by putting the entire emotional payload in the first four lines, before a single credential appears. Everything below the tldr — the five named companies, the specific stack (Next.js, Python, Node, Go, GraphQL, gRPC, AWS/GCP over K8s), the specific claim of scaling "internal micro-services to thousands of workflows and users" — exists to make the tldr's claim *checkable*, not to replace it. That ordering matters: credentials-first reads as "please assess my qualifications"; identity-first-then-credentials reads as "here's who I am, and here's the proof."

## Email 2: the one that gives it away

> I hope this email finds you well! I've been following your impressive work at *JustPaid (YC W23)*, and I'm genuinely inspired by how you're revolutionizing revenue collections with AI-powered billing. The real-time analytics blog post was a standout — brilliant execution!

Structurally, this is close to identical to Email 1 — flattery, then background, then a soft ask, then "looking forward to hearing from you." But it fails the test the previous chapter's Communication Accommodation Theory section named directly: the compliment is generic. "Revolutionizing revenue collections with AI-powered billing" is a paraphrase of what JustPaid's own homepage says about itself, not a sign that Parekh engaged with anything a stranger couldn't get from a thirty-second skim. "The real-time analytics blog post was a standout — brilliant execution" is even more exposed, because it praises the existence of a blog post without saying one specific thing about what was actually in it — no number, no technique, no disagreement, no detail that couldn't be swapped into a template about a different company's blog post. It's the over-accommodation failure mode described in the last chapter: language that gestures at familiarity with the company without demonstrating it.

"I hope this email finds you well!" is doing real work here too, and not in Parekh's favor — it's one of the exact hedge-phrase tells chapter 6 names as a negative signal recruiters now pattern-match against instantly, alongside "I am thrilled to apply for this position." It's the kind of opener that survives in a template precisely because it fits any recipient, which is the same property that makes it worthless to any specific one.

## Why the comparison is the whole lesson

Put side by side, these two emails are the same skeleton wearing different clothes — and that's exactly the property that got Parekh caught. Once several founders compared notes (which is how the July 2025 story broke in the first place), the pattern was obvious: the same structure, the same tone, the same claims, sent to company after company with only the specifics swapped out. Email 1's tldr paragraph is genuinely unrepeatable — you couldn't send that same "I don't have hobbies outside coding" paragraph to five different companies and have it land the same way each time, because its power comes from sounding like a single, specific person's unfiltered self-description. Email 2's compliment paragraph is trivially repeatable — you could send "I've been following your impressive work at [Company], and I'm genuinely inspired by how you're [paraphrase of their homepage]. The [content type] was a standout" to a hundred companies with a find-and-replace, and several founders apparently got a version close enough to that to notice.

That's the same principle chapter 6 already established about mass-apply tools and generic AI output — genericness is a *detectable, structural* property, not a matter of degree — and chapter 9 makes the same point about why the templated-scrape-and-blast architecture always needs a human-reviewed send at the end. Parekh's own two emails prove it from the inside: the version of his pitch that was actually a specific, unscalable act of noticing (email 1) is a legitimately excellent piece of persuasive writing. The version that was a template with the noun changed (email 2) is exactly the kind of email that, at scale, is how a person juggling four simultaneous jobs eventually gets caught by the very founders he was writing to. The next chapter is about how to build the first kind on purpose, honestly, for one job at a time.
