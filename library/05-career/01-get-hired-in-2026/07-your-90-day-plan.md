# Your 90-Day Plan

Everything in this book fails if it stays theoretical. Here's the plan in a shape you can actually run — weekly, low-volume-but-high-specificity, across all three channels at once, because chapter 2 already established that running one channel in isolation for months is the most common way this goes nowhere.

## Before week 1: the two assets everything else depends on

1. **Two resumes, not one** — a backend/infra-framed version and a Web3/AI-framed version, built from the exact reframing exercise in chapter 5 (same true projects, different lead sentence per bullet). Keep both current; update the moment a new project ships.
2. **One "proof" doc** — a single page, or a pinned section of your portfolio site, listing every checkable artifact: REACTOR's mainnet link and grant, Hashtro's user numbers, the press mentions, rc-agents' x402 flow, the GitHub orgs. This is what you drop into a cold email in one line instead of an attachment.

## The weekly cadence (repeat for ~12 weeks)

**Startup/YC channel — 3 targeted actions/week**
- Scan Work at a Startup (filtered: crypto/Web3, AI) and HN's current or past "Who is Hiring" thread for 2–3 new targets.
- Send 2–3 direct-founder emails or DMs using the specificity format from chapter 3 — never more than that in a week; quality over volume is the whole thesis.
- Once a month, post in HN's "Who wants to be hired?" thread.

**Referral/LinkedIn channel — 5 targeted actions/week**
- Identify 5 people per week at target companies (ideally 1–2 degrees away, or simply engineers/managers on teams you're targeting) and send a personalized note per the response-rate-backed structure in chapter 4/5: common ground, one proof point, low-friction ask.
- Track replies. If a channel of outreach is converting under ~10%, revisit the specificity before sending more volume through it — a low reply rate is a message-quality problem, not a "send more" problem.

**Direct-application channel — 2–3 applications/week, chosen not mass-applied**
- From your target list (chapter 5's funding/headcount/engineering-blog signals), apply within 24–48 hours of any new posting.
- Pair every application with an attempt to reach the hiring manager directly the same day — never submit-and-wait alone.

## Tracking (the part people skip)

A simple spreadsheet, one row per contact: company, channel, date, message sent, response, next action. Two numbers matter every week: **contacts made** (should be steady, ~10/week across channels) and **response rate by channel** (should trend up over the 12 weeks as your message quality improves — if it doesn't, that's a signal to rewrite the template, not to send more of it).

## Milestones

- **End of week 2:** first resume/proof-doc pair finished, outreach cadence running.
- **End of week 4:** should have 3–5 real conversations started (not just sent — replied-to) across the three channels combined. If not, the message quality is the bottleneck — go back to chapters 3–5 and tighten specificity before adding volume.
- **End of week 8:** at least one channel should have produced a first-round interview. If Web3/YC outreach is converting much better than backend outreach (or vice versa), that's real market signal about which side of chapter 1's decision to lean into — let the data update the plan, not the other way around.
- **End of week 12:** re-run this plan for another cycle if needed, but revisit chapter 1's framework explicitly at this point — three months of real response-rate data across three channels is a far better input to the Web3-vs-backend-vs-both decision than any market article, including the ones in this book.

## The one rule that overrides everything else here

If a week gets busy and something has to drop, drop volume before you drop specificity. Two sharp, specific messages beat ten generic ones in every channel this book covers — that's not a motivational line, it's the single most consistent finding across every source this book pulled from, from YC founder-hiring advice to hiring-manager cold-outreach data to the hidden-job-market debunk in chapter 2. The tactics change per channel; that principle doesn't.
