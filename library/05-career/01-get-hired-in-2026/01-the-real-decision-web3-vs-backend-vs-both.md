# The Real Decision: Web3, Backend, or Both

This is the question underneath all the tactics: not "how do I send a better cold email" but "what am I even applying *for*." You have deep, production Web3 experience that came almost entirely through one channel — hackathons — that's producing fewer offers than it used to. You also have real backend/infra skills that have never been tested by a company that pays you specifically for them. Three paths are on the table, and there's now enough hard 2026 data to reason about them instead of guessing.

## The market data, stated plainly

Crypto hiring cooled sharply going into 2026. One hiring analysis tracking major crypto job boards counted roughly 85–90 new roles posted in the first two weeks of January 2026, against 1,192 postings in the same window of January 2025 — a rough 80% year-over-year collapse, with the daily posting rate falling from about 38/day to 6.5/day. A competing set of sources (crypto-specific boards like crypto.jobs and cryptorecruit.com) reports the opposite headline — postings up 45% year-over-year, smart-contract-specific listings up 58%, security-engineering roles up 71% — and the discrepancy is real and probably reflects different board coverage rather than one side being wrong. But both readings agree on the *shape* of what's hiring: roughly 65% of open crypto roles now sit at Staff/Senior/Lead/Head level requiring 5+ years (7+ for leadership), and about 60% of active hiring concentrates in infrastructure, stablecoins, and payments/fintech — not generalist "smart contract dev" postings. That's the segment that's gotten harder, and it's exactly the segment a hackathon win used to funnel you into.

Two more data points worth having. First, Solana has been quietly pulling developer share away from Ethereum — 2024 was reportedly the first year since 2016 that another ecosystem attracted a higher share of new contributing developers than Ethereum (Solana at >22% vs Ethereum's ~16%), and funding into Solana projects was up 70% quarter-over-quarter into Q3 2025. If EVM-only positioning has felt harder to place lately, that's measurable, not just a personal impression. Second, the hackathon pipeline itself hasn't disappeared, but its shape has changed: it increasingly routes into *accelerators and funding conversations* rather than direct hires. Colosseum has committed $60M explicitly to formalize a Hackathon → Accelerator → Funding pipeline; Superteam Talent runs similar community-based matching. Worth naming plainly: no source says hackathons stopped mattering — the shift is in what door they open. A strong hackathon build increasingly leads to "come pitch this as a company," not "come join our engineering team." That's a different door than the one that used to work for you, and it's worth knowing which door you're walking through before you invest another weekend expecting the old outcome.

## Option A: Double down on Web3

The case is simple — it's where your strongest, most verifiable proof already lives. A $9K grant, four press mentions, a live product on Base mainnet, a real role at Reactive Network. Nobody has to take your word for any of it. The risk, per the data above, is that generalist "Solidity dev" is exactly the sub-niche that's thinning fastest, while security auditing (Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, Spearbit, contest platforms like Code4rena and Sherlock), L2/protocol infra, and agentic on-chain systems are the pockets still hiring — and still hiring senior. Going all-in on Option A now means explicitly repositioning away from "generalist Solidity" toward one of those thinner, higher-bar niches, not coasting on the old hackathon-generalist story.

## Option B: Pivot to backend/infra, treat Web3 as a closed chapter

Backend and infra are bigger markets with more total openings and a more standardized hiring bar you can prepare against directly. The problem is the one you already named: real backend engineering *work* (rate limiting, caching, idempotency keys, a multi-service architecture in Hashtro, CI/CD via GitHub Actions) but zero backend engineering *job title*, and a generic backend pipeline at a mid-size or large company will filter on title first. The credential substitute that actually moves the needle here, per the surrounding hiring-manager literature, is legible production evidence — not a tutorial-shaped side project, but a system with uptime, real users, and visible infra decisions (migrations, monitoring, retry/backoff), narrated around one or two specific incidents (a schema migration under load, an RLS policy bug, a rate-limit design call) rather than a skills list. Open-source contribution to a project a target company actually depends on outperforms an equivalent amount of solo work, because it's third-party-verified rather than self-reported. This path is winnable — chapter 5's "let the project be the experience" argument applies directly — but it means competing on a company's standard bar with no shortcut, and discarding the one network (Web3-adjacent) where you currently have real pull.

## Option C: Position the intersection, not either pole

The strongest version of your candidacy on paper isn't "Web3 engineer" or "backend engineer" — it's the specific, less-crowded combination you already have shipped proof of: **production Web3 systems, wired to AI agents, that move real value.** rc-agents isn't a generic Solidity project or a generic backend project — it's autonomous agents making x402 micropayments to purchase Aave liquidation protection, exactly the "agentic commerce" pattern that's new enough that very few engineers anywhere have shipped something real in it. Hashtro is the same intersection from a different angle — an LLM making decisions that trigger real financial transactions, with the backend rigor to make that safe. This also has a name in the market now, not just as a personal framing: "Web3 Blockchain Infrastructure Engineer" is described in current industry writing as a distinct, in-demand, and explicitly *overlooked* track — the layer that builds APIs and services connecting blockchain nodes, wallets, and smart contracts (indexers, middleware, custodial services), sitting between pure L1/L2 protocol engineering and pure backend work. Roles on this bridge — DevRel/Developer Advocate (roughly $100K–$200K per web3-adjacent sourcing) and Protocol Engineer ($160K–$300K) — reward exactly "can ship a real backend service" plus "understands EVM/chain mechanics well enough to extend it." Given that 60% of current crypto hiring concentrates in infrastructure and payments, this isn't a hedge — it's the segment of the market that's actually still hiring, and it's a smaller pool because most candidates self-sort into either "pure Web3" or "pure backend" rather than presenting as both on purpose.

## What this means for how you apply

Don't pick one identity for your resume and bury the rest — pick one identity **per target**, drawn from the same true body of work, the way chapter 5 describes for backend-framed versus Web3-framed language on the same Hashtro project:

- **Applying to a pure Web3/DeFi protocol team:** lead with Reactive Network, the grant, the press, REACTOR's mainnet deployment. Backend skills are supporting evidence ("built with proper infra discipline"), not the headline.
- **Applying to a pure backend/infra role at a non-crypto company:** lead with the service architecture, the CI/CD, the reliability patterns. Web3 experience becomes "shipped and operated production systems handling real financial value under adversarial conditions" — true, and a genuinely strong backend-hiring signal, without requiring the reader to care about crypto.
- **Applying anywhere doing AI-agent infrastructure, agentic commerce, or AI × crypto:** lead with the full intersection — rc-agents and Hashtro together, framed exactly as what they are. This is the target where you're least replaceable, and given the 60%-infra concentration in current crypto hiring, it's worth actively building your target list (chapter 5) around companies working in this exact seam rather than assuming there are only a handful.

On tailoring philosophy: there's a real split in career-coaching advice between "tailor every resume to every posting or get filtered" and an 80/20 counter-view that says pick one sharply-targeted resume for the single next role you actually want, reserving heavy customization for the roughly 20% of postings that are a genuine strong match. Applied here, the synthesis is: don't run one generalist resume listing both skill clusters side by side — that reads as unfocused to both an ATS keyword match and a human skim. Maintain two or three targeted narratives (pure smart-contract/security; Web3-infra/protocol-adjacent; backend/infra at a non-crypto company) and choose per application which one leads, using the others as supporting range rather than the headline.
