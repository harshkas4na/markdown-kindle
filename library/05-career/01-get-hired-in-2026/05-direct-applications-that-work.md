# Direct Applications That Work

"Direct application" sounds like the boring, brute-force channel — pick a company, fill out the form, wait. Done badly, that's exactly what it is, and it's the lowest-converting channel in this book when done that way. Done well, it looks completely different: you pick the company *first*, for real reasons, find the person who actually owns the hiring decision, and only then does the form become a formality that confirms what a human already half-decided.

## The ATS myth, corrected

The "75% of resumes never reach a human" statistic that circulates constantly has no traceable source — it's a zombie stat. What's actually true: modern ATS platforms (Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, Ashby) are organizational databases and search/ranking tools, not rejection machines. Only a small minority of recruiters — one estimate puts it around 8% — configure hard content-based auto-rejection; the rest still have a human read every application, just fast and triaged. The one place software genuinely does auto-reject you is **knockout questions** ("Do you require visa sponsorship?", "Do you have 5+ years of X?"), where a wrong answer filters you out before a human ever sees the resume. So: obsessing over keyword-stuffing your PDF is mostly wasted effort — a resume so keyword-bare it doesn't surface when a recruiter searches "Solidity" or "FastAPI" is the real failure mode, not a resume that fails some invisible density threshold. Answering screening questions honestly-but-strategically is where the actual leverage is.

## Timing matters more than formatting

Popular postings draw 100–250+ applications in the first 24–48 hours, and most hiring teams start reviewing within that same window — roughly 70% of eventual interviews reportedly go to people who applied within the first 7 days. (A widely-repeated "90% of interviews go to people who apply within 24 hours" stat traces to a low-rigor newsletter, not a study — treat it as directional, not gospel.) The actionable version: set up alerts (LinkedIn, company career-page RSS where available) and apply same-day when a role fits. A tailored application on day one beats a perfect one on day ten, because by day ten the shortlist may already exist.

## Choose the company before you choose the job

Mass-applying to whatever matches a keyword search puts you in competition with hundreds of other volume-strategy applicants. Targeting is a signal strategy — the resume matters less when it's obvious you picked *this* company on purpose. Career offices at Duke, Yale, and NYU independently converge on a version of the same method, sometimes called **LAMP** (List, Alumni/Advocate, Motivation, Posting): build a list of 20–40 companies, not thousands of job-board results; for each, note any alumni or personal connection inside; score your genuine motivation honestly (0–3, where 3 is "would take this over almost anything else"); track current postings. The point of scoring motivation honestly is to stop spending expensive direct-outreach energy on companies you don't actually want — and to revisit the list every few weeks as a living document, not a one-time export. Populate it by scanning "best of" industry lists, and by checking LinkedIn for where people *currently* at your target companies worked immediately before — that reveals the actual feeder pattern, not the one you'd guess.

Nick Corcodilos, who has run the *Ask The Headhunter* column since the late 1990s and has answered more than 30,000 reader questions, has one consistent and unfashionable message underneath all of it: job boards are where employers dump reqs they haven't filled through people they already trust, and headhunters get paid large fees precisely because personal introductions still beat forms. His prescription: stop treating the online application as your primary channel. Find the person doing the job you want, talk to them about the actual work — not "can you refer me" — and let the conversation lead somewhere. Slower than spraying applications, but the highest yield per hour invested.

## Skip the recruiter inbox when you can

Still submit the form (it costs little, and occasionally a recruiter reviews it before your outreach lands), but it shouldn't be the primary move. The primary move is finding whoever would actually manage the role: check the posting for a "Meet the team" section (increasingly common on Ashby/Greenhouse postings), search the company's LinkedIn People tab filtered to "Engineering Manager" or the specific team name, or — if you're already talking to a recruiter — simply ask "who would this role report to." Nick Singh's breakdown of cold emails that actually landed interviews (three verified cases: Periscope Data, Airbnb, Reddit) gives a reusable shape: 50–125 words, lead with a specific accomplishment, find a genuine personal connection (shared school, a prior meetup, a mutual contact), state one explicit ask, and follow up two to three times over the following two weeks. The Reddit case is the most instructive for a project-heavy candidate: the applicant didn't just say "I built something," they said "I built RapStock.io, it has 2,000 users, and 1,400 of them came from your platform" — turning the project into evidence the company already cared about, before the ask was even made.

## Viral stunts work exactly once, and mostly as marketing, not hiring

The canonical case is Nina Mufleh, who in 2015 built a full website styled like Airbnb's own product, arguing precisely why Airbnb needed someone with her market-entry experience. It got nearly half a million views and got Brian Chesky and Airbnb's CMO to personally respond, landing her interviews at Airbnb, Uber, LinkedIn, and Dropbox. The part worth remembering: **Airbnb never hired her** — there wasn't an open role that fit — and she ended up at Upwork instead. The lesson isn't "make a stunt," it's "make something so specific to one company's actual problem that it's undeniable," while accepting that even a viral one is a lead-generation tool, not a guaranteed offer.

## Build-in-public can replace the interview funnel entirely — inside open source

Zed Industries, the code editor company, has documented in a blog series literally titled "Hired Through GitHub" that a real hiring channel for them looks like: a contributor shows up in their GitHub Discussions or Discord, opens PRs that consistently fix real bugs or ship real features, becomes a recognized name in the team's internal Slack — and by the time a conversation about joining happens, the "interview" is a chat with the CEO about the codebase, not a leetcode loop. This is the cleanest data point for someone whose strength is shipped, verifiable work rather than an interview-format resume: contributing meaningfully to a company's actual open-source repo is a documented hiring funnel, not just character-building. It requires picking a company that has real public repos, actually reviews external PRs, and is small enough that maintainers notice a name.

## When you don't have "professional experience" — let the project be the experience

This is the direct-application channel's biggest lever for you specifically: a resume with no big-company line and a live product with a $9K grant and four press mentions is not a weaker resume — it's a different kind of proof, and for backend/infra-adjacent roles at smaller and mid-size companies, verifiable proof beats a job title fairly often. The recurring pattern across "hired via open source" and portfolio-led hiring stories is consistent: engineers get pulled into interviews because a recruiter or engineer found a specific pull request, repo, or deployed product that solved a real problem — not because a resume bullet said "familiar with Docker." The skeptical counterpoint shows up in the same threads and is worth taking seriously: a scattered pile of small, unfinished side projects reads as a distraction, not a credential — the effect only holds for work that's finished, public, and ideally used by someone other than you. That's precisely the "ship-to-finish, no incomplete work" standard already in your own working philosophy — lean into it rather than diluting it with more half-projects.

**Practically, for the backend/infra roles where you have no title to point to:** lead the resume and the outreach note with the infra-shaped parts of what you've already shipped, described in infra language rather than product language. "Built a 3-service microarchitecture (Next.js / FastAPI / Solana trading service) with rate limiting, TTL caching, and idempotency keys, deployed via Docker + GitHub Actions CI/CD" is a backend-engineering sentence, not a Web3 sentence — it happens to be true about Hashtro. Say it that way when the audience is a backend team, and say the DeFi-and-grant version when the audience is a Web3 team. Same work, framed for the reader — not dishonesty, just choosing which true thing to lead with.
