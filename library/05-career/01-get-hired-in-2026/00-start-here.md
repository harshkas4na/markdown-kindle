# Start Here

You've gotten every serious opportunity so far through hackathons — six wins, including two placements at the same International Reactive Hackathon, a role at Reactive Network that grew out of that world, a $9K grant, press coverage. That pipeline worked. It's also a pipeline with a shrinking supply of entries: hackathon season is intermittent, the pool of judges who see your work is small and overlapping, and "win enough hackathons to get noticed" isn't a repeatable weekly action the way "send five targeted messages" is. This book exists because you need a second pipeline that runs continuously, in parallel with whatever hackathons come next — not a replacement for what's worked, a second engine.

## What this book is not

It is not a generic "how to job search" guide. There are a thousand of those and most of them are written for someone with neither your assets nor your specific gap. It also isn't a motivational pep talk about "believing in yourself." It's a set of channels — YC's startup ecosystem, LinkedIn referrals, and direct company applications — explained with what actually converts in each one, followed by a synthesis of what's genuinely changed about job-hunting in the AI era, and a concrete decision framework for the question you keep circling: **Web3, backend/infra, or something that's honestly neither and is worth naming on its own terms.**

## Your actual starting position, stated plainly

Strip the modesty and the self-doubt and here's what's true on paper: you're a final-year engineer with five-plus live, shipped products (not prototypes — live URLs, real users, in one case press coverage across four outlets and an unsolicited $9K grant), production experience with Solidity and Reactive Smart Contracts, working backend systems in Node and FastAPI with real infrastructure concerns handled (rate limiting, caching, idempotency, CI/CD), and — increasingly rare and valuable — hands-on experience wiring LLMs into production systems that touch money (Gemini in Hashtro's trading logic, Claude Code skill-authoring, MCP, x402 agent-to-contract payments). The gap isn't skill. The gap is that none of it has a "Google" or "Series B unicorn" logo next to it, and you don't yet have a repeatable *process* for turning strangers into interviews. Both of those are fixable with tactics, not with more projects.

## How to use this book

Read chapters 1–6 once, straight through — they build on each other. Chapter 7 is the one you'll come back to weekly: a 90-day plan with a specific, small, repeatable cadence per channel. Chapter 8 is a reference appendix of every message template used earlier in the book, collected in one place so you're not hunting back through chapters when you sit down to actually write outreach.

One honest note on sourcing: every chapter here is pulled from a live research pass done in July 2026 — YC's own hiring advice, Hacker News threads, named career-advice writers who publish their actual response-rate data, documented hiring-side reporting (Gergely Orosz's newsletter, hiring-manager blogs, a live 2026 crypto-market analysis), and real people's own accounts of what worked and what backfired, named and linked where a name exists. Where a commonly repeated "fact" turned out to be weakly sourced or outright wrong — the "85% of jobs are hidden" myth, the "90% of interviews go to people who apply in 24 hours" myth — it's flagged as such rather than repeated because it sounds true. The market-facing numbers (exactly how the Web3 hiring market looked in January 2026, current GitHub star counts on the tools mentioned in chapter 6) are a snapshot, not a permanent fact — worth a quick re-check against live job-board data before you bet a full quarter of outreach on any single one of them holding steady. Job-search advice is one of the most confidently-stated, poorly-sourced genres on the internet — this book tries hard not to add to that pile, including about itself.
