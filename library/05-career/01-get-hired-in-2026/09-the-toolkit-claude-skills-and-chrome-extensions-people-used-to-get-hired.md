# The Toolkit: Claude Skills and Chrome Extensions People Actually Used to Get Hired

Chapter 6 made the case that the credible "AI in job search" stories are people building small agentic pipelines around their own search, not people pasting one job description into a chat window. This chapter is the reference list behind that claim — every Claude Code skill and Chrome extension below is a real, named, checkable thing someone built or shipped, with a link, what it actually does, and (where it exists) the author's own claim about whether it worked. The point isn't "install all of these." It's the same point chapter 6 makes about REACTOR and Hashtro: seeing exactly what other people built tells you what's possible, so you can build the version that fits your own search instead of copying theirs wholesale.

Star and fork counts below were pulled directly from the GitHub API, not from marketing copy, so they're a real (if noisy) proxy for "other engineers actually looked at this and found it worth bookmarking."

## Claude Code skills — agentic job-search pipelines

**[MadsLorentzen/ai-job-search](https://github.com/MadsLorentzen/ai-job-search)** — 23,500+ stars, 7,500+ forks. A Claude Code framework with slash commands (`/setup`, `/scrape`, `/apply`, `/outcome`) that scrapes job boards, tailors a LaTeX CV per posting using a drafter-and-reviewer agent pair (one model writes, a second critiques before anything gets sent), and tracks interview/offer/rejection outcomes to recalibrate what's actually converting. Runs entirely on your machine — nothing goes through a third-party SaaS. The creator's own line: "it got me hired," with a specific claim of 69 tailored applications, 20 first-round interviews, and one signed offer.

**[santifer/career-ops](https://github.com/santifer/career-ops)** — 60,000+ stars, 11,800+ forks, and growing faster than any other tool on this list. Local-first, works with Claude Code, Gemini, Codex, or OpenCode. Scans job portals, scores each listing A–F against your own profile (not a generic template), tailors your CV per posting, and tracks applications — all without your data leaving your machine. Creator's quote: "It worked: I used it to land my current role."

**[noamseg/interview-coach-skill](https://github.com/noamseg/interview-coach-skill)** — 1,600+ stars. Built by Noam Segal, Community Research Lead at Lenny's Newsletter, after interviewing 30+ tech professionals and hiring managers specifically about how AI is reshaping hiring. Covers the full interview lifecycle through 23 commands: `research`/`decode`/`prep` to build company- and role-specific prep, `stories` to build a searchable "story bank" from prompts like *"when were you at your best?"* so you have a sharp answer ready instead of freezing, `mock`/`practice` for escalating-pressure drills (30-second answer limits, panel simulations), `analyze` to score a recorded practice interview across substance, structure, relevance, credibility, and differentiation, and `negotiate` for word-for-word offer scripts. Installed by renaming `SKILL.md` to `CLAUDE.md` and saying `kickoff`. The most mechanically detailed, best-documented tool in this whole list.

**[Paramchoudhary/ResumeSkills](https://github.com/Paramchoudhary/ResumeSkills)** — 1,100+ stars. A collection of narrower, single-purpose Claude skills — resume optimization, ATS keyword matching, interview prep — built for job seekers and career changers who want something more composable than a single monolithic framework.

**[dabydat/resume-builder-skill](https://github.com/dabydat/resume-builder-skill)** — Smaller repo (single digits of stars as of this writing) but worth including for its provenance: it encodes the Harvard Career Services resume standard and the norms of r/EngineeringResumes, a subreddit whose volunteer reviewers have iterated on ATS-safe resume formatting across, per the repo, 500,000+ engineers' resumes. Good source if you want the skill's judgment to be grounded in a known, high-volume review process rather than one person's taste.

**[suxrobgm/jobpilot](https://github.com/suxrobgm/jobpilot)** and **[gregorymm/job-scout](https://github.com/gregorymm/job-scout)** — smaller, less battle-tested projects (tens of stars, not thousands), but useful as *pattern* examples: both are Claude Code/Codex plugins that scrape boards, score fit, and tailor a CV automatically. They show this architecture (scrape → score → tailor → track) has been independently reinvented by several different people, which is a stronger signal than any single project's popularity — it means the shape of the tool, not just one implementation, is genuinely useful.

**The pattern across all of the above:** none of them are "write my cover letter." They're pipelines with a human-reviewed send at the end — scrape, score against your real background, tailor, track outcomes, recalibrate. That's the exact "copilot, not autopilot" shape chapter 6 argues is the only version of AI-assisted search that survives contact with a real hiring process.

## Chrome extensions — the legitimate "copilot" tier

**[Simplify Copilot](https://chromewebstore.google.com/detail/simplify-jobs-autofill-a/pbbepnclddjjfmiofcpokdmlkbenincj)** — the highest-rated pure autofill extension in this space (4.9/5 across 3,700+ Chrome Web Store ratings, 500,000+ users as of recent counts). It autofills application forms across major ATS platforms but does not submit for you — you still click Submit yourself. That one design choice is why it's cited approvingly in chapter 6: it removes the mechanical typing without removing the human judgment on what actually gets sent, which keeps it on the right side of most job boards' terms of service and away from the bot-detection systems that flag mass-apply tools.

**[Teal](https://www.tealhq.com/tool/job-search-chrome-extension)** — weaker autofill than the two above, but its actual strength is the job tracker: a Kanban board that captures a listing (and, via the extension, the company/contact data around it) the moment you find it, so a complex multi-company search doesn't collapse into a pile of forgotten tabs and unread emails. Worth it specifically if your bottleneck is organization, not typing speed.

**[Jobscan](https://www.jobscan.co/)** — not an autofill tool at all; it's a resume-to-job-description keyword-gap analyzer with a browser extension component. You paste (or the extension pulls) a job description, it scores your resume against it, and flags missing keywords an ATS parser is likely screening on. Complements the tailoring skills above rather than competing with them — a good second opinion after an LLM has already rewritten a bullet.

A caveat worth stating plainly: several "best Chrome extensions for job search" roundups circulating right now (JobWizard and similar all-in-one autofill-plus-scoring-plus-referral-finder tools) come from sites that read as SEO/affiliate content rather than independent reviews, and their user counts are harder to verify than the GitHub-star or Chrome-Web-Store-rating numbers above. Treat claims from that tier as "worth a five-minute look," not "verified," until you've tried them yourself.

## The tier to be deliberate about: real-time interview copilots

A separate and much more ethically loaded category exists: extensions like **Interview Coder** and **Final Round AI's Copilot** that run during a live interview call (Zoom, Teams, Meet), transcribe the interviewer's question in real time, and feed the candidate an AI-generated answer through an overlay designed to be invisible on a shared screen. These are real, widely used, and — per reporting on the space — account for a large share of documented interview-cheating incidents; dedicated tools like these reportedly make up roughly 45% of caught cheating cases in one 2026 dataset of tracked interviews. Companies are responding directly: 58%+ report moving to custom, company-specific interview questions instead of standard LeetCode-style problems specifically because they're no longer trustworthy as a signal, and some (Google among them) are reconsidering unproctored virtual interviews altogether.

These are included here for the same reason chapter 6 documents auto-apply tools' failure rates — not as a recommendation, but because "creative moves other people are actually making" has to include the ones that are backfiring, so you can see the boundary rather than guess at it. Everything else in this chapter *removes friction from work you actually did*. This category *fabricates the work in real time*, and the market (detection tooling, custom questions, employer distrust) is actively closing the gap it depends on. It's the one item on this list where "it's possible" is not the same as "it's a good idea."

## Building your own

If nothing here fits your exact search — and given the Web3/backend-infra target from chapter 1, nothing here is tuned to that specifically — chapter 6 already makes the case for building a small version yourself: watch Work at a Startup, HN's monthly threads, and your own target-company list from chapter 5, draft a first-pass tailored note using the What/How/Impact structure from chapter 3, and leave every send as a manual, reviewed action. Written up and shared the way MadsLorentzen and Segal did theirs, it becomes a second portfolio piece, not just a private tool — a live, checkable thing a stranger can verify in under two minutes, which chapter 6 argues is the one thing in this entire list that AI genuinely cannot fake for you.
