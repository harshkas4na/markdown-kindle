# The AI-Era Playbook

Two things are true at once about AI and job hunting right now, and most advice only tells you one of them. First: AI has made the mechanical parts of a job search — tailoring a resume to a JD, drafting outreach, prepping for a specific interviewer — dramatically faster, and using it for that is table stakes, not cheating. Second: AI has also made it trivially easy to flood every channel with generic, mass-produced applications, and hiring teams have gotten fast at smelling that and discounting it. The net effect is that the bar for what counts as a differentiated candidate has gone *up*, even though the effort to look differentiated has gone down.

## The volume problem is now measurable

Gergely Orosz's newsletter *The Pragmatic Engineer* reports that Mike Julian, CEO of the DuckBill Group, sees roughly 1,000 applications a day land on a single posting — of which about two are actually relevant. Recruiters reportedly spend under eight seconds on a first resume pass against that volume. Generic AI output has become a negative signal inside that flood, not a neutral one: recruiters have pattern-matched phrases like "I am thrilled to apply for this position" and "I believe my skills align perfectly" into near-instant rejections. Tailoring — real, specific tailoring — is reported as the single biggest predictor of getting an interview, ahead of every other resume variable.

**What legitimately works:** use an LLM to compress the *research* step, not to write the final output. Paste a job description alongside your real project history and ask which specific accomplishments map to which requirements, or ask for a rewrite of one bullet in the language a specific team would use — the FastAPI/Docker/CI-CD framing versus the Solidity/DeFi framing of the same Hashtro project, exactly as chapter 1 lays out. The failure mode isn't using AI, it's letting the first draft *be* the final draft — generated text has a flatness (uniform sentence length, hedge words like "leverage" and "seamless," no specific numbers) that experienced readers now recognize almost instantly, because they've seen thousands of AI-flattened applications.

## Auto-apply tools are empirically failing

Roughly 48% of job seekers now use automated tools to mass-apply — 50 to 500 applications at a stretch. The results are bad: about 75% of these applications get zero response, rising to 95% in tech specifically. Wonsulting, an early and credible player in this space, shut down its bulk auto-apply feature in August 2025 after clients averaged one interview per 50 applications sent — a 2% hit rate. LazyApply sits at roughly 2 stars on Trustpilot, with users reporting it applies to roles outside their stated skills entirely. Mass-applying also violates most job boards' terms of service, and LinkedIn actively monitors and suspends accounts for bot-like application patterns. The tool category that survives scrutiny is "copilot, not autopilot" — Simplify's free tier autofills form fields but doesn't submit for you, keeping a human in the loop for the part that actually needs judgment. This is the exact opposite of everything in chapters 3–5: those channels are all about *increasing* specificity per contact. Volume-over-specificity is a strategy for a candidate with no differentiated story to tell. You have one — REACTOR, Hashtro, rc-agents, the grant, the press. Don't trade it for a spray-and-pray tool.

## The real "Claude skills in action" stories

There's a small but real, verifiable genre of engineers building their own AI agents *as* their job search, rather than just using a chat window as a writing assistant — and it's exactly the kind of tooling you already know how to build, since you've authored Claude Code skills professionally.

**MadsLorentzen/ai-job-search** is an open-source Claude Code framework — commands like `/setup`, `/scrape`, `/apply`, `/outcome` — that scrapes job boards, tailors a LaTeX CV per posting using a drafter-and-reviewer agent pair (one model writes, a second critiques before anything is sent), and, critically, tracks interview/offer/rejection outcomes to recalibrate what's actually converting. It's a real project with real traction — roughly 12,900 GitHub stars and 4,100 forks — and its creator's own line on it is blunt: "it got me hired." **santifer/career-ops** does a similar thing independently: local-first, scores job listings A–F against your own profile, with the creator writing "It worked: I used it to land my current role." A handful of adjacent tools (gregorymm/job-scout, suxrobgm/jobpilot, Paramchoudhary/ResumeSkills) show this has become a small pattern, not an isolated hack — job search treated as an agentic pipeline with a human reviewing every send, not a chatbot you paste one job description into at a time.

The interview-prep side has an even sharper example. Noam Segal, Community Research Lead at Lenny's Newsletter, interviewed more than 30 tech professionals and hiring managers specifically about how AI is changing the hiring process, then encoded what he learned into a free, public Claude Code skill (`noamseg/interview-coach-skill`). The mechanics are worth stealing directly, not just admiring: record a real practice interview (he uses Granola) and run `analyze` to get it scored across substance, structure, relevance, credibility, and differentiation; build a "story bank" using prompts like *"When were you at your best?"* indexed by skill, so a sharp story is retrievable under pressure instead of you freezing; run `prep [company name]` to generate predicted questions plus likely objections and counters; paste an interviewer's LinkedIn URL to get a card on their functional lens and probable focus areas; run escalating-pressure drills (30-second answer limits, panel simulations, pushback handling); and use `negotiate` for word-for-word offer-negotiation scripts. This is the most credible, best-documented example of "AI as an actual interview weapon" that exists right now, not folklore — and it's a direct model for what you could build or fork for yourself, tuned to the Web3/backend-infra targets in chapter 1 rather than generic SWE roles.

Given that you already build Claude Code skills as part of your normal work (`reactive-network-dev`), the natural move isn't to admire this genre from outside it — it's to join it. A lightweight pipeline that watches Work at a Startup, HN's monthly thread, and your own target-list companies (chapter 5) for new postings, drafts a first-pass tailored note from your real project history using the What/How/Impact structure from chapter 3, and leaves every send as a manual, reviewed action, is a small build for you specifically. Written up and shared the way MadsLorentzen and Segal did theirs, it stops being just a tool and becomes exactly the kind of public, checkable artifact this whole book argues you should be leading with — a job-search tool that is itself a portfolio piece about how you build.

## Where AI backfires hardest: getting caught

The Markup, a nonprofit newsroom, posted a real engineering job in January 2026 and got over 400 applications in 12 hours — a large share fraudulent or AI-slop. The tells they documented publicly: the same email or phone number reused across supposedly different applicants; near-identical "Why do you want to work here?" answers that just paraphrased the company's own About page back at them; claimed experience that mirrored the job description's own language rather than describing real past work. Survey data cited alongside that story: 78% of hiring managers actively look for personalization as a genuine-interest signal, 62% say unpersonalized AI-generated resumes get rejected outright, and 80% report discomfort with visibly AI-written content in an application. Detection is now partly automated on the hiring side too — tools like Pangram Labs sell AI-cover-letter checkers that plug directly into ATS pipelines. What still reads as human, per this same body of reporting: specific, checkable claims (numbers, project names, a link that actually works), natural sentence-length variation, and — per Segal's interview-coach data — a story that came from a real "when were you at your best" moment rather than a bullet list mirrored back from the job description.

## The thing AI genuinely cannot fake for you

Every credible source in this chapter converges on the same answer to "what still differentiates a candidate": a live, specific, checkable thing you built that a stranger can verify in under two minutes. A GitHub repo with real commits and real users. A deployed product with a URL. A grant or a press mention a third party — not you — decided to give. This is the entire thesis of *Show Your Work!* elsewhere in this library, and it maps almost exactly onto what you already have with REACTOR, Hashtro, and the rest: none of it can be generated by a language model in thirty seconds, which is precisely what makes it valuable in a market where everything else can be.
