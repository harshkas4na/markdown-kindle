# Order Types, Routing & Auctions

**74 hook projects.**

## The problem, explained simply

A plain Uniswap AMM only really supports one kind of trade: "swap right now, at whatever the current pool price is" (a market order). Traditional exchanges (stock markets, centralized crypto exchanges) offer much richer order types — a **limit order** ("only execute my trade if the price reaches X"), a **stop order**, scheduled/algorithmic execution, and so on. Before hooks, replicating any of this on Uniswap required an entirely separate off-chain or external system watching prices and submitting transactions on your behalf. That's extra infrastructure, extra trust, and extra points of failure.

A second, related problem is **routing and market efficiency** — a trade doesn't always get the best execution just because it went through "a" Uniswap pool; there might be a better price via a different path, a different fee tier, or by having someone else with an opposing trade matched against you directly instead of going through the pool at all. And a third, related idea is **auctions** as a fairer/more efficient way to decide *who* gets to do something advantageous (like set the pool fee, or capture arbitrage) rather than a fixed rule — see also the am-AMM discussion in `dynamic-fees-lp-optimization.md` and `mev-lvr-protection.md`.

## Why so many teams attack this

Limit orders are one of the most-requested, most obviously "missing" features compared to centralized exchanges, and hooks are a natural place to implement them because a `beforeSwap`/`afterSwap` hook can hold a pending order and only let it execute when a price condition is met — no external bot required. Auction mechanisms are attractive to more advanced teams because they connect to the well-developed am-AMM/MEV-redistribution research already popular in the Uniswap v4 hook community.

## Common approaches seen in this dataset

1. **On-chain limit orders as single-sided liquidity** — place a limit order by depositing a single-sided liquidity position at the target price tick; when a swap moves the price through that tick, the position is consumed, effectively executing the "order," and the depositor earns LP fees on top.
2. **Cross-chain limit orders** — same idea, but the order is placed on one chain and redeemed/settled on another via a cross-chain messaging layer (see `cross-chain-hooks.md`).
3. **Custom routers** — an alternate entry point for swaps that adds logic (fee routing, compliance checks, batching) before or after handing the trade to the core pool, without modifying the pool itself.
4. **Auction-based rights allocation (am-AMM pattern)** — auction off a temporary right (e.g. first-in-block execution priority, or the right to set the pool fee) rather than giving it out by a fixed rule, letting the market price that right and pay the proceeds to LPs.
5. **Dutch auctions for execution timing** — let a price/premium decay over time until someone finds it profitable to act, a common mechanism for triggering automated strategies at the "right" moment without needing a centralized keeper.

## Prior art / reference material

- Standard limit-order-book exchange design (TradFi and CEXs) — the baseline feature set these hooks are trying to replicate in a fully on-chain, non-custodial way.
- am-AMM (auction-managed AMM) research — the same auction-based-fee-and-rights framework referenced in `dynamic-fees-lp-optimization.md` and `mev-lvr-protection.md`, applied here to order execution rights specifically.
- CoW Protocol's batch auction design — a precedent for "auction as a trade-matching mechanism" rather than continuous order matching.
- Gnosis's original Dutch auction mechanism (used historically for token distribution) — the general pattern several "Dutch auction trigger" hooks borrow.
