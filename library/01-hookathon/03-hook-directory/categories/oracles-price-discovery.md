# Oracles & Price Discovery

**75 hook projects.**

## The problem, explained simply

An AMM pool only knows one thing directly: the ratio of the two tokens sitting inside it right now. It has no built-in concept of the "real" external market price. That's normally fine — arbitrageurs keep the pool price roughly in line with the outside world by trading against it whenever it drifts (see `mev-lvr-protection.md` for why that's a double-edged sword for LPs). But plenty of hook designs *need* to know the outside "true" price directly, for example to:

- Detect that a stablecoin has depegged (you need an external reference for "$1.00").
- Price a dynamic fee based on how far the pool has drifted from the market (Nezlobin-style directional fees).
- Price an option or leveraged position fairly.
- Prevent a low-liquidity pool from being trivially manipulated — an attacker with enough capital can push a thin pool's *own* price wherever they want for one transaction (a "flash loan price manipulation" attack), so anything that reads the pool's instantaneous price as ground truth is exploitable.

**Oracles** are the systems that bring external, trust-minimized price data on-chain. Getting this right is hard: a naive oracle (just reading the pool's current spot price) is trivially manipulable; a good oracle needs to resist manipulation while still being reasonably fast to update.

## Why so many teams attack this

Price oracles are one of the most historically exploited components in all of DeFi — most large DeFi hacks (Mango Markets, Cream Finance, and many others) trace back to oracle manipulation, not a flaw in the core lending/trading logic. So it's a well-known, well-documented risk that any serious hook builder has to think about, and building a *better* oracle is a common differentiator.

## Common approaches seen in this dataset

1. **TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price)** — instead of reading the instantaneous spot price (easy to manipulate for one block), average the price over a window of time, making a manipulation attack much more expensive since an attacker has to sustain the fake price over many blocks. This is the same core idea Uniswap v2/v3 already use internally for their built-in oracle.
2. **External oracle integration (Chainlink, Pyth)** — pull a price from an established, decentralized off-chain data network rather than trusting the pool's own recent trades at all.
3. **Reflexive / self-referential oracles with insurance** — combine an oracle with an insurance mechanism that compensates users if the oracle is later found to have been wrong or manipulated (ROSIP).
4. **AVS-based (Actively Validated Service) oracles** — use EigenLayer restaking operators (see `staking-restaking.md`) as a decentralized network specifically tasked with attesting to volatility or price data, adding cryptoeconomic security on top of the raw data feed.
5. **Social/sentiment-based price signals** — a less conventional approach: derive trading signals from public social data (e.g. crypto Telegram groups) rather than purely price-based oracles (SocialPool, Sentiment).

## Prior art / reference material

- Uniswap v2/v3 built-in TWAP oracle design — the baseline manipulation-resistant pricing mechanism most "oracle hook" projects are extending or replacing.
- Chainlink price feeds and Pyth Network — the two most commonly integrated third-party oracle providers referenced across this dataset.
- Post-mortems of oracle-manipulation DeFi hacks (e.g. Mango Markets 2022, Cream Finance 2021) — the applied "why this matters" case studies behind manipulation-resistant oracle design.
- EigenLayer AVS (Actively Validated Services) model — the restaking-secured oracle/data-attestation pattern several hooks build on (see also `staking-restaking.md`).
