# Staking & Restaking

**33 hook projects.**

## The problem, explained simply

When someone stakes ETH to help secure Ethereum, they normally lock it up and can't use it for anything else while it's staked. **Liquid Staking Tokens (LSTs)** — like Lido's stETH or Rocket Pool's rETH — solve that by giving the staker a tradeable token representing their staked ETH plus rewards, so they can still use that value elsewhere in DeFi while it keeps earning staking yield. **EigenLayer** took this further with **restaking**: letting already-staked ETH (or LSTs) be "re-pledged" as economic security for other services (oracles, bridges, and other infrastructure, called AVSs — Actively Validated Services), earning additional yield on top of base staking rewards, producing **Liquid Restaking Tokens (LRTs)**.

This creates two DeFi problems relevant to Uniswap hooks:

1. **LST/LRT liquidity is fragmented and needs efficient AMM pools** — there are many competing LSTs/LRTs (stETH, rETH, weETH, and dozens more), each needs deep liquidity against ETH/USDC for people to actually use them, and their prices should track very closely to the underlying ETH (a "near-peg" problem similar to stablecoins), so they benefit from the same specialized-curve thinking discussed in `amm-curve-design.md`.
2. **AVS-based security as a building block for hooks generally** — separately from LST/LRT liquidity specifically, several hooks in *other* categories (oracles, MEV auctions) use EigenLayer's AVS model as a way to get a decentralized, economically-secured off-chain computation layer (e.g. a group of operators who stake collateral and get slashed if they misbehave) to do something a plain smart contract can't do alone, like verifying volatility data or auction winners.

## Why so many teams attack this

EigenLayer restaking was one of the most actively hyped and heavily sponsored/integrated technologies during the UHI program's run, so a large number of teams specifically built to showcase AVS integration, independent of whether restaking was strictly necessary for their idea. LST/LRT liquidity is also a genuinely large and growing pool of on-chain value, making "better liquidity for staked assets" an easy value proposition to pitch.

## Common approaches seen in this dataset

1. **Unified/aggregated LST liquidity pools** — let one pool represent liquidity across multiple LSTs rather than needing a separate pool per LST/ETH pair (UniStake, MultiSwap), similar in spirit to Sanctum's multi-LST model on Solana.
2. **Idle-liquidity restaking** — automatically restake out-of-range or idle LP capital into EigenLayer to earn extra yield while it isn't earning swap fees (LightHook, see also `liquidity-management-incentives.md`).
3. **AVS-secured oracles and auctions** — use EigenLayer operators as a trust-minimized data/verification layer for volatility feeds, MEV auction outcomes, or depeg detection (see `oracles-price-discovery.md` and `mev-lvr-protection.md` for the mechanisms this enables).
4. **Staking-yield-aware dynamic fees** — set swap fees relative to the "risk-free" ETH staking rate as a benchmark, so LP returns are explicitly compared against the alternative of just staking (APR LinkHook).

## Prior art / reference material

- Lido and Rocket Pool documentation — the reference implementations of liquid staking tokens (LSTs) that most LST-liquidity hooks are built around.
- EigenLayer whitepaper and documentation on restaking and Actively Validated Services (AVS) — the direct technical foundation for every "AVS-secured" hook in this dataset.
- Sanctum (Solana) — explicitly cited by one project (MultiSwap) as the cross-ecosystem precedent for a unified multi-LST liquidity layer.
