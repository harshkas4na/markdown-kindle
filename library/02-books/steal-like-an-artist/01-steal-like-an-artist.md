# 1. Steal Like an Artist

**The idea in one line:** nothing you make is truly original — it's assembled from everything you've read, watched, and admired — so the real skill is choosing your influences deliberately and transforming them honestly instead of pretending you invented them from nothing.

## Nothing is original

Every idea has a lineage. The painter learned from other painters, the musician from other musicians, going back further than anyone can trace. The myth of the isolated genius who invents something from a blank slate doesn't survive contact with how any working artist actually describes their own process — pull the thread on any "original" idea and you find a chain of earlier ideas it's built from. Accepting this isn't depressing, it's freeing: it means you don't need a lightning bolt of pure inspiration before you're allowed to start, you just need to start paying attention to what you already love.

## Imitate, then emulate

There's a useful distinction between copying one thing exactly and stealing well. Copying a single influence too closely just makes you a lesser version of them — a tribute act. The move is to draw from *many* influences at once, closely enough that you absorb how each of them thinks, until the combination of five or ten sources filtered through your own taste stops resembling any one of them. Early on this means deliberately imitating — studying a specific person's work closely enough to reproduce their technique — as practice. Over time, as more influences pile in and compete with each other in your head, the output stops looking like imitation and starts looking like a style.

## Draw your own genealogy

A concrete exercise: write down the people your current work most obviously draws from, and then go one layer deeper — who influenced *them*. This family tree does two things. It keeps you honest about what you're building on, so credit is easy to give rather than something you have to be caught not giving. And it usually turns up influences-of-influences that are more interesting to steal from directly than the people you started with, because they're a step further from what everyone in your immediate scene is already copying.

## You are what you let in

If everything you make is a remix of your inputs, curating those inputs is a creative act in itself. What you read, watch, and pay close attention to today is raw material for what you'll make later — treat the selection of influences with the same seriousness as the work itself, rather than consuming passively and hoping something original falls out.

## Takeaways for practice

- List your five biggest influences, then find and study *their* biggest influences.
- Deliberately imitate one person's specific technique as an exercise, not as your finished output.
- Mix enough different sources that no single one is recognizable in the result.
- Give credit as a habit — it costs nothing and it's accurate.
