# 6. The Secret: Do Good Work and Share It With People

**The idea in one line:** there's no trick that substitutes for having both halves — work that's actually good, and enough visibility for people to encounter it — because either half alone goes nowhere.

## Two failure modes

Creative careers tend to fail in one of two predictable ways. The first is quality without visibility: genuinely good work made in private, never shown to anyone, that nobody ever gets the chance to value because nobody knows it exists. The second is visibility without quality: aggressive self-promotion around work that isn't good enough yet to justify the attention, which burns trust rather than building it. Neither failure mode is really about talent — both are about mismanaging the relationship between the making and the showing.

## Do the work first

Of the two halves, quality has to come first chronologically, because promotion amplifies whatever you feed it — good work shown widely builds a reputation, but weak work shown widely just makes the weakness more visible, faster. The instinct to skip straight to marketing before the work can withstand scrutiny usually backfires; better to under-promote early, mediocre work and over-promote later work once it's actually solid.

## Then show it, plainly

Once the work is good, the second half doesn't need to be complicated. Simple, honest sharing — put the thing where people who might care about it can actually see it — is enough; elaborate marketing tactics are frequently an attempt to compensate for work that isn't good enough to speak for itself yet. Good work shown plainly tends to outperform mediocre work shown cleverly.

## The two halves reinforce each other

Sharing isn't only a distribution step tacked onto the end of making — the act of showing work, even in progress, generates feedback that makes the next round of work better, which makes it more worth sharing, which brings more feedback. Treated as a loop rather than two separate stages, "do good work" and "share it" compound each other over time instead of competing for attention.

## Takeaways for practice

- Before promoting something, ask honestly whether it's actually good yet — not whether it's finished.
- Default to plain, direct sharing over elaborate marketing; let the work carry the weight.
- Use sharing as a feedback loop, not just a distribution event — show work-in-progress, not only finished pieces.
- If growth has stalled, diagnose which half is missing before assuming you need "more marketing."
