# 9. Be Boring (It's the Only Way to Get Work Done)

**The idea in one line:** a stable, unglamorous daily routine — regular sleep, protected work hours, a low-drama life — is what actually produces a large body of work over a career, not the chaotic-genius lifestyle it gets romanticized as.

## The myth of the chaotic genius

Popular stories about creative people tend to foreground the volatile parts — late nights, disorder, dramatic personal lives — and quietly skip the parts where the same people showed up, day after day, to do the actual work. The drama makes for a better story after the fact, but it's rarely the mechanism that produced the output; it's usually a cost the work had to survive despite, not because of.

## Boring life, interesting work

There's a practical reason routine wins: creative energy and attention are finite, and chaos spends them on things unrelated to the work — recovering from a bad night, managing a self-inflicted crisis, dealing with the fallout of unpredictability. A life with fewer surprises leaves more of that finite energy actually available for the work itself. The people who sustain a large body of work across decades tend to have deliberately unremarkable daily lives, precisely so the remarkable part can happen on the page, canvas, or screen instead of in their personal life.

## Protect a routine

This doesn't require a rigid schedule down to the minute, but it does mean treating certain basics — enough sleep, a consistent time set aside for the work, limits on things that reliably derail the next day — as non-negotiable infrastructure rather than optional nice-to-haves. The routine isn't the opposite of creativity; it's the container that makes sustained creativity possible, by keeping the rest of life from constantly interrupting it.

## Discipline is what "inspiration" actually looks like from the outside

What looks like inspiration striking is more often just a person who reliably shows up to the same desk at the same time, so that when a good idea does arrive, there's already a working session ready to catch it. Waiting for inspiration to strike outside of any routine means missing most of the sessions where it would have shown up.

## Takeaways for practice

- Protect sleep and a consistent work block as seriously as you'd protect a client deadline.
- Notice when "creative chaos" in your life is actually just stealing energy from the work.
- Build a routine boring enough that showing up doesn't require willpower each time.
- Judge a working life by output over years, not by how eventful any single week looked.
