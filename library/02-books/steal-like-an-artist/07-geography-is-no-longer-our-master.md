# 7. Geography Is No Longer Our Master

**The idea in one line:** physical creative scenes still matter, but the internet means you no longer have to live inside one to participate in it.

## The old geography of art

For most of history, being part of a creative movement meant being physically present in a specific place — a city with the right cafés, studios, and cross-pollination of people working on similar problems. Proximity was the whole mechanism: ideas spread by being in the room, seeing the work in progress, running into the right person at the right event. If you weren't in that city, you were, for practical purposes, not in that conversation.

## The internet as a scene

That constraint has loosened. A community organized around a shared interest — a forum, a niche corner of social media, a group of people who follow each other's work — can now function like a scene without anyone occupying the same physical space. You can watch work in progress, respond to it, and be watched and responded to in turn, from anywhere with a connection. This doesn't eliminate the value of physical scenes, but it means they're no longer the only route in.

## Build both, deliberately

The strongest position isn't choosing one over the other — it's treating local and online community as two different, complementary networks. A local group gives you the things proximity is genuinely still good at: spontaneous conversation, seeing someone's actual working process, low-stakes collaboration. An online community gives you reach and access to people who share your specific interests but don't happen to live near you, which is often a much larger and more relevant pool than whoever happens to live in your city.

## Where you live is a choice again, not a requirement

Because participation no longer requires relocation, the decision of where to physically live can be made on its own merits — cost, quality of life, family — rather than as a forced tradeoff against creative opportunity. That's a genuinely new freedom compared to earlier eras, where being cut off from the right city could mean being cut off from the field entirely.

## Takeaways for practice

- Build an online presence and community deliberately, not as a lesser substitute for a physical scene.
- Still seek out local, in-person collaborators where you can — the two aren't in competition.
- Don't treat your city as a ceiling on what community you can be part of.
- Use online visibility to reach the specific people who care about your niche, wherever they are.
