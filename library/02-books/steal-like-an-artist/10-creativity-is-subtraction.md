# 10. Creativity Is Subtraction

**The idea in one line:** unlimited freedom — infinite time, tools, and options — tends to paralyze rather than help, while real constraints force the specific, interesting decisions that actually produce creative work.

## Limitless options paralyze

Faced with a truly blank slate — no deadline, no budget, no medium chosen, no theme — most people don't produce their best work, they produce nothing, because every choice is still open and none of them has been forced yet. The blank page isn't a gift, it's a problem: without any constraint to react against, there's no way to tell a good decision from a bad one, so decisions stall.

## Constraints as a creative engine

A limit — a tight deadline, a small budget, one tool instead of ten, a fixed word count — doesn't reduce what's possible so much as it reduces what's *available*, which is exactly what makes choosing among the remaining options tractable. Working within a real constraint forces trade-offs, and trade-offs are where interesting, specific decisions get made. This is why briefs, prompts, and rules exist in creative fields at all: not to restrict good work, but to make it findable inside an otherwise infinite space of options.

## Give yourself an artificial limit

When a project genuinely has no external constraint, the practical move is to invent one on purpose: pick one tool and forbid the rest, set an artificial deadline, cap the length, restrict the palette. The limit doesn't need to be the "correct" one — almost any consistent limit is more generative than no limit at all, because its function is to eliminate the paralysis of total freedom, not to enforce some ideal boundary.

## Subtraction, not addition, is the lever

The instinct when work feels flat is often to add more — more features, more options, more scope — but the more reliable fix is usually the opposite: cut something, narrow the brief, remove a tool from the table. What's left over after subtraction tends to be sharper and more specific than what was there when everything was still an option.

## Takeaways for practice

- When stuck at the start of a project, impose an artificial constraint immediately rather than waiting for a "natural" one.
- Treat a tight deadline or small toolset as an asset, not an obstacle.
- When work feels unfocused, try removing an option or a tool before adding a new one.
- Judge a constraint by whether it makes decisions easier to make, not by whether it feels limiting.
