# 5. Side Projects and Hobbies Are Important

**The idea in one line:** the work that pays the bills and the work you're most drawn to aren't always the same thing — and treating the second kind as disposable "just a hobby" throws away a real source of creative energy.

## Two kinds of work

Most people doing creative work have a day job — the thing that reliably pays — and a separate pull toward something else entirely, pursued in the margins with no clear payoff attached. It's tempting to treat the day job as the "real" work and the rest as indulgence, but the causality often runs the other way over a long career: the unpaid, unpressured side work is frequently where the most distinctive ideas develop, precisely because nothing commercial is riding on it yet.

## Hobbies without a business plan

A hobby pursued purely because it's enjoyable, with no audience or income attached, is protected from the pressures that shape "serious" work — pressure to be marketable, to fit a niche, to satisfy a client. That protection is valuable. Ideas that would get filtered out of paid work for being too weird, too narrow, or too impractical get to survive in a hobby, and some of those surviving ideas turn out later to be exactly the distinctive thing that separates your paid work from everyone else's.

## Play is where you take risks you can't take at work

Paid work usually comes with real stakes: a client, a deadline, a reputation to protect. That's not a place to experiment freely. A side project has none of those constraints, so it can absorb failed experiments that would be too costly to run in the paying work — and the experiments that do pay off can migrate back into the serious work once they've been proven out somewhere lower-stakes.

## Don't rush to monetize everything

There's a pull toward turning any successful hobby into income immediately. Doing that changes its function — the moment it has to perform for money, it stops being the pressure-free space it was, and you lose the thing that made it valuable as a hobby in the first place. Some side projects are worth keeping unmonetized on purpose, specifically so they keep doing the job only a no-stakes project can do.

## Takeaways for practice

- Maintain at least one project with genuinely no commercial goal attached to it.
- Let your paid work and your hobby work cross-pollinate deliberately — revisit hobby ideas when the paid work needs something fresh.
- Resist monetizing a hobby the moment it shows promise; ask what you'd lose first.
- Treat time spent on unpaid, unpressured projects as an investment, not as time stolen from "real" work.
