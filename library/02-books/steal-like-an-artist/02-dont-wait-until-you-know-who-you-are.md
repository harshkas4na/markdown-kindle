# 2. Don't Wait Until You Know Who You Are to Get Started

**The idea in one line:** you don't discover your voice by thinking about it first — you discover it by doing a lot of work and copying a lot of people, and the voice shows up later as a side effect.

## Voice is a byproduct, not a precondition

A common trap: waiting to feel like a "real" writer, designer, or engineer before starting, as though identity is a credential you earn in private and then bring to the work. It runs backward. The people whose style feels unmistakable didn't arrive at it by introspection — they arrived at it by producing a large volume of work, copying whoever they admired at the time, and letting the accumulated friction between all those influences slowly sand down into something recognizable. Style is a residue of doing, not a prerequisite for it.

## Copy more than one hero

Copying a single influence too closely just makes you their shadow. The fix isn't to copy less, it's to copy from more places at once. When you're pulling from several different, sometimes contradictory influences simultaneously, you're forced to make judgment calls about which parts to keep and which to drop — and those judgment calls, repeated over hundreds of decisions, are where something like "you" starts to show up. One influence produces an imitator. Many influences, actively synthesized, produce a point of view.

## The gap closes with volume, not deliberation

Early work rarely looks or sounds like the work you admire, and the instinct is to treat that gap as proof you're not ready yet. It's the opposite: the gap only closes through repetition. Every piece of finished work — even a mediocre one — teaches you something a plan never will, because it forces real decisions under real constraints. Waiting for clarity before starting guarantees you never get the only kind of practice that produces clarity.

## Start before permission arrives

Nobody is going to formally certify you as ready. The permission you're waiting for isn't coming from outside, and it isn't going to arrive as a feeling either — it arrives, if it ever does, as a retrospective observation after you've already been working for a while. The practical move is to treat "I don't know who I am yet" as irrelevant information and start anyway, using whoever you currently admire as scaffolding.

## Takeaways for practice

- Don't wait for a fully-formed style before shipping anything — style is downstream of output, not upstream.
- Actively copy multiple, even conflicting influences at once rather than mastering one.
- Treat each finished piece as a data point about your own judgment, not a verdict on your talent.
- Replace "am I ready" with "what would I make this week if I just started."
