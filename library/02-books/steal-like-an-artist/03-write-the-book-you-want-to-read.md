# 3. Write the Book You Want to Read

**The idea in one line:** the most reliable source of both motivation and audience is making the thing you personally wish existed but can't find.

## Make what's missing

When you notice a gap — a book, tool, or piece of writing you keep looking for and not finding — that gap is a brief, not just an annoyance. Someone with your specific combination of taste and frustration is well-positioned to fill it, precisely because you already know exactly what's wrong with the existing options. Treat the mental note "I wish someone had made this" as an actionable prompt rather than a passing complaint.

## You are the first audience

Making something you genuinely want, rather than guessing at what a hypothetical audience wants, solves a real problem: audience research is hard and often wrong, but you already have perfect access to one real audience member — yourself. If the thing satisfies you, you have proof it can satisfy at least one person, and you can trust your own reactions to it in a way you can't trust guesses about strangers. It also sustains you through the unglamorous middle of a long project, because the payoff isn't hypothetical — you'll actually use or read or watch the thing when it's done.

## Small and honest beats broad and vague

Aiming to please "everyone" tends to produce something that pleases no one in particular, because it has no specific point of view driving its decisions. Making the thing for a specific, honest reason — because *you* wanted it — gives every decision a clear tiebreaker: does this serve the reason I'm making this, or not. That specificity, oddly, is usually what makes the result resonate with far more people than "everyone" ever would have, because a particular, well-realized point of view reads as more trustworthy than a generic one.

## The gap you noticed isn't only yours

If you wanted something and couldn't find it, it's likely other people hit the same gap and also gave up looking. You don't need to survey them first — the fact that you searched and came up empty is itself decent evidence there's room for the thing you're about to make.

## Takeaways for practice

- Keep a running list of things you've wished existed but couldn't find — that list is a project backlog.
- When stuck on scope, ask "would I actually use this" before asking "would other people."
- Resist smoothing out the specific, personal reason you started — it's what gives the work a point of view.
- Trust your own dissatisfaction with existing options as real signal, not just pickiness.
