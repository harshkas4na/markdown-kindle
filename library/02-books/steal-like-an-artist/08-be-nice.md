# 8. Be Nice (The World Is a Small Town)

**The idea in one line:** creative fields are smaller and more reputational than they look from the outside, so how you treat people compounds — generosity opens doors years later, and burned bridges tend to stay burned.

## A small world with a long memory

Any given creative field, no matter how large it seems, is really a network of a few thousand people who keep encountering each other over decades — as collaborators, competitors, employers, and employees, often more than once and in different roles each time. Reputation travels through that network faster and further than any individual interaction suggests it will. A single ungenerous moment can precede you into a room you haven't entered yet.

## Give credit generously

Since the previous chapters already establish that everything is built on borrowed influence, generosity about crediting people isn't optional politeness — it's just accuracy. Naming your influences and collaborators costs the credit-giver nothing and gives the credited person something real: visibility they can use. It also signals confidence; only insecure work needs to hide its sources.

## Praise publicly, handle criticism privately

A simple, durable rule for how to treat people in public: when something is worth praising, say so where others can see it — public praise costs little and helps someone's reputation. When something needs correcting, do it privately if at all — public criticism mostly serves the critic's ego rather than actually helping the other person improve, and it's the kind of interaction people remember longest and least fondly.

## Kindness is a long-term strategy, not a soft one

None of this is really about being agreeable for its own sake — it's a recognition that a career is long, the field is small, and most opportunities eventually arrive through some person who remembers how you treated them once. Being difficult might feel justified in the moment; it rarely pays off across the multi-decade timescale a real career runs on.

## Takeaways for practice

- Default to naming people you learned from, worked with, or were inspired by, publicly and specifically.
- Praise in public; raise concerns in private, and only when it's actually useful to the other person.
- Treat every interaction as part of a long game, not a one-off — the field is smaller than it feels.
- When in doubt about how to handle a conflict, choose the version you'd be comfortable being remembered for.
