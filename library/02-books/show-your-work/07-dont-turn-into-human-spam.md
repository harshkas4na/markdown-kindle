# 7. Don't Turn Into Human Spam

**The idea in one line:** sharing only works as a two-way street — if you want attention but never give any, you're spam.

## Shut up and listen

Every field has them: the people who show up only to broadcast. They don't read others' work, don't ask questions, don't listen — they just push their own stuff, everywhere, at everyone. Kleon's blunt name for this is *human spam*.

The fix is participation before promotion. Sceniuses (chapter 1) run on exchange: you have to consume the scene — read the others, comment thoughtfully, amplify good work — to have any standing in it. If you want to be interesting, be *interested* first. The practical ratio most communities intuit: give far more than you ask.

A related trap: obsessing over follower counts. Chasing an audience directly is backwards — the audience is a *byproduct* of consistently good, generous sharing. As Kleon puts it, stop worrying about how many people follow you and start worrying about the quality of the people who do.

## You want hearts, not eyeballs

Raw traffic is the wrong metric. A million indifferent impressions are worth less than a hundred people who genuinely care. Optimize for depth of connection, not breadth of reach — hearts, not eyeballs.

## The vampire test

How do you decide what and whom to keep in your life? Kleon borrows a rule from artist Marina Abramović's world: the **vampire test**. After spending time with a person (or project, or community), check your energy. If it drains you, it's a vampire — cut it. If it energizes you, keep it close. Apply the same test to what you share and where you hang out online.

## Find your fellow travelers

The goal of all this sharing isn't fame — it's finding your *peers*: the small number of people doing the same weird thing you are (Kleon calls them "knuckleballers," after the tiny fraternity of pitchers who throw that pitch and trade tips even though they compete). Recognize them, connect with them, keep them.

And take it offline: **meet in meatspace**. Online connection becomes real friendship over coffee, meetups, and conferences. The internet finds your people; the real world cements them.

## Takeaways for practice

- Audit your ratio: comment on, share, and credit other people's work more often than you post your own.
- Track "hearts" (real conversations, repeat readers, collaborators) rather than counts.
- Run the vampire test quarterly on your platforms, projects, and communities.
