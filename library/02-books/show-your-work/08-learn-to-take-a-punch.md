# 8. Learn to Take a Punch

**The idea in one line:** putting work in public means getting hit — build the tolerance for criticism instead of avoiding the ring.

## Let 'em take their best shot

If you share work for long enough, criticism is guaranteed. The mistake is treating it as an emergency. Kleon's boxing framing: you can't dodge every punch, so train to *absorb* them. Concretely:

- **Relax.** The imagined catastrophe never matches reality; nobody dies of a bad comment. Breathe.
- **Practice getting hit.** Exposure builds tolerance. Share more, not less — the tenth harsh comment stings far less than the first.
- **Roll with the punches.** Keep moving; the answer to criticism is the next piece of work, not a defense of the last one.
- **Protect your vulnerable spots.** Some work is too close to the bone to share while it's raw. It's fine to keep certain things private until they can survive contact.
- **Keep your balance.** You are not your work. If a piece gets destroyed, *you* are still standing, and you can make more.

The deeper point: the fear of criticism silences more work than criticism ever does. The people who get anywhere are the ones who ship anyway, absorb the hits, and let the criticism inform (not dictate) the next thing.

## Don't feed the trolls

Critics come in two kinds. Real criticism engages with the work — even harshly — and is worth reading. **Trolls** aren't critiquing the work; they want a reaction from *you*. The rule: don't feed them. Don't reply, don't debate, delete and block without guilt. Kleon compares comment moderation to housekeeping — clearing vandalism isn't censorship, it's sweeping the floor of your own space.

A corollary about where you publish: on your own site you set the rules — another argument for owning your home base (chapter 3).

## Takeaways for practice

- Ship something small that scares you slightly; repeat until it doesn't.
- Sort incoming fire: engages-with-the-work → consider it; wants-a-reaction → delete, block, move on.
- Never let one loud response outweigh the quiet majority who liked or used the thing.
