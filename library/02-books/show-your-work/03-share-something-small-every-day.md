# 3. Share Something Small Every Day

**The idea in one line:** forget the big launch — a small, daily dispatch from your work compounds into an audience.

## Send out a daily dispatch

Overnight success stories are edited highlight reels; underneath is almost always a decade of unglamorous daily work. Since the work is daily anyway, the sharing can be too. Once a day, post *something* from your process: a sketch, a paragraph, a screenshot, a thing you learned, a work-in-progress shot, a link that shaped your thinking.

Where you are in a project suggests what to share:

- **Early stage:** share your influences and what's inspiring the direction.
- **Middle:** share methods, drafts, and works in progress.
- **Done:** share the finished piece, the leftovers, and what you learned.

Daily sharing has a hidden benefit beyond audience-building: it keeps you conscious of your own progress and creates useful pressure to have *done* something worth sharing.

## The "So what?" test

Daily doesn't mean indiscriminate. Before posting, ask: *is this useful, interesting, or entertaining to someone besides me?* If you hesitate, let it sit — post it later or not at all. Don't overshare the merely personal; share the personally *useful*. And the practical filter for the oversharing age: don't post anything you wouldn't want your boss, your mother, or your future self to read.

## Turn your flow into stock

Borrowing economist-turned-writer Robin Sloan's framing: **flow** is the feed — the daily posts and updates that scroll by and are forgotten. **Stock** is the durable stuff — the essays, guides, and projects that stay useful for years and keep finding new readers.

The trick is that stock is *made of* flow. Daily small pieces, reviewed and connected over time, reveal patterns; the patterns become the durable work. A year of daily notes becomes a talk; a series of posts becomes a book chapter. Keep the flow going, then periodically mine it.

## Build a good (domain) name

Social platforms are rented land — they change rules, decay, and die. Own your home base: a personal website at a domain you control, where all your sharing ultimately accumulates. It's the one place on the internet where you set the terms, and over years it becomes the durable record of who you are and what you've made.

## Takeaways for practice

- Set a tiny daily quota: one shared artifact per working day, however small.
- Apply the "so what?" filter — useful to at least one other human.
- Own your archive: post to your own site (or repo), syndicate everywhere else.
