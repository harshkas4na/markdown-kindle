# 6. Teach What You Know

**The idea in one line:** teaching your process doesn't create competitors — it creates an audience that's invested in you.

## Share your trade secrets

The instinct is to guard hard-won knowledge: if I show them how I do it, why would they need me? Kleon's counterexample is the barbecue legend whose restaurant openly demonstrates its whole method — and has *more* customers for it, because knowing how the thing is made makes people value it more, and because knowing *how* is a long way from being able to *do*.

Teaching is the strongest form of showing your work:

- **It deepens your own understanding.** You don't fully know a thing until you can explain it; teaching forces you to step back, examine your process, and articulate what you actually do.
- **It generates interest, not competition.** People who learn from you don't replace you — they follow you. A reader you've taught something is bonded to your work in a way a passive viewer never is.
- **It's the gift that recruits.** Every tutorial, guide, and explained decision is an act of generosity that compounds into reputation.

## Teach at every stage

You don't need mastery to teach — you need to be *one step ahead* of someone. The moment you learn something, you're the best-positioned person alive to teach it to the person right behind you, because you still remember what confused you (this connects back to the amateur advantage from chapter 1).

Practical forms: write the guide you wish you'd found, record the walkthrough, annotate your source, publish the checklist, answer the beginner's question thoroughly and in public.

## Takeaways for practice

- After every project, write the "how I did it" post — while the confusion is still fresh in memory.
- Treat every question you're asked twice as a signal: answer it once, in public, in durable form.
- Don't fear giving away the method. The method without the practice, taste, and reps is just a recipe — and sharing recipes is how chefs get famous.
