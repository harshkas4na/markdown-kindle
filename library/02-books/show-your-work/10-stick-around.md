# 10. Stick Around

**The idea in one line:** careers are marathons of chained projects — the people who win are mostly the people who didn't quit.

## Don't quit your show

Kleon's model of a creative career is a long-running show, not a single performance. The ones who make it are rarely the most talented; they're the ones still standing at year ten because they treated the work as ongoing. Every project ends — ship it, then start the next one. Don't let a failure end the show, and don't let a success end it either (resting on a hit is just a slower way of quitting).

## Chain-smoke

The image: light the next cigarette off the end of the last one. Applied to work — **use the end of one project to ignite the next**. Avoid the dead air of "what now?" by mining what you just finished: the leftover ideas, the questions the work raised, the thing you learned that deserves its own exploration. The last chapter of any project contains the first chapter of another, if you look.

This is also why documenting process (chapter 2) pays off at the end of a project: your own archive is the best source of next moves.

## Go away so you can come back

Sticking around doesn't mean never stopping. Burnout is real, and the antidote is strategic absence — Kleon's term is the **practical sabbatical**. Three everyday versions:

- **The commute** — in-between time where nobody can reach you; good thinking happens in transit.
- **Exercise and walks** — the body moves, the mind unclenches.
- **Nature and disconnection** — regular blocks of time genuinely offline.

And occasionally the bigger version: leave — travel, change contexts, take real time off — so you can return with fresh eyes. Absence isn't the opposite of sticking around; it's what makes sticking around sustainable.

## Begin again

The book ends where *Steal Like an Artist* begins: when you finish learning something, go back to the start and learn it deeper. Every ending is a commencement. Reread chapter 1 — you're an amateur again, at whatever comes next, and that's the advantage.

## Takeaways for practice

- Never end a project without writing down the three ideas it generated for the next one.
- Schedule disconnection like you schedule work — daily small doses, yearly big ones.
- The meta-rule of the whole book: share your work, every day, for years. Then keep going.
