# Injury-Proofing and Prehab

**The idea in one line:** fix the mobility and stability problems that cause injuries *before* they cause an injury, instead of treating rehab as something you only start after something already tore.

## Prehab vs. rehab

**Rehabilitation** happens after an injury, to restore lost function. **Prehabilitation ("prehab")** is the same category of corrective work, done proactively, on joints and movement patterns that are statistically likely to break down under continued training — the argument being that most training injuries aren't random bad luck, they're the predictable result of a mobility restriction or stability weakness that was there all along and eventually got loaded past its limit.

## Common problem areas addressed

- **Shoulders** — mobility and rotator-cuff stability work, given how much overhead and pressing volume typical strength training involves and how often shoulder impingement shows up as a result.
- **Knees** — tracking and stability work around the knee joint, particularly relevant given the compound, heavy-leg-focused lifts emphasized in the strength chapters.
- **Lower back** — core stability and hip mobility work aimed at protecting the spine during hinge-pattern lifts (deadlift-family movements), since poor hip mobility is argued to be a common hidden cause of people compensating with their lower back.
- **General joint mobility** as a category — the throughline being that restricted range of motion at one joint often shows up as an injury somewhere else, because the body finds compensation patterns rather than simply refusing to move.

## Why this chapter sits next to sleep and sex

Recovery capacity — not just training stimulus — determines whether the strength protocols earlier in the book actually produce progress instead of injury. This chapter is the connective tissue between "train hard" (Occam's Protocol) and "actually be able to keep training hard for years," making it as much a longevity chapter as a training chapter.

## Takeaways for practice

- Treat mobility and stability work as a permanent line item in training, not something you add only after an injury.
- Shoulders, knees, and hips/lower back are the specific high-yield areas called out — prioritize those over generic full-body stretching.
- Pair this chapter with Occam's Protocol: heavy compound lifting without matching mobility work is presented as the actual injury mechanism, not the lifting itself.
