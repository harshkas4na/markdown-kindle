# Engineering Better Sleep

**The idea in one line:** treat sleep as a variable you can measure and tune, the same way the rest of the book treats diet and training, instead of something you either "have trouble with" or don't.

## Measure before you intervene

Consistent with the book's opening self-experimentation chapter, the sleep material starts with the same instruction: get an actual baseline (sleep tracking, or at minimum a consistent log of time-to-fall-asleep and how rested you feel) before trying interventions, so you can tell whether any single change actually moved the number.

## Environmental and behavioral levers

- **Temperature** — a cooler room is favored, tied to the body's natural core-temperature drop as part of falling asleep; a room that's too warm is framed as one of the most common, most fixable sleep saboteurs.
- **Light exposure** — bright light, especially blue-spectrum light, close to bedtime is treated as directly counter to the body's evening melatonin signal; darkness (blackout curtains, dimming screens) is the corrective.
- **Consistency of timing** — a stable wake time (even more than a stable bedtime) is emphasized as the stronger lever for regulating the body's internal clock.
- **Caffeine cutoff timing** — pushed earlier in the day than most people default to, given caffeine's multi-hour half-life.

## Sleep-cycle framing and naps

The chapter leans on the idea of **90-minute sleep cycles**: waking up mid-cycle (in deep sleep) tends to feel much worse than waking at a cycle boundary, so timing sleep duration in roughly 90-minute increments (e.g. targeting waking at the end of a cycle rather than an arbitrary clock time) is offered as a way to feel more rested even without adding total sleep time. The same cycle logic is applied to short naps — a nap kept short enough to avoid dropping into deep sleep avoids the grogginess of being woken mid-cycle.

## Supplement and biohack experiments

A handful of sleep-onset aids are discussed in the same self-experiment framing as PAGG — things Ferriss tested on himself (certain amino acids and minerals associated with relaxation/sleep onset) rather than a universally prescribed stack, alongside simpler non-supplement tactics like a genuinely dark, cool room being treated as higher-leverage than any pill.

## Takeaways for practice

- Fix environment first (temperature, light, timing) — it's the highest-leverage, lowest-cost category here.
- A consistent wake time does more for sleep quality than most people expect, independent of bedtime.
- If you nap, keep it short and treat sleep duration in ~90-minute blocks rather than round clock numbers.
