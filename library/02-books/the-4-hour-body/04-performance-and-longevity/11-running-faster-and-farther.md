# Running Faster and Farther

**The idea in one line:** most running injuries and speed ceilings come from *how* people strike the ground, not from a lack of fitness — so fixing form is a higher-leverage lever than just running more miles.

## The form-first argument

The chapter's core claim is that the standard heel-strike running gait most people default to (encouraged, in this framing, by decades of heavily cushioned running shoe design) creates a hard braking force with every step, which both caps efficiency and is a major contributor to chronic running injuries — shin splints, knee pain, and similar overuse issues. Rather than treating those injuries as a fitness or toughness problem to push through, the chapter treats them as a biomechanics problem to fix.

## The corrective technique

Ferriss draws on minimalist/**POSE-style running** technique — landing on the midfoot or forefoot under the body's center of mass, rather than reaching the foot forward and striking heel-first ahead of the body. The claimed benefits:
- **Less braking force per step**, since a midfoot landing under the hips doesn't act like a forward-planted brake the way a heel-strike ahead of the body does.
- **More elastic energy return**, using the foot and calf's natural spring rather than absorbing impact directly through the joints.
- **Lower injury rates** for the specific overuse injuries tied to repeated heel-strike impact.

This is presented as a skill to relearn deliberately — shortening stride, increasing cadence (more steps per minute, each one shorter), and consciously landing under the body — rather than something that fixes itself by simply switching shoes.

## Why this belongs in the "performance and longevity" part

Distance and speed work is framed less as its own isolated goal and more as testing ground for the book's general philosophy: a technique change (form) can outperform a volume change (more miles) for the same outcome, echoing the Minimum Effective Dose idea from the book's opening chapter, just applied to running instead of dieting or lifting.

## Takeaways for practice

- Before adding mileage, check whether stride and foot-strike are actually the bottleneck — more volume on a bad pattern mostly means more repetitions of the injury-causing motion.
- Shortening stride and increasing cadence is the practical entry point into a midfoot-strike pattern.
- Change gait gradually — like every protocol in this book, this is something to test on yourself over weeks, not switch overnight.
