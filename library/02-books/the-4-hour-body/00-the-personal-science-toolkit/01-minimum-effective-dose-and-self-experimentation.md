# Minimum Effective Dose and Self-Experimentation

**The idea in one line:** don't ask "what's the best way to do this," ask "what's the *least* I can do to get this specific result" — then go find out for yourself, on yourself.

## The minimum effective dose

Ferriss borrows a pharmacology concept: the **minimum effective dose (MED)** is the smallest input that produces a given output. Past that point, more input doesn't produce more result — it just produces more input. Boiling water at sea level is 100°C; 120°C boils it no "more." Most fitness and diet advice ignores this and treats effort as if it were linear with outcome — train harder, eat stricter, do more — when the real skill is finding the smallest lever that actually moves the number you're chasing.

This reframes the whole book: every protocol that follows is presented as "the least you can do for this specific effect," not "everything that might help." That's also why the book reads as a series of narrow, aggressive tactics instead of one balanced lifestyle plan — each chapter is optimizing one dial in isolation.

## Self-experimentation as the method

Ferriss's core methodology, borrowed loosely from the "quantified self" and personal-science movements, is **N=1 experimentation**: treat your own body as the test subject, pick one variable, measure a real number before and after, change only that variable, and see what happens. This is explicitly *not* the same standard of evidence as a controlled clinical trial — he says so directly and repeatedly — but he argues it's still more useful to an individual than population-level averages, because you are not the average of the population a study was run on.

The practical toolkit he pushes throughout the book:
- **Pick a small number of measurable markers** (bodyfat %, waist circumference, a specific lift, a resting heart rate, a blood marker) instead of vague feelings.
- **Baseline first.** Measure before you change anything.
- **Change one variable at a time**, or you won't know what caused what.
- **Give it a real window** — days to weeks, not one session — before judging.
- **Distrust averages, trust your own data.** Individual response to diet, drugs, and training varies more than most advice admits.

## Why this matters before any of the specific protocols

Every later chapter (slow-carb, cold exposure, Occam's Protocol, sleep engineering) is a *specific instance* of this same pattern: isolate one lever, measure one outcome, run a short aggressive test, keep what works for your body and discard what doesn't. Reading the rest of the book as a fixed prescription misses the point — Ferriss's actual claim is narrower and more honest: "this worked for me and for people I coached, here's the mechanism I believe explains it, go test it on yourself and keep only what replicates."

## Takeaways for practice

- Before starting any protocol, write down the one number you're trying to move and how you'll measure it weekly.
- Change one thing at a time — stacking five interventions at once destroys your ability to learn anything.
- Treat every protocol in this book (and elsewhere) as a hypothesis to test on yourself, not a rule to obey.
