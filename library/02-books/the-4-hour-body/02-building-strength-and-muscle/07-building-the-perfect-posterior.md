# Building the Perfect Posterior

**The idea in one line:** the glutes are the largest muscle group in the body and are chronically undertrained by generic leg workouts that let the quads and hamstrings take over the work.

## Why glutes get skipped by default

Ferriss's argument is mechanical: many common "leg day" exercises (squats, leg press, lunges as usually performed) let the quads and hamstrings dominate the movement, especially if range of motion is shallow or the exerciser leans forward, so the glutes end up doing far less work than their size and strength potential would suggest. Because most people never feel a strong glute-specific contraction, they assume the muscle is simply "responding slowly" rather than recognizing it isn't being meaningfully targeted at all.

## The corrective approach

The protocol emphasizes exercises and cues that intentionally isolate the glutes rather than letting them ride along as a secondary mover:
- **Deeper range of motion and hip-hinge-dominant movements** (rather than knee-dominant ones) to shift more of the load onto the hip extensors.
- **Deliberate mind-muscle focus and pauses at peak contraction**, so the set is judged by whether the glutes are fatigued afterward, not just whether a weight moved.
- **Higher-rep, higher-frequency work specifically for this muscle group**, distinct from the low-frequency Occam's Protocol approach used for overall strength — the reasoning given is that glute fibers respond well to a different rep/frequency profile than the compound-lift protocol described for general strength.
- **Targeted isolation movements** (hip-thrust and bridge-style movements, in particular) that make it mechanically difficult for the quads or hamstrings to compensate.

## How this fits with Occam's Protocol

This chapter is best read as a specialization, not a contradiction, of the general strength chapter: Occam's Protocol optimizes for whole-body strength efficiently; this chapter argues one specific, oft-neglected muscle group benefits from a different rep range and more deliberate isolation work layered on top, because it's disproportionately easy to train around it entirely without noticing.

## Takeaways for practice

- If a "leg" exercise doesn't produce a felt, sustained glute contraction, it's probably being dominated by another muscle group — the fix is form and range of motion, not just heavier weight.
- Hip-hinge movements and isolation work (hip thrusts/bridges) are the direct correctives called out here.
- This is a good candidate for the book's own self-experimentation method: track a specific lift and a felt-effort log before assuming a change in exercise selection is working.
