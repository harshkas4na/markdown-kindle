# The Slow-Carb Diet

**The idea in one line:** a small set of "don't eat this" rules, repeated every meal, beats calorie counting — plus one planned cheat day a week to keep it sustainable.

## The five rules

Ferriss's flagship diet is defined by exclusion, not measurement — no calorie counting, no weighing food. The rules, applied at every meal, six days a week:

1. **Avoid "white" starchy carbohydrates** — bread, rice, pasta, potatoes, cereal. (Notably, this isn't "low carb" in the keto sense — vegetables are unlimited.)
2. **Eat the same few meals repeatedly.** Pick 3–4 meals you like and rotate them, so decisions become automatic instead of a daily negotiation.
3. **Don't drink calories.** No fruit juice, no soda, no milk; black coffee and tea and, per Ferriss, wine in moderation are fine.
4. **Don't eat fruit.** Framed as a fructose/liver-glycogen argument rather than a "fruit is bad" claim.
5. **Every meal = protein + legumes/beans + vegetables.** The protein-plus-legume combination is meant to be filling and stabilize blood sugar without needing starches.

## The mechanism he argues for

The claimed mechanism is blood-sugar and insulin stabilization: starchy/refined carbs and sugary drinks spike blood glucose, which spikes insulin, which promotes fat storage and triggers hunger swings a few hours later. By removing the spike-inducing foods and front-loading protein and fiber (legumes, vegetables), the claim is you stay satiated on fewer total calories without consciously restricting them. This is presented as Ferriss's synthesis of low-glycemic-diet ideas rather than a novel scientific discovery — the "own words" version: eat things that don't spike your blood sugar, and satiety takes care of portion control for you.

## The weekly cheat day

Once a week (traditionally Saturday), the plan flips completely: eat whatever you want, including the exact foods excluded the other six days. Two justifications are offered:
- **Psychological:** total restriction fails long-term because willpower is finite; a guaranteed release valve makes the other six days sustainable instead of a countdown to failure.
- **Metabolic:** a claimed leptin/metabolism benefit from an occasional large calorie and carbohydrate spike, preventing the "starvation" adaptation that slows metabolic rate on continuous restriction.

The cheat day is a scheduled, all-in binge, not "a slightly bigger meal" — the point is a genuine, planned break, not a slow leak of small daily exceptions to the rules.

## Takeaways for practice

- Build a short rotating meal list (protein + beans + veg) so you never have to decide what to eat from scratch.
- Cut liquid calories and white starches first — they're the highest-leverage exclusions.
- Put the cheat day on the calendar in advance; it's a feature of the plan, not a failure of it.
