# Law 8: Make Other People Come to You — Use Bait if Necessary

**The idea in one line:** the person who initiates a move is usually the one taking on risk and revealing intent — it's structurally better to be the one who gets approached.

## The psychology

Chasing anything — a deal, a person, an opportunity — puts you in a weaker negotiating position almost by definition, because you've signaled that you want it more than the other side needs to give it to you. The party who waits, or who arranges things so the other side arrives first, keeps the advantage of reacting to a known situation rather than committing to an unknown one. Whoever moves first in a negotiation typically reveals more about their position, their urgency, and their constraints than they intend to.

## The "bait" mechanism

Making people come to you rarely happens by accident — it usually requires constructing a reason for them to. That can be as simple as visible scarcity (something clearly in demand attracts approach on its own), or as deliberate as creating a situation where the other party concludes, on their own, that reaching out serves their interest. The key distinction from simple manipulation is that the bait has to offer something genuinely valuable to the other side too — a pure trap without real value on offer gets noticed and burns trust once it's understood.

## Where this fails

Not every situation rewards passivity. Waiting for someone to come to you when they have no actual incentive to just results in nothing happening — the law depends on there being real leverage or genuine value on your side that makes waiting rational rather than just avoidant. Passivity dressed up as strategy, when you actually have no leverage, is just inaction with a better story attached.

## Practical reading

The useful version of this law is less about literal waiting and more about *positioning*: building a track record, a body of work, or a reputation substantial enough that opportunities arrive without you having to chase every one individually. It also means recognizing, in any specific negotiation, whether urgency is on your side or the other side's — and if it's not on yours, finding a way to signal patience rather than eagerness, even when you don't feel it.
