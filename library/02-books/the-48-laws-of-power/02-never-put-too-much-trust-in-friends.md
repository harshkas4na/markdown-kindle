# Law 2: Never Put Too Much Trust in Friends, Learn How to Use Enemies

**The idea in one line:** friends are more dangerous than enemies in one specific way — you don't watch them, and when they betray you it's a surprise; enemies you already watch closely, so their moves rarely catch you off guard.

## The psychology

A friendship carries an unspoken contract: familiarity, favors, shared history. When a friend is handed real power over you — a promotion that makes them your boss, access to something valuable — that contract gets tested against self-interest, and self-interest quietly tends to win more often than the friendship narrative admits. The betrayal stings extra hard precisely because you weren't guarding against it. An enemy, by contrast, has already declared their incentives. You know exactly what they want and you've built in defenses accordingly.

There's also a subtler mechanism: friends hired or promoted on the basis of affection rather than competence tend to perform worse and resent the asymmetry of the relationship — they know they got the position partly through charity, which breeds a specific kind of quiet resentment rather than gratitude.

## The "use enemies" half

The more counterintuitive half of the law is that a former enemy, once won over, often becomes a more loyal and more useful ally than a friend who was never tested. They have something to prove. They know exactly how easily the relationship could have gone the other way, and that awareness produces effort a comfortable friendship rarely does. Historical figures who deliberately converted rivals into lieutenants weren't being naive about human nature — they were exploiting exactly this dynamic.

## Practical reading

This isn't an argument against having friends. It's an argument against *conflating* friendship with a hiring or delegation decision. Before handing someone real leverage over your position, ask whether you'd choose them on competence and incentive-alignment alone, stripped of the relationship. If the answer changes when you strip the friendship away, that's the signal to be careful — not necessarily to say no, but to build in the same checks you'd build in for anyone else.

## The failure mode to watch for

The riskiest version of this law is applying it as cynicism rather than diligence — treating every friend as a suspect drains relationships of the trust that makes them worth having in the first place. The useful version is narrower: keep the emotional friendship and the professional structure separately audited, so one doesn't quietly corrupt the other.
