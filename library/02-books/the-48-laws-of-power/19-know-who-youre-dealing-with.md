# Law 19: Know Who You're Dealing With — Do Not Offend the Wrong Person

**The idea in one line:** the same action lands completely differently depending on who's on the receiving end — treating everyone with an identical playbook guarantees you'll eventually apply the wrong approach to someone who reacts in a way you didn't plan for.

## The psychology

People vary enormously in what they'll tolerate, how they retaliate, how long they hold a grudge, and what actually motivates them — and most social and professional friction comes not from bad intentions but from misreading which kind of person you're actually dealing with. A tactic that works cleanly on someone conflict-averse can badly backfire on someone who responds to pressure with disproportionate, sustained retaliation. Confidence in a "read" of someone that's actually wrong is more dangerous than uncertainty, because it produces decisive action based on a false model.

## The practical skill this points to

This law is really an argument for observation before action — paying attention to how someone has handled comparable situations before, what they seem to value, how they respond under pressure, before committing to an approach with them. It also flags a specific category of risk: people who seem harmless or easy to overlook — quiet, low-status, or unassuming — occasionally turn out to have outsized capacity for retaliation or influence that wasn't visible on the surface, and dismissing them without checking is a common, avoidable mistake.

## Where this can go wrong

Overapplied, this becomes a kind of constant threat-assessment mode that's exhausting and treats every interaction as a risk calculation rather than a normal human exchange — most people, most of the time, are not landmines requiring careful study before engagement. The law is most relevant in higher-stakes situations — a real negotiation, a real conflict, a decision that could provoke a real reaction — not everyday low-stakes interactions, where the cost of misjudging someone is trivial.

## Practical reading

Before an important interaction — a negotiation, a piece of critical feedback, any move that could provoke a reaction — it's worth deliberately asking what you actually know about how this specific person responds to pressure or conflict, rather than defaulting to a generic approach calibrated for an average person who may not resemble them at all.
