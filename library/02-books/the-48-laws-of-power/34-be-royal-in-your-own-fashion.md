# Law 34: Be Royal in Your Own Fashion — Act Like a King to Be Treated Like One

**The idea in one line:** how you carry yourself sets the baseline other people use to decide how to treat you — self-deprecation and visible insecurity invite exactly the diminished treatment they seem to be pre-emptively apologizing for.

## The psychology

People take strong cues from how someone presents themselves when they don't have much other information to go on — and even when they do, self-presentation still colors interpretation of everything else. Someone who carries themselves with quiet confidence, who doesn't over-apologize or diminish their own contributions, tends to be granted more respect and deference by default, independent of the objective merits of the situation. Conversely, visible self-doubt, excessive apologizing, or reflexively small self-presentation signals to others that lower regard is acceptable — and people generally take the cue.

## Why this isn't the same as arrogance

There's a meaningful difference between quiet self-respect and loud self-promotion. Genuine, grounded confidence tends to read as calm and secure rather than as showing off — it doesn't need external validation constantly, and it doesn't require putting others down to establish itself. Arrogance and insecurity, ironically, often look similar from a distance (both involve an outsized need to be seen as impressive) but read very differently up close, and people are usually good at eventually telling them apart.

## Where this actually helps

This matters most in ambiguous situations where status hasn't been externally established — a new room, a negotiation with a stranger, any interaction where the other party doesn't yet have independent information about your actual standing. In those moments, self-presentation is doing a disproportionate amount of the work in shaping how you're treated, simply because there's nothing else yet to go on.

## Practical reading

This isn't a license for entitlement or for demanding treatment that hasn't been earned — it's an observation that reflexive self-diminishment costs more than people realize, and that carrying real, grounded confidence (distinct from performative confidence) tends to be self-fulfilling in a way that's worth being deliberate about, especially in situations where first impressions carry outsized weight.
