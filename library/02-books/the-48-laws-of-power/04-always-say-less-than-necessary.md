# Law 4: Always Say Less Than Necessary

**The idea in one line:** the more you talk, the more ordinary and controllable you seem, and the more ammunition you hand people for later use against you.

## The psychology

Silence reads as weight. When someone says little, listeners fill the gap with their own assumptions — usually more flattering ones than reality would justify. Talk too much, on the other hand, and you inevitably say something imprecise, something contradictory, something that reveals more than you meant to. Volume of speech is inversely related to control over how you're perceived; every extra sentence is another data point someone else gets to use in forming their opinion of you, and most of those data points weren't necessary to give away.

There's a related effect: people who talk under pressure — nervous energy filling silence — tend to negotiate worse, because the other side simply waits them out and lets the talker undercut their own position.

## Where this shows up

Negotiations are the clearest case: the side that stays quiet longer usually extracts more, because silence puts pressure on the other party to fill it, often with concessions. But the pattern is broader — vague or brief answers to probing questions preserve more optionality than detailed ones, and a reputation for saying little makes the few things you do say land with more weight, precisely because they're rare.

## The failure mode

Taken too far, this becomes stonewalling, and stonewalling reads as either arrogance or evasiveness rather than gravitas — the effect depends entirely on context and relationship, not just on word count. Silence from someone with no established credibility just looks like they have nothing to say. The law works because it's selectively applied by people who've already earned the right to be listened to; used as a blanket personality trait by someone who hasn't, it can just as easily read as awkwardness.

## Practical reading

The actionable version isn't "talk less in general" — it's noticing the specific moments where saying nothing costs you nothing and where saying something costs you information, leverage, or the high ground. Answer the question that was actually asked, not the three adjacent ones nobody asked. Resist the urge to fill silence just because it's uncomfortable; the discomfort is usually doing more work for you than more words would.
