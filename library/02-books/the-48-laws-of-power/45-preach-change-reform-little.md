# Law 45: Preach the Need for Change, but Never Reform Too Much at Once

**The idea in one line:** people say they want change, but what they actually tolerate well is much smaller and slower than what they endorse in the abstract — sweeping, sudden reform tends to provoke exactly the resistance it was meant to overcome.

## The psychology

Existing habits, systems, and routines carry a kind of psychological comfort just by being familiar, independent of whether they're actually working well. When change arrives abruptly and comprehensively, it strips away that familiarity all at once, and the resulting disorientation often gets blamed on the change itself rather than on whatever problem the change was meant to fix. Gradual change allows people to adjust incrementally, keeping enough of the familiar intact at each step that the new normal never triggers full-blown resistance — even if the cumulative result, over time, ends up just as transformative as the abrupt version would have been.

## Why this matters even when the abrupt version is technically correct

Being right about what needs to change doesn't guarantee the change survives contact with the people who have to live through it — reform that provokes enough resistance gets rolled back, sabotaged, or simply outlasted by people waiting it out, regardless of whether it was the objectively better plan. A slower, more tolerable version of the same reform that actually survives and sticks accomplishes more, in practice, than a faster, more disruptive version that gets reversed.

## The real cost of this approach

Gradualism has genuine costs of its own — problems that need urgent fixing don't wait patiently while change is phased in carefully, and there are situations (crises, genuinely urgent structural problems) where the slow, careful version arrives too late to matter. This law is a reasonable default for change management in stable conditions, not a universal rule for every situation, including ones where speed itself is the more important variable.

## Practical reading

Before implementing significant change, it's worth asking honestly whether the situation actually requires speed, or whether speed is being chosen out of impatience rather than necessity — and if the latter, a more incremental rollout that lets people adjust is usually more likely to actually stick than a comprehensive one imposed all at once.
