# Law 1: Never Outshine the Master

**The idea in one line:** when someone above you holds the power to help or hurt your position, making them feel outclassed is a self-inflicted wound — insecurity in a superior curdles into resentment, and resentment gets you cut loose.

## The psychology

People in authority want to believe their position is earned, not merely held. A subordinate who is visibly sharper, faster, or more charming doesn't read as an asset to most insecure superiors — it reads as a live comparison they're losing. Competence isn't the threat; competence *displayed carelessly*, in front of others, in a way that makes the boss look slow by contrast, is the threat. Greene's broader point is that talent alone has never been sufficient for survival in a hierarchy — the management of how that talent is perceived matters just as much.

## How it plays out

The failure mode is rarely intentional sabotage. It's usually someone competent and a little tone-deaf: correcting a superior's mistake in a meeting instead of privately, being the one who gets the laugh the boss was going for, solving the problem the boss couldn't solve and making sure everyone knows it. None of that is malicious. All of it is remembered.

The corrective isn't to under-perform — that creates its own problems and rarely goes unnoticed either. It's to route credit thoughtfully: let a superior's contribution be visible even when yours did the heavier lifting, frame your ideas as extensions of theirs rather than replacements, and save the moments where you genuinely need to shine for audiences where the superior isn't the one being compared.

## The limit of this law

This only holds while you actually need the relationship. Once you're no longer dependent on that person's goodwill — you've moved on, you have leverage of your own, or the relationship has become adversarial anyway — the calculus flips, and playing small stops being strategy and starts being just habit. The skill isn't perpetual self-dimming; it's reading correctly which relationships still require it.

## Where it applies beyond formal hierarchies

The same dynamic shows up with mentors, with senior collaborators, even with parents — anyone whose self-image depends partly on being ahead of you. The tell that you've crossed the line isn't a single incident, it's a pattern of that person becoming subtly less warm, less forthcoming with opportunities, more likely to find fault. By the time it's obvious, the damage is usually already done.
