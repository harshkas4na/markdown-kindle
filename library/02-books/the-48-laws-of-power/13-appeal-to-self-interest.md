# Law 13: When Asking for Help, Appeal to People's Self-Interest, Never to Their Mercy or Gratitude

**The idea in one line:** "help me because you owe me" or "help me because I'm struggling" asks someone to act against a cooler calculation of their own interest — "help me because it benefits you" doesn't require them to be generous at all, just self-interested, which is a far more reliable trait to depend on.

## The psychology

Gratitude fades fast — favors done for someone months or years ago carry much less emotional weight in the present than they did when they happened, no matter how large they felt at the time. Mercy is even less reliable as a lever, since it depends entirely on the other person's current mood, values, and how compelling your situation seems to them in that specific moment. Both routes make your outcome contingent on someone else's generosity, which is a variable you don't control and can't really predict.

Self-interest, by contrast, barely requires trust in the other person's character at all. If helping you is straightforwardly good for them — a favor that sets up a future exchange, a request that also solves a problem they have — the ask is far more likely to land, because you're not asking them to be better than usual, just consistent.

## How to actually apply it

The practical shift is in how a request gets framed, not necessarily in what's being asked. Instead of "I really need this," the stronger version explains what's in it for the other party — even a modest, honestly stated benefit tends to outperform an appeal to sympathy, because it gives the other person a reason that doesn't depend on how generous they're feeling that day.

## The limit of this law

Real relationships built on trust and reciprocity over time absolutely do involve favors, gratitude, and goodwill, and treating every single interaction as a self-interest calculation is both exhausting and, eventually, detectable — people notice when they're only ever approached transactionally. The law is most applicable to one-off or high-stakes asks, particularly with people who don't already have a deep reserve of goodwill toward you, where you can't afford to be wrong about how much mercy or gratitude is actually available.
