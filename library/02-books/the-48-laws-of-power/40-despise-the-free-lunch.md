# Law 40: Despise the Free Lunch

**The idea in one line:** things offered for free almost always carry hidden costs — obligation, diminished value, or strings attached — that a fair price would have made explicit up front.

## The psychology

Paying for something establishes a clean, understood transaction: value exchanged for value, with no lingering ambiguity about what's owed. Accepting something free creates a much murkier situation — an implicit sense of obligation that isn't formally specified, isn't capped in advance, and can be called in later on terms you didn't negotiate. The "free" framing also tends to lower scrutiny of the thing itself; people evaluate free offers far less critically than priced ones, in part because the absence of a price tag makes it feel like there's nothing to lose by accepting.

## Where this shows up

Unsolicited favors from people who don't typically offer favors without reason, free services or products from businesses whose actual revenue model isn't obvious, generous gestures early in a relationship that seem to establish a debt rather than genuine goodwill — in each case, the useful question isn't "is this good" but "what is this actually costing, and to whom, since it isn't costing me money." Value that seems to come from nowhere usually comes from somewhere; the cost has just been moved somewhere less visible.

## The honest counterexample this law has to contend with

Genuine generosity, without hidden strings, is real and common — friends do favors, mentors give time, some things really are given freely without an implicit invoice attached. Treating every generous gesture with transactional suspicion is both exhausting and a poor way to move through relationships that depend on actual trust and goodwill to function. The skill this law describes is discernment, not blanket cynicism.

## Practical reading

The useful habit isn't rejecting free things on principle — it's pausing briefly to ask what the actual incentive is behind an offer that costs the giver something, since most people and most businesses don't give away real value without a reason. Where the reason is legitimate goodwill, accept it in that spirit. Where it isn't obvious, that's worth noticing before accepting.
