# Law 26: Keep Your Hands Clean

**The idea in one line:** the unpleasant, necessary, or morally ambiguous actions that keep any operation running have to happen — but being personally and visibly tied to them costs more than delegating them ever would.

## The psychology

Reputation is built on the visible pattern of what you're seen doing, and difficult or unpleasant actions — layoffs, discipline, hard negotiating tactics, cutting ties — tend to be remembered vividly and attributed personally to whoever executed them. It's possible to want, even require, a certain outcome without being the one whose fingerprints are directly on the harder part of achieving it. Delegating execution while retaining the decision preserves the benefit of the outcome without absorbing the reputational cost of the method.

## Where this is legitimate management

In any organization of reasonable size, this is close to ordinary structure rather than manipulation: a leader sets direction and outcomes, and specific difficult implementations are handled by the appropriate function or person, rather than the top of the hierarchy personally executing every hard call. That's not evasion — it's how delegation and division of labor normally work, and it's reasonable for someone setting overall strategy not to be the one, say, personally delivering every piece of bad news generated by that strategy.

## Where this becomes evasive rather than efficient

The uncomfortable edge of this law is using distance from a decision to avoid accountability for it — publicly disclaiming responsibility for an outcome you privately directed, or deliberately structuring plausible deniability so blame lands elsewhere when things go wrong. That's a different thing from ordinary delegation, and it tends to be recognized for what it is once patterns repeat, damaging trust more than owning a hard decision directly would have.

## Practical reading

There's a real and useful distinction between delegating *execution* of hard decisions — normal, often necessary — and avoiding *accountability* for the decisions themselves — corrosive, and eventually visible. The first is functional structure. The second is the part of this law worth being skeptical of, both in how you apply it and in how you read it from people above you.
