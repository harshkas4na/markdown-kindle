# Law 46: Never Appear Too Perfect

**The idea in one line:** someone with no visible flaws doesn't inspire pure admiration for long — it tends to curdle into envy and a quiet desire to see them fail, since a flawless image leaves no point of relatable contact.

## The psychology

Admiration and envy are closely related emotions, and which one dominates depends heavily on perceived relatability. A person who is impressive but also visibly human — with acknowledged flaws, occasional mistakes, ordinary struggles — remains relatable, which keeps admiration from tipping over into resentment. A person who presents as flawless removes that relatable footing, and observers start looking, consciously or not, for a crack in the image, partly because a completely flawless presentation itself starts to feel less like reality and more like a performance being maintained.

## Why this happens even when the "perfection" is genuine

This isn't really about whether the underlying excellence is authentic — it's about presentation. Someone genuinely excellent who is also open about occasional struggles or minor flaws reads as impressive *and* human. The same level of genuine excellence, presented without any acknowledged imperfection, reads as either inauthentic or as an implicit reminder of others' comparative shortcomings — and either read tends to generate quiet resentment rather than goodwill.

## How this gets managed in practice

This often shows up as strategically revealing a minor, non-threatening flaw or admitting a small mistake, which humanizes an otherwise very strong track record without undercutting the substance of it. The flaw revealed matters — it should be minor and relatable, not one that calls the core competence into question, or it stops humanizing and starts undermining.

## Practical reading

Consistently strong performance doesn't need to be hidden, but it's worth being deliberate about occasionally showing the ordinary, human side alongside it — an acknowledged mistake, a visible struggle, a moment of not having the answer — since an unbroken record of visible perfection tends to generate quiet resentment that undercuts the goodwill the performance should otherwise be earning.
