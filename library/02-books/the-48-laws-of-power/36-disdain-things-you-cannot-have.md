# Law 36: Disdain Things You Cannot Have — Ignoring Them Is the Best Revenge

**The idea in one line:** visibly wanting something you can't get, or reacting to a slight, gives it — and the person or situation behind it — power over your attention and emotional state that indifference simply refuses to grant.

## The psychology

Attention is itself a resource, and directing it toward something you can't influence or obtain is a pure loss — it produces frustration without producing any actual progress toward the thing wanted. Worse, visible frustration or resentment is often exactly the reaction an adversary or a difficult situation is designed to provoke, and supplying that reaction hands them a form of control over your emotional state that they otherwise wouldn't have. Indifference denies that payoff entirely.

There's also a status dimension: chasing or resenting something out of reach reads, to observers, as being controlled by that thing. Genuine indifference — not performed, but real — reads as being above it, which is a stronger position both practically and socially.

## Where the line is between healthy detachment and denial

This law is easy to misuse as a justification for giving up on things that are actually still attainable with more effort, dressed up as noble indifference. The useful version applies specifically to things genuinely outside your control or reach — a decision already made by someone else, an outcome that's already settled, a person who has clearly moved on. Applying disdain reflexively to anything difficult, rather than specifically to things that are truly unattainable, just becomes a way of avoiding effort.

## The practical benefit beyond emotional peace

Beyond the internal benefit of not wasting energy on the unattainable, there's an external one: people and situations that can't provoke a reaction from you lose a specific kind of leverage over you, and that composure under a situation designed to needle you is frequently more disarming to an adversary than any counter-move would be.

## Practical reading

Before investing more emotional energy into wanting or resenting something, it's worth honestly checking whether it's actually still attainable with more effort, or whether it's genuinely settled — and if it's the latter, redirecting that energy elsewhere isn't defeat, it's just accurate accounting of where effort still pays off.
