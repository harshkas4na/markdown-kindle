# Law 18: Do Not Build Fortresses to Protect Yourself — Isolation Is Dangerous

**The idea in one line:** cutting yourself off in the name of safety trades away the very thing that keeps you safe — current information, allies, and early warning about threats before they become unmanageable.

## The psychology

Isolation feels protective because it limits exposure to conflict, criticism, and risk. But it comes at a steep and often invisible cost: without regular contact with the outside world, you lose the steady stream of small signals that let you notice problems while they're still small. Threats don't stop developing just because you've stopped watching for them — they just develop unobserved, and by the time they force their way into your isolated position, they've usually grown much larger and much harder to counter.

There's also a social cost. People who withdraw tend to be forgotten or, worse, actively plotted against precisely because they're no longer present to defend their interests or notice what's being said about them. Isolation removes you from the flow of both information and influence at the same time.

## Where this shows up

The pattern recurs anywhere someone with resources or position insulates themselves from ordinary contact — a leader who stops hearing dissenting views because they've surrounded themselves only with agreement, a person who withdraws from a social or professional circle after a setback and loses the relationships that would have helped them recover, an organization that stops engaging with feedback from the outside. In each case the isolation is chosen for safety and ends up creating a much larger blind spot.

## Practical reading

Security comes less from insulation and more from staying embedded — maintaining real relationships, staying reachable, keeping channels open even with people you don't fully trust, because those channels are also your early-warning system. The instinct to retreat after a setback or under threat is understandable, but it's usually the opposite of what actually protects you. Engagement, even uncomfortable engagement, keeps you informed; withdrawal just keeps you unaware.
