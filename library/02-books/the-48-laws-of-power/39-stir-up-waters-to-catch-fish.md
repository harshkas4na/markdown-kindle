# Law 39: Stir Up Waters to Catch Fish

**The idea in one line:** a calm, composed opponent is a well-defended one — provoking them into agitation or anger tends to degrade their judgment, and a worse decision-maker is easier to out-negotiate or outmaneuver.

## The psychology

Strong emotion, particularly anger or anxiety, measurably narrows cognitive focus and degrades the quality of decision-making — people under emotional agitation think less clearly, react rather than plan, and are far more likely to make a mistake they wouldn't make in a calmer state. Someone who is provoked into that state has effectively been knocked off their strongest footing without a single direct concession being extracted from them. Composure is a real competitive advantage, and disrupting someone else's composure is a way of attacking that advantage indirectly.

## How this is actually done

Deliberate provocation doesn't have to be obvious hostility — it can be a needling comment, a manufactured minor slight, an unexpected change that forces a rushed reaction, or simply withholding something the other party expects, forcing them to react rather than plan. The goal isn't necessarily visible anger; it's any disruption that pulls someone out of calm, deliberate decision-making and into a more reactive state.

## The defensive reading, which is arguably more useful

Because this tactic is recognizable once named, the more broadly applicable skill is defensive: noticing when you've been deliberately provoked and treating the resulting emotional spike as a signal to slow down rather than as a legitimate basis for a decision. The people least vulnerable to this law aren't the ones who never get provoked — they're the ones who've built the habit of recognizing provocation in the moment and refusing to act from it.

## Practical reading

Offensively, this is one of the more ethically fraught laws in the list, since deliberately destabilizing someone's emotional state to gain advantage is manipulation in a fairly direct sense. Defensively, it's genuinely valuable: build the habit of noticing when a situation seems engineered to provoke a reaction, and treat that noticing itself as the cue to slow down before responding.
