# Law 6: Court Attention at All Cost

**The idea in one line:** being unnoticed is invisible to opportunity too — attention is the raw material that everything else (credibility, advancement, influence) gets built from, so being competent in obscurity accomplishes very little.

## The psychology

Rooms full of competent people are common. What separates the ones who advance from the ones who don't is often not skill but *legibility* — whether their skill is visible to the people who make decisions. Quiet, reliable work gets absorbed into the background; it becomes the assumed baseline rather than a distinguishing signal. Attention, on the other hand, is what makes a person a candidate for opportunities in the first place, because you can't be considered for something you haven't been noticed for.

There's also a compounding effect: attention early tends to generate more attention later, because people who are already visible get asked to do more visible things, while people who stay in the background keep getting handed more background work. The gap widens on its own.

## The risk side of this law

Attention cuts both ways — it makes triumphs bigger, but it makes failures bigger too, and it invites scrutiny that quieter operators never have to survive. Courting attention indiscriminately, or through manufactured controversy rather than genuine distinctive work, tends to produce a reputation for noise rather than substance, which burns out fast once people catch on that there's less behind it than the volume suggested.

## Practical reading

The sustainable version of this law isn't "be loud" — it's "be legible." Make your actual contributions visible through the natural channels available (a well-communicated result, a distinctive point of view stated clearly, work that's shared rather than filed away), rather than assuming good work speaks for itself. It rarely does, on its own, at the volume and speed needed to matter. Pick the moments and channels where getting noticed serves a real goal, rather than pursuing attention as an end in itself — attention without a clear thing to be attentive *to* just becomes a liability once people look closer.
