# Law 15: Crush Your Enemy Totally

**The idea in one line:** a defeated but not eliminated rival remains a live threat — partial victories tend to leave enough resentment and enough residual capability for the conflict to resurface later, often worse.

## The psychology

There's a natural pull toward moderation once a conflict is clearly won — mercy feels virtuous, and pressing further feels excessive once the outcome is decided. But an opponent who has been humbled rather than fully neutralized doesn't typically respond with gratitude for the restraint; they respond with the motivation of someone who has something to prove and a specific grievance to settle, now armed with the lesson of exactly what went wrong the first time. The half-measure produces the worst combination: an active grudge plus improved information about how to win next time.

## Why "total" doesn't have to mean cruelty

This law is easy to read as an endorsement of ruthlessness for its own sake, but the sharper reading is about *decisiveness and completeness*, not gratuitous harm. In a competitive or business context, this looks like fully resolving a conflict rather than leaving it in an ambiguous state where both sides believe they might still win — a clean, complete negotiated outcome, a decisively won market position, a fully settled dispute. The danger isn't stopping short of maximal aggression; it's stopping in a way that leaves the underlying conflict technically unresolved.

## Where this law is most dangerous to follow literally

Applied without judgment, "total" victory in interpersonal or organizational conflict tends to create exactly the kind of bitter, motivated opposition the law is meant to prevent — humiliating someone completely, beyond what the situation required, is a highly effective way to manufacture a permanent enemy who had no path back to reconciliation and therefore nothing left to lose. Greene's own framing acknowledges this is really advice for genuine, high-stakes conflict, not everyday disagreements, where over-application does more harm than the incomplete-victory problem it's meant to solve.

## Practical reading

The steelman version: when a conflict is real and consequential, resolve it decisively enough that it doesn't quietly reopen later — don't leave a rival with both motive and means. But distinguish that from ordinary disagreements, where completeness usually means a fair, clear resolution rather than anything resembling domination.
