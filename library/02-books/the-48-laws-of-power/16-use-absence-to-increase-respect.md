# Law 16: Use Absence to Increase Respect and Honor

**The idea in one line:** familiarity dilutes perceived value — the more constantly available and visible someone is, the more ordinary they start to seem, regardless of their actual quality.

## The psychology

Scarcity shapes perceived value across almost every domain, and attention is no exception. When someone is always present, always reachable, always the same, the mind stops registering them as noteworthy — they become part of the background. Absence interrupts that habituation. It creates a gap that draws attention back to the value that was there all along, this time without the numbing effect of constant exposure. This is why a person's departure, even temporary, often provokes a more accurate appreciation of their contribution than their continued presence ever did.

## Where this plays out

The clean example is anyone whose constant availability slowly become taken for granted — a colleague always on call, a friend always the one reaching out first, a public figure with no gap between appearances. Deliberate, occasional withdrawal — not disappearing from responsibility, but reducing over-exposure — resets the baseline and lets absence do the work that presence, no matter how good, eventually stops doing.

## The risk

Absence only increases respect if what's absent was genuinely valued to begin with — stepping back when you haven't yet established real value just reads as unreliability or disengagement, not mystique. It's also easy to overplay: strategic scarcity that curdles into actual unavailability breaks the relationships and expectations that made the presence valuable in the first place. There's a difference between a well-timed pause and simply becoming unreliable, and the line is easy to cross without noticing.

## Practical reading

This is less a call to constant, calculated withdrawal and more a caution against the trap of assuming that maximal availability is maximal value. Occasionally stepping back — from a project, a conversation, a relationship — lets both sides recalibrate what your presence is actually worth, rather than letting it flatten into an assumed constant that nobody actively appreciates anymore.
