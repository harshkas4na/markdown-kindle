# Law 3: Conceal Your Intentions

**The idea in one line:** the moment people can see where you're headed, they can organize to help themselves at your expense — either by blocking you or by getting there first.

## The psychology

Announcing a plan does two things at once: it invites resistance from anyone whose interests it threatens, and it gives everyone else time to adjust their own position in response. Neither helps you. People aren't generally trying to sabotage you out of malice — they're just protecting themselves once they know what's coming, and protective moves by others are often indistinguishable from obstruction.

Concealment isn't the same as lying, though it can include misdirection. Often the simplest version is just silence: not narrating your reasoning, not previewing your next move, letting actions explain themselves after the fact rather than before.

## The two directions this takes

One version is pure omission — you simply don't say what you're planning until it's already underway or done, at which point objections are moot. The other, more active version is misdirection: giving people a plausible but incomplete story that satisfies their curiosity and keeps them from probing further, while the real reasoning stays private. Both rely on the same underlying fact — most people take the explanation they're given at face value rather than interrogating it, especially if it's delivered with confidence.

## Where this goes wrong

Concealment used reflexively, on everything, with everyone, stops being a tactical choice and starts reading as evasiveness — and evasiveness itself becomes a signal that invites the exact suspicion you were trying to avoid. It also erodes the kind of trust that makes collaboration possible; people who feel consistently kept in the dark eventually stop extending goodwill, even where none was needed.

## Practical reading

The useful discipline here isn't "never explain yourself" — it's noticing which specific pieces of information, in which specific situations, would let someone else act against your interests if they had them early. Withhold those pieces. Share everything else freely. Most people conflate transparency with sharing every thought as it happens; the more defensible version of transparency is being honest about what you've already decided, while staying quiet about what you're still deciding and why.
