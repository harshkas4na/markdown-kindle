# Law 41: Avoid Stepping into a Great Man's Shoes

**The idea in one line:** succeeding someone widely admired sets an unfair comparison that has nothing to do with your own actual performance — the successor is measured against a fixed, often idealized memory rather than against a fair, independent standard.

## The psychology

Once someone leaves a well-regarded position, memory of them tends to smooth over their actual flaws and inflate their strengths — nostalgia is a consistently generous editor. Whoever follows inherits comparison not against the real, flawed predecessor but against this idealized version, a standard that's both unfair and functionally very hard to beat, because it isn't a fixed target so much as a moving one shaped by whoever's doing the remembering. Early struggles that would be treated as normal for anyone starting fresh get read instead as decline or inadequacy specifically because of the comparison being made.

## Why this is a real trap, not just a mood

Successors often make things worse for themselves by explicitly trying to continue the predecessor's exact approach, which locks them into being evaluated purely on a copy of someone else's style rather than establishing an independent standard for what good performance in the role actually looks like under their own leadership. Continuity can look safe, but it forfeits the one thing that would let a successor actually escape the comparison: a distinct identity for the role that isn't just "how well did they imitate the last person."

## The way out

The more resilient path is establishing a clearly different approach and a clearly different standard early — not necessarily better in every dimension, but different enough that direct comparison becomes harder to make cleanly. This resets the frame from "are they as good as the last one" to "what are they actually doing," which is a fairer and more winnable question.

## Practical reading

If you're stepping into a role someone well-regarded just left, resist the urge to continue exactly as they did, even where their approach seemed to work — deliberately establish your own distinct standard early, so you're judged on your own terms rather than on fidelity to a memory that's already been quietly polished into something no one could realistically match.
