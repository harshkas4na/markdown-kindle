# Law 7: Get Others to Do the Work for You, but Always Take the Credit

**The idea in one line:** the scarce resource is judgment and direction, not hours — so the highest-leverage move is often orchestrating other people's effort rather than supplying all of it yourself, while making sure the outcome is still legibly attributed to you.

## The psychology

There's a natural instinct to prove worth through personal effort — to be seen grinding. But effort isn't actually what gets rewarded at the level that matters; outcomes are, and outcomes scale much further through delegation and coordination than through individual output. The people who accomplish the most visible things are very often the ones who assembled the right talent and pointed it in the right direction, not the ones who did every task personally.

The "take the credit" half is the part people find distasteful, but it follows the same logic as reputation management elsewhere in this list: credit is a signal that determines future opportunity, and if it's diffused equally across everyone who contributed, the person who organized the effort — arguably the highest-leverage contribution — often gets underweighted relative to their actual impact.

## The tension this creates

This law is in obvious friction with basic fairness, and pursued without any counterbalance it burns collaborators — people who feel used once tend not to offer their best effort a second time. The versions of this that work sustainably usually involve *some* real credit-sharing, enough to keep collaborators willing to keep contributing, while the person doing the orchestrating still captures a disproportionate — not exclusive — share of the recognition for the outcome.

## Practical reading

The legitimate core of this law is less about credit theft and more about recognizing that direction, sequencing, and quality control are themselves a form of work, even when they don't look like the visible labor. Leading a project, setting its standards, and being the person who catches what would otherwise go wrong is real value — the mistake is in either doing everything yourself out of a sense that only your own hands count, or in taking credit for value you didn't actually add. The sustainable middle: contribute the judgment that only you could supply, and let your name be attached to the outcome you meaningfully shaped, without erasing the specific people whose effort made it real.
