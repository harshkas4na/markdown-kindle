# Law 20: Do Not Commit to Anyone

**The idea in one line:** binding yourself firmly to one side of any conflict or camp caps your future options at whatever that side's fortunes turn out to be — staying independent keeps every side interested in earning your support rather than assuming it.

## The psychology

Once you're perceived as firmly aligned with a person, faction, or position, two things happen: your value to the people you've aligned with drops, because they no longer need to work to keep you, and your value to everyone else drops too, because you're no longer a plausible ally for them. Uncommitted parties get courted by all sides precisely because their support is still up for grabs. Committed ones get taken for granted by their own side and written off by everyone else.

There's also a downside-protection angle: full commitment to one side means fully inheriting that side's losses if things go badly, with no independent position to fall back on.

## Where this gets uncomfortable

This is one of the laws most in tension with ordinary human values — loyalty, partnership, and long-term commitment are generally treated as virtues, and for good reason: they're what make deep trust and real collaboration possible. Read literally and applied broadly, "do not commit to anyone" describes a permanently transactional, uncommitted way of moving through relationships that most people would find both lonely and, frankly, untrustworthy from the outside.

## The narrower, more defensible reading

Where this law is more genuinely useful is in specifically factional or political situations — internal organizational rivalries, competing camps with no clear winner yet, situations where picking a side early mostly just narrows your options without buying you real security. In those specific, adversarial contexts, preserving independence until the situation clarifies is sound risk management, not disloyalty.

## Practical reading

The distinction worth holding onto: genuine relationships and partnerships built on trust are different from factional alignment in an unresolved power struggle. Applying this law to the former damages exactly the kind of durable trust the other laws depend on; reserving it for the latter — genuinely undecided, adversarial situations — is where it actually earns its keep.
