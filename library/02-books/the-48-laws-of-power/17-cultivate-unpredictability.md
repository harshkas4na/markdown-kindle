# Law 17: Keep Others in Suspended Terror — Cultivate an Air of Unpredictability

**The idea in one line:** predictable people are easy to plan around and easy to control, because everyone knows exactly how they'll react — unpredictability removes that ability and puts the other side on the back foot, working reactively instead of proactively.

## The psychology

Once someone can reliably anticipate your response to a given situation, they've effectively gained power over the interaction — they can engineer situations that produce the response they want, or route around the responses they don't. A degree of genuine unpredictability closes that gap. If people can't fully model your next move, they have to engage more cautiously, more attentively, and with more respect for the possibility that this time might be different.

## What this looks like in practice

This isn't about chaos for its own sake — it's about not being fully legible. It can be as simple as not reacting the same way twice to a similar provocation, occasionally doing the less expected thing when the expected thing has become too easy to plan around, or simply not telegraphing decisions before they're made. The effect isn't fear in the literal sense the law's title suggests so much as heightened attentiveness — people paying closer attention because they can't autopilot their way through dealing with you.

## The cost side

Unpredictability is corrosive to trust and to functional collaboration if it's applied broadly rather than selectively. People who genuinely can't predict how you'll respond to ordinary situations — not just competitive ones — find it exhausting and eventually stop investing in the relationship altogether, because reliability is what makes cooperation efficient in the first place. This law is really only advisable in adversarial or competitive contexts, not in relationships that depend on trust and consistency to function.

## Practical reading

The narrow, useful version: in specifically competitive situations, avoid becoming so consistent that your reactions are fully mapped out by the other side in advance. The broad, harmful version: applying it to people who depend on your reliability, where the result is just erosion of trust with nothing gained in return. Knowing which relationships are which is most of the skill here.
