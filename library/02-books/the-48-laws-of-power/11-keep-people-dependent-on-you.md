# Law 11: Learn to Keep People Dependent on You

**The idea in one line:** being needed is a more durable source of security than being liked — goodwill can evaporate, but a genuine gap only you fill keeps people invested in your continued presence.

## The psychology

Affection is a fragile foundation for a relationship of unequal power, because affection is subjective and can shift for reasons that have nothing to do with your actual value. Necessity is sturdier: if someone genuinely cannot get a specific result without you, their incentive to keep you close doesn't depend on their mood. This is essentially the logic of specialization applied to relationships and organizations — the more uniquely useful your specific contribution is, the less replaceable you are, independent of how much anyone personally warms to you.

## How dependency actually gets built

The honest version of this law is just becoming genuinely, distinctively good at something that's hard to substitute — depth of skill, a specific relationship or piece of institutional knowledge nobody else has bothered to acquire, being the person who reliably solves the problem others can't. The less honest version involves deliberately withholding knowledge or capability that could easily be shared, purely to manufacture indispensability. The first builds real security; the second tends to be discovered and resented once people notice the withholding.

## The risk in over-applying it

Total dependency runs in both directions — an organization or person that has made itself irreplaceable can also become a bottleneck others start actively trying to route around, precisely because the leverage becomes uncomfortable for the other party once they notice it. Overplaying this law invites the other side to invest, quietly, in reducing their dependency on you, which is a slower but real form of losing the relationship.

## Practical reading

The sustainable version isn't hoarding critical information out of fear — it's investing in skills, relationships, and institutional knowledge that are genuinely hard to replicate quickly, and making sure that value is visible rather than assumed. Being needed and being generous with what you know aren't actually in conflict most of the time; real expertise is rarely fully transferable just by explaining it once, which leaves plenty of room to be both useful and open.
