# Law 23: Concentrate Your Forces

**The idea in one line:** effort and resources split across many fronts produce mediocre results everywhere — the same total effort applied to one point produces a breakthrough.

## The psychology

Spreading attention across multiple goals feels like it hedges risk, but it usually just guarantees that nothing gets the depth of focus needed to actually succeed. Meaningful results in most competitive domains require a level of concentrated effort well above the average — and average effort split evenly across several fronts stays average everywhere. The mind also has a strong pull toward variety and toward hedging bets, both of which work against sustained concentration on one thing long enough to break through.

## Where this shows up

This applies at every scale — a person spread across too many simultaneous projects, a company with resources thinly distributed across too many product lines, a negotiation strategy that tries to win on every point at once instead of identifying the one point that actually matters. In each case, the fix is the same: identify the smallest set of efforts that would actually move the outcome, and route disproportionate resources there, deliberately starving the rest.

## The counterargument this law is usually pitted against

Diversification has real, well-established value as a hedge against uncertainty — you don't know in advance which bet will pay off, so spreading exposure limits the damage from any single failure. This law isn't actually opposed to that in contexts of genuine uncertainty; it's aimed more narrowly at situations where the winning move is already reasonably clear, and the failure mode is diluting a known good bet with unnecessary hedges out of habit or discomfort with commitment.

## Practical reading

The useful question isn't "should I diversify or concentrate" as a universal rule — it's "do I actually know enough here to justify concentrating." When the answer is yes, half-measures and hedged effort are usually the more expensive mistake. When genuine uncertainty is still high, some diversification is simply rational risk management, not a violation of this law.
