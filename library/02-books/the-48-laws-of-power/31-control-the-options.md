# Law 31: Control the Options — Get Others to Play with the Cards You Deal

**The idea in one line:** the person who defines the set of available choices controls the outcome more than the person who ultimately picks between them — a choice among options you designed isn't really a free choice at all.

## The psychology

People generally feel autonomous and satisfied when they're offered a decision to make, even when the actual range of options was constructed by someone else with a preferred outcome in mind. Choosing among a curated set of options *feels* like agency, which makes the resulting decision feel more legitimate and more owned by the chooser — even though the real leverage point was upstream, in deciding what made it onto the list in the first place. Framing effects and the way options are presented shape decisions far more than most people realize while they're making them.

## How this actually gets constructed

This shows up as narrowing a decision to two or three options, all of which lead toward an acceptable outcome for the person who designed them — including sometimes a deliberately weaker option included mainly to make the preferred option look better by comparison. It also shows up in negotiations as controlling which questions get asked, which alternatives get raised, and which are quietly left off the table.

## The ethical line

There's a meaningful difference between framing a genuine set of good options thoughtfully — which is just good facilitation or good management — and deliberately constructing a false choice designed to manipulate someone toward an outcome that isn't actually in their interest. The first is a normal, defensible skill. The second, discovered, tends to permanently damage trust, because it retroactively makes every past "choice" someone made feel manufactured rather than free.

## Practical reading

On the offensive side: when presenting a decision to someone else, it's worth being deliberate about which options are actually on the table, since that framing does real work regardless of intent. On the defensive side: when handed a curated set of options, it's worth periodically asking what wasn't included, and why — the missing alternatives are often more informative than the ones presented.
