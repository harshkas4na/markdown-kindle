# Law 47: Do Not Go Past the Mark You Aimed For; In Victory, Learn When to Stop

**The idea in one line:** success creates momentum that tempts you to keep pushing past the original goal, and that extra push is disproportionately where things go wrong — the win itself was rarely the problem, the overreach afterward usually was.

## The psychology

Winning produces a rush of confidence that tends to distort risk assessment — the same judgment that correctly identified an achievable goal starts to feel infallible after that goal is reached, and infallible-feeling judgment stops checking itself as carefully. This is a well-documented pattern: success breeds the kind of overconfidence that makes the next, more ambitious move feel obviously safe, right up until it isn't. The original goal was calibrated against real constraints; the expanded goal that follows a win is often calibrated against nothing but the emotional high of having just succeeded.

## Where this actually causes damage

The recurring shape of this failure: a genuinely good outcome — a negotiation won, a competitive advantage secured, a conflict resolved in your favor — followed by continued pushing past the point where the original goal was satisfied, driven by momentum rather than by any fresh, sober assessment of whether further gains are actually worth the increased risk. The extension frequently costs more than the original win was worth, either by provoking retaliation, by overextending resources, or by simply running past the point of diminishing returns into actual losses.

## Why stopping at the mark is harder than it sounds

Recognizing "the mark" requires having defined success clearly before the momentum of winning arrives to blur the definition — vague goals are especially vulnerable to this, because there's no clear finish line to notice you've crossed. This is really an argument for setting explicit success criteria in advance, precisely because in-the-moment judgment, flush with a recent win, is a poor tool for deciding when enough is enough.

## Practical reading

Before pursuing something, it's worth defining concretely what success actually looks like — a specific outcome, not a vague sense of momentum — so that when it's reached, there's a clear signal to reassess deliberately rather than simply continuing on the strength of recent success alone.
