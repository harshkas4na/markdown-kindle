## Part I: Child Development and Pedagogy

**Q1.** Which question promotes critical and creative thinking?
**Correct answer: (1) — Which can be the best way to save water and why?**
Questions that promote critical and creative thinking are open-ended: they ask for reasoning, alternatives, and justification ("how" and "why") rather than a single retrievable fact. Options 2–4 are closed, factual-recall questions with one correct, locatable answer. A good pedagogical habit is to notice whether a question demands thinking or just remembering.

**Q2.** Role of evidence in critical thinking.
**Correct answer: (1) — Evidence should be evaluated in a logical and systematic way.**
Critical thinking is fundamentally about weighing evidence rationally before drawing conclusions, not accepting or rejecting it blindly. Options that dismiss evidence (2, 3, 4) describe the opposite of critical thinking — uncritical acceptance, irrelevance, or bias — and are classic distractors testing whether you can spot the definition being inverted.

**Q3.** What does inclusive education entail?
**Correct answer: (4) — Flexibility in the curriculum as a result of recognition of individual differences.**
Inclusion means adapting the system to the diversity of learners, not sorting learners to fit a fixed system. Options 1–3 describe exclusionary or segregating practices (standardisation, categorisation, restricting some learners to only vocational tracks), which are precisely what inclusive education seeks to move away from.

**Q4.** Assertion-Reason on Piaget/Vygotsky and discovery learning.
**Correct answer: (2) — Both (A) and (R) are true, but (R) is not the correct explanation of (A).**
Both statements are factually correct on their own: Piaget and Vygotsky genuinely disagreed about language's role in cognition (Piaget saw thought preceding language; Vygotsky saw them as intertwined), and discovery learning genuinely does work by letting students construct knowledge. But R is a general description of discovery learning — it does not explain why these two theorists specifically differed on language's role, so it fails the "correct explanation" test that assertion-reason items always check.

**Q5.** Characteristics of gifted students.
**Correct answer: (4) — A and B.**
Gifted learners are typically marked by an advanced depth of conceptual understanding and a faster pace of learning — both intrinsic learning traits. "Higher dependence on others" and "rapid physical development" are not defining features of giftedness; giftedness is a cognitive/academic trait, not a physical one, so C and D are distractors based on stereotype rather than research.

**Q6.** Hearing impairment — correct statement.
**Correct answer: (1) — Hearing impairment can affect a child's ability to communicate effectively.**
This is the only statement that is true in all cases without overgeneralising. Option 2 wrongly assumes all hearing-impaired children use sign language (many use hearing aids, lip-reading, or spoken language). Option 3 wrongly claims loud noises give a reliable diagnosis (only calibrated audiometric testing does). Option 4 wrongly claims impairment is always physical (it can also be neural/sensorineural or due to auditory processing issues).

**Q7.** Accommodation vs. modification in curriculum.
**Correct answer: (4) — accommodation; modification.**
In inclusive pedagogy, "accommodation" changes *how* content is taught or accessed while keeping learning expectations the same for everyone; "modification" changes *what* is expected, so outcomes genuinely differ across learners. This distinction is frequently tested — remember accommodation = same goal, different method; modification = different goal.

**Q8.** Constructivism — which statement is NOT correct.
**Correct answer: (3) — Learners need to be extrinsically motivated to ensure effective learning.**
Constructivism holds that learners actively build knowledge from their own experience and are best driven by intrinsic curiosity and meaning-making, not external rewards. The other three options (knowledge as subjective, contextual, and pluralistic) are core constructivist tenets, making option 3 the odd one out.

**Q9.** Example of intrinsic motivation.
**Correct answer: (2) — Playing a musical instrument for personal enjoyment.**
Intrinsic motivation means engaging in an activity for the inherent satisfaction it gives, not for an external outcome. The other three options are driven by external factors — impressing someone, avoiding punishment, or winning money — which are all forms of extrinsic motivation.

**Q10.** Assertion-Reason on breaking stereotypes.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**
Teachers do need to actively counter ethnic stereotypes (A), and this connects directly to the broader goal of fostering critical self-reflection in students (R) — helping them examine and question their own assumptions is exactly the mechanism through which stereotypes get broken down, so R properly explains A.

**Q11.** Mastery goals vs. performance goals.
**Correct answer: (2).**
Mastery goals are about self-referenced improvement — getting better at a task and deepening one's own understanding. Performance goals are about other-referenced outcomes — grades, rewards, or how one is judged relative to others. Any option that swaps these definitions, or ties mastery goals to competition/approval, is testing whether you've confused the two categories.

**Q12.** Sibling relationships.
**Correct answer: (1) — are different than relationships with parents and peers and play an important role in socialization throughout childhood.**
Developmental psychology treats sibling relationships as a distinct category of relationship (neither purely vertical like parent-child, nor purely horizontal like peer relationships) that meaningfully shapes socialisation across the whole span of childhood, not just briefly in early years.

**Q13.** Assertion-Reason on developmental rate variation.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**
Children genuinely do develop at different rates (A), and this variability is properly explained by R: development results from a complex interplay between genetic makeup and individual experiences, so R is a valid causal explanation of A, not just a coincidentally true statement.

**Q14.** Principle of development illustrated by delayed language exposure.
**Correct answer: (4) — There is a sensitive period of language development.**
The statement describes a window early in life during which language is acquired most easily; missing that window causes lasting difficulty — the textbook definition of a sensitive (or critical) period. This is different from saying development is "genetic," "unpredictable," or purely about "language-cognition links" — the specific idea being tested is the time-bound nature of language acquisition.

**Q15.** Teaching a dyslexic student to read.
**Correct answer: (4) — through systemic phonological training.**
Dyslexia is fundamentally a difficulty processing the sound structure of language (phonological processing), so structured, systematic phonics-based instruction is the evidence-based remedy. Punishment, distraction, or above-level texts (options 1–3) would only compound the difficulty rather than address its root cause.

**Q16.** Underlying belief of understanding-based teaching.
**Correct answer: (3) — learners can construct knowledge when provided with a facilitative environment to do so.**
"Understanding-based teaching" is grounded in constructivism: the teacher's job is to create the right conditions (materials, questions, scaffolding) for learners to build their own understanding, rather than transmitting ready-made facts (option 4) or assuming learners are passive vessels (option 2).

**Q17.** Helping Aman develop problem-solving skills.
**Correct answer: (1) — Encourage him to brainstorm and generate ideas.**
Developing problem-solving skill requires practice in generating and evaluating one's own possible solutions. Giving the answer directly (4), discouraging risk-taking (3), or telling him to give up (2) all remove the very process — independent ideation — through which the skill is built.

**Q18.** What a conducive middle-school classroom centres around.
**Correct answer: (2) — co-operative learning.**
A classroom environment conducive to learning at the middle-school stage is built on collaboration and peer support, not fear, competition, or rote conditioning. Cooperative learning fosters psychological safety, which research consistently links to better engagement and learning outcomes at this age.

**Q19.** Vygotsky's view of development.
**Correct answer: (4) — results directly from social interaction.**
Vygotsky's sociocultural theory holds that higher cognitive functions originate in social interaction (with more knowledgeable others) before being internalised by the individual — captured in his idea of the Zone of Proximal Development. This contrasts with Piaget, who emphasised the child's independent construction of knowledge through maturation and self-directed exploration.

**Q20.** Piaget's four stages.
**Correct answer: (4) — represent universal patterns of development of children.**
Piaget proposed that all children everywhere pass through the same sequence of qualitatively distinct stages (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational), regardless of culture — hence "universal." This is also exactly why his theory is criticised (see Q21) for underplaying cultural variation.

**Q21.** Limitation of Piaget's theory.
**Correct answer: (4) — It does not account for the influence of culture and social factors on cognitive development.**
This is the standard critique levelled against Piaget (largely from Vygotsky's sociocultural perspective): Piaget's stages are presented as universal and biologically driven, largely ignoring how culture, language, and social interaction shape the pace and nature of cognitive development.

**Q22.** Assertion-Reason on gender roles being taught vs. innate.
**Correct answer: (1) — (A) is true, but (R) is false.**
Lata's classroom practice (inviting a female maths professor and male nurse) is a real, true example of countering gender stereotypes (A). But R claims gender differences are innate and not learned from society — this is false; developmental psychology is clear that gender roles and stereotypes are substantially learned through socialisation, which is exactly why Lata's intervention matters.

**Q23.** Progressive classroom — what students do.
**Correct answer: (3) — B, C and D.**
A progressive, learner-centred classroom encourages active questioning in multiple directions: to the teacher, to peers, and reflectively to oneself. Passive, question-avoidant behaviour (option A) is the opposite of what a progressive classroom fosters, so it's excluded.

**Q24.** Gender roles — filling the blanks.
**Correct answer: (1) — gender roles; throughout childhood and into adulthood.**
"Gender roles" is the correct term for socially expected behaviours associated with being male or female, and these are learned continuously — not confined to one narrow developmental window — from early childhood right into adulthood through ongoing socialisation.

**Q25.** View of intelligence supporting respect for diversity.
**Correct answer: (4) — Intelligence is multidimensional and can change over time.**
Viewing intelligence as multidimensional (as in Gardner's theory) and malleable respects that learners can be strong in different ways and can grow — supporting differentiated, respectful pedagogy. A fixed, single-number, purely genetic view of intelligence (options 1–3) tends to justify labelling and ranking learners rather than valuing their diversity.

**Q26.** Who critiqued 'general' intelligence.
**Correct answer: (2) — Howard Gardner.**
Gardner proposed the theory of Multiple Intelligences, directly challenging Spearman's idea of a single general intelligence factor ('g'). Piaget and Vygotsky are developmental theorists, not intelligence theorists, so they are distractors based on topic proximity rather than content.

**Q27.** Gilligan's critique of Kohlberg.
**Correct answer: (1) — ignoring gender differences in moral reasoning.**
Carol Gilligan argued that Kohlberg's stages of moral development, based on research with male subjects, favoured a justice-based reasoning style typical of boys and undervalued a care-and-relationship-based reasoning style she found more common in girls — hence her critique centred on gender bias, not on methodology or the existence of stages themselves.

**Q28.** Strategy to develop metacognitive skills.
**Correct answer: (2) — Encouraging students to reflect on their own learning and evaluate their progress.**
Metacognition means "thinking about one's own thinking" — monitoring, evaluating, and regulating one's learning. Self-reflection and self-evaluation directly practise this skill, whereas grades, rote memorisation, or over-prescriptive instructions (options 1, 3, 4) do the thinking *for* the student instead of building their own monitoring ability.

**Q29.** Assertion-Reason on examples/non-examples.
**Correct answer: (1) — (A) is true, but (R) is false.**
Asking students to generate their own examples and non-examples is a well-established way to strengthen concept understanding (A is true). But R is false in claiming non-examples "confuse" students — in fact, non-examples help sharpen a concept's boundaries by showing what it is *not*, which clarifies rather than confuses.

**Q30.** General principles of learning — sequencing instruction.
**Correct answer: (3) — first introduces the concept overall and then moves on to the intricacies of it.**
This reflects the general-to-specific (or "whole-to-part") principle of instruction: give learners the big picture first so they have a framework, then fill in details and nuances. This helps learners situate new details meaningfully rather than being overwhelmed by fragments first.

## Part II: Mathematics and Science

**Q31.** Concepts introducible through crossing lines/roads and letters like H, Z.
**Correct answer: (1) — B and C (Transversal and Parallel lines).**
A railway line crossed by several other lines is a real-life model of a transversal cutting a set of lines; roads crossing each other model parallel and intersecting lines; the letters given (H, Z) are commonly used to visually introduce the idea of parallel lines cut by a transversal. Line segments and right angles specifically (A, D) aren't the natural takeaway of these particular examples — the teaching point here is angle relationships formed by a transversal, which is what makes B and C correct.

**Q32.** Most appropriate way to teach mathematics.
**Correct answer: (4) — Motivate the learners to develop their existing capabilities.**
Good mathematics pedagogy builds on what a learner already knows and can do, motivating growth from there — rather than a uniform, one-size-fits-all approach (3), or narrowly targeting only "gaps" or "low performers" in ways that stigmatise (1, 2).

**Q33.** True statement about CCE.
**Correct answer: (1) — In CCE, assessment process includes assessment of previous knowledge, understanding level and learning process.**
Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation is meant to track the whole learning process over time — prior knowledge, conceptual understanding, and the process of learning — not just end marks. It is not about daily homework as a proxy for assessment, converting marks mechanically into grades, or increasing testing at the cost of teaching (options 2–4 misrepresent CCE's spirit).

**Q34.** Essential features of a concept map in mathematics.
**Correct answer: (4) — A and B.**
A concept map represents the conceptual structure of an idea using linked sub-concepts (A), and is directional — arrows show prerequisite relationships between concepts (B). Concept maps are, in fact, useful diagnostically to spot learning gaps (contradicting C) and can be used repeatedly as a regular teaching-learning tool (contradicting D).

**Q35.** NOT true about the nature of mathematics.
**Correct answer: (1) — Mathematics at school level requires special aptitude in learners.**
This is the common myth that mathematics education researchers actively reject — that only some learners are "naturally" capable of mathematics. School mathematics is learnable by all children with appropriate teaching. The other statements (abstract primary concepts, deductive reasoning, being more abstract/hierarchic than other subjects) are genuinely accurate descriptions of mathematics's nature.

**Q36.** Appropriate statements about "Argumentation" in mathematics classrooms.
**Correct answer: (1) — A and C.**
Argumentation in mathematics is reasoning that shows or explains why a mathematical result is true (A) — this is the accepted definition. It is also genuinely part of the heuristic, discovery-based approach in mathematics education (C), since guiding students to construct and justify their own reasoning is itself a heuristic teaching strategy. Statement B is false: mathematical argumentation is not "always helpful in the same manner as in other empirical disciplines" — mathematics rests on logical/deductive proof, unlike empirical sciences where claims rely on observation and evidence, so the two are not interchangeable. Statement D is also false: argumentation is valuable for elementary-grade learners too — being harder to assess doesn't make it pedagogically less useful.

**Q37.** NOT correct about a mathematics textbook.
**Correct answer: (2) — A mathematics textbook should have solved examples only.**
A good mathematics textbook must include varied, unsolved practice exercises so students actively work through problems themselves — a textbook of only solved examples turns students into passive readers rather than active problem-solvers. The other statements (daily-life relevance, manageable size, varied exercises) describe genuinely desirable textbook features.

**Q38.** What is a conjecture in mathematics?
**Correct answer: (4) — a proposition or conclusion that is assumed to be true on a tentative basis without proof.**
A conjecture is an educated guess based on patterns or observations, awaiting proof (or disproof) — it becomes a theorem only once proved. This is distinct from an axiom (accepted without proof as a starting assumption) or a theorem (already proved), and conjectures are pedagogically valuable at all levels because they model how mathematical thinking and discovery actually work.

**Q39.** "1 less/more than a multiple of 6 is prime" — what kind of statement is this?
**Correct answer: (3) — Conjecture.**
This claim looks true for several small numbers (5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19...) but is not proven for all numbers and, in fact, eventually fails (e.g., 25 = 1 less than 26, a multiple of 6, but 25 is not prime) — this is precisely what a conjecture is: a pattern-based guess, not an established, universally proved rule (a theorem) or a foundational assumption (an axiom).

**Q40.** Unitary-method daily-life problems among A, B, C.
**Correct answer: (4) — A and B.**
The unitary method finds the value of a single unit before scaling up or down: in A, you find one month's rent to scale to a year; in B, you find the price of one banana to work out how many can be bought. C is a proportional-reasoning/speed-distance problem (comparing relative positions at equal speeds), which is a different mathematical structure, not a unitary-method problem, even though it's also a daily-life scenario.

**Q41.** Circumference of a circle whose area equals a 176 cm × 56 cm rectangle.
**Correct answer: (2) — 352 cm.**
Rectangle area = 176 × 56 = 9856 cm². Setting πr² = 9856 with π = 22/7 gives r² = 9856 × 7/22 = 3136, so r = 56 cm. Circumference = 2πr = 2 × 22/7 × 56 = 352 cm. The key technique: equate the two areas first, solve for radius, then apply the circumference formula — don't confuse area and circumference formulas.

**Q42.** Value of x in a cuboid painting-cost problem.
**Correct answer: (2) — 15.**
Total surface area of the cuboid = 2(40×36 + 36×x + 40×x) = 2(1440 + 76x). Cost = ₹20 per 100 cm², total cost ₹1032, so total area = 1032/20 × 100 = 5160 cm². So 2(1440 + 76x) = 5160 → 1440 + 76x = 2580 → 76x = 1140 → x = 15. The key idea: convert the rate (₹ per 100 cm²) into total area using a simple proportion before equating to the surface-area formula.

**Q43.** Quadrilateral PQRS with PQ = PS and RQ = RS.
**Correct answer: (2) — Its diagonals are perpendicular to each other.**
Since P is equidistant from Q and S, and R is equidistant from Q and S, both P and R lie on the perpendicular bisector of QS — meaning line PR is exactly the perpendicular bisector of diagonal QS. This is the defining property of a "kite" shape, and it's a useful general fact: whenever two vertices of a quadrilateral are each equidistant from the other two, the diagonal joining them perpendicularly bisects the other diagonal.

**Q44.** Rhombus ABCD, ∠ADB = 25°; find 2∠BAD − ∠ABC.
**Correct answer: (3) — 210°.**
In a rhombus, diagonals bisect the vertex angles, so ∠ADC = 2 × 25° = 50°. Since consecutive angles in a rhombus (parallelogram) are supplementary, ∠BAD = 180° − 50° = 130°, and ∠ABC = 180° − ∠BAD = 50°. So 2∠BAD − ∠ABC = 260° − 50° = 210°. The core rhombus properties to remember: diagonals bisect vertex angles, and adjacent angles are supplementary.

**Q45.** Mean of mode, median, and range of a data set.
**Correct answer: (1) — 13.**
Sorted data: 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 11, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 23. Mode = 11 (appears twice); Median = average of 6th and 7th values = (11+13)/2 = 12; Range = 23 − 7 = 16. Mean of (11, 12, 16) = 39/3 = 13. The trick here is being careful to sort the data first — mode, median, and range are all meaningless if read off an unsorted list.

**Q46.** Value of 1/x + 1/y where x = √198 × √550, y = ∛99 × ∛363.
**Correct answer: (2) — 0·03.**
x = √(198 × 550) = √108900 = 330. y = ∛(99 × 363) = ∛35937 = 33. So 1/x + 1/y = 1/330 + 1/33 = 1/330 + 10/330 = 11/330 = 1/30 = 0·0333… ≈ 0·03. The technique: combine the roots first (√a × √b = √(ab); similarly for cube roots) before computing, rather than estimating each root separately.

**Q47.** p = (12·34 × 10¹⁰) − (5·67 × 10⁹) in standard form.
**Correct answer: (1) — 1·1773 × 10¹¹.**
Rewrite both terms with the same power of 10: 12·34 × 10¹⁰ = 123·4 × 10⁹. So p = 123·4 × 10⁹ − 5·67 × 10⁹ = 117·73 × 10⁹ = 1·1773 × 10¹¹. Note that shifting the decimal one place changes the exponent by one — this is the step test-takers often get wrong, mixing up 10¹⁰ and 10¹¹ in the final answer.

**Q48.** Number to multiply (−1/4)⁻¹ by to get the reciprocal of (−7/4)⁻¹.
**Correct answer: (1) — 7/16.**
(−1/4)⁻¹ = −4. (−7/4)⁻¹ = −4/7, whose reciprocal is −7/4. We need −4 × k = −7/4, so k = 7/16. The recurring idea: a negative number raised to the power −1 is just its reciprocal with the sign kept, and "reciprocal of a reciprocal" simply returns the original fraction — track signs carefully through each step.

**Q49.** 313632 = p² × q⁵ × r⁴; find p + q − 2r.
**Correct answer: (2) — 7.**
Prime factorising 313632 gives 2⁵ × 3⁴ × 11² (i.e., p = 11, q = 2, r = 3, matching the exponents 2, 5, 4 respectively as p²q⁵r⁴ = 11² × 2⁵ × 3⁴). So p + q − 2r = 11 + 2 − 6 = 7. The lesson: when matching a numeric factorisation to a symbolic expression, match exponents carefully to the correct base rather than assuming an order.

**Q50.** Pie-chart angle for Mathematics marks.
**Correct answer: (3) — 86·4°.**
Total marks = 72+84+96+80+68 = 400. Angle for Mathematics = (96/400) × 360° = 86·4°. The general formula to remember: sector angle = (component value ÷ total value) × 360°.

**Q51.** Curved surface area of a cylinder given volume and length.
**Correct answer: (1) — 2640 cm².**
Length = 120 cm. Volume = πr²h = 4620, so r² = 4620 × 7 / (22 × 120) = 4620×7/2640 = 12·25, so r = 3·5 cm. Curved surface area = 2πrh = 2 × 22/7 × 3·5 × 120 = 2640 cm². Always convert all lengths to the same unit (metres to cm here) before substituting into formulas — a very common slip in this question.

**Q52.** Relation true for F, V, E of a triangular prism.
**Correct answer: (2) — 2F + 3V − 2E = 10.**
A triangular prism has F = 5 faces, V = 6 vertices, E = 9 edges (satisfying Euler's formula F + V − E = 2, i.e., 5+6−9=2). Substituting into option 2: 2(5) + 3(6) − 2(9) = 10 + 18 − 18 = 10 ✓. This question is really testing Euler's formula for polyhedra along with basic substitution — always count F, V, E for the actual solid before testing each option.

**Q53.** Value of (8x − 10°) given ∠QPA = 7x, ∠QPB = 5x, P on line AB.
**Correct answer: (2) — 110°.**
Since A, P, B are collinear, ∠QPA + ∠QPB = 180° (angles on a straight line). So 7x + 5x = 180° → 12x = 180° → x = 15°. Then 8x − 10° = 120° − 10° = 110°. The key concept: angles on a straight line are supplementary (sum to 180°) — this "linear pair" idea underlies many geometry questions.

**Q54.** Value of (2∠PQR − ∠PRQ) given RS = PS = QS, ∠QPR = 15°.
**Correct answer: (2) — 60°.**
Since S is equidistant from P, Q, R, S is the circumcentre of triangle PQR lying on PQ — meaning PQ is a diameter of the circumcircle, so ∠PRQ = 90° (angle in a semicircle). Also, PS = RS means triangle PSR is isosceles, so ∠SPR = ∠SRP = 15°, and since ∠PRQ = 90° = ∠SRP + ∠SRQ, we get ∠SRQ = 75°; as QS = RS, triangle QSR is isosceles too, so ∠SQR = ∠SRQ = 75°, meaning ∠PQR = 75°. So 2(75°) − 90° = 60°. The reusable idea: when a point is equidistant from all three vertices of a triangle inscribed so that one side becomes a diameter, the angle in the semicircle is always 90°.

**Q55.** Profit/loss percent when selling item A (loss 20% at ₹384) and item B (profit 25% at ₹400) together for ₹852.
**Correct answer: (1) — Profit, 6·5%.**
Cost price of A = 384/0·8 = ₹480. Cost price of B = 400/1·25 = ₹320. Total CP = ₹800. Total SP = ₹852 (given, combined). Profit = 852 − 800 = ₹52. Profit% = 52/800 × 100 = 6·5%. The core technique: always find cost price from selling price and the profit/loss percentage using CP = SP/(1 ± profit or loss fraction), then compare total CP to total SP.

**Q56.** Amount at same rate compounded annually instead of half-yearly.
**Correct answer: (1) — ₹15,840.**
First find the rate from the half-yearly case: 12000(1 + r/200)³ = 15972, giving (1+r/200)³ = 1·331 = (1·1)³, so 1 + r/200 = 1·1 → r/200 = 0·1 → r = 20% per annum. Compounded annually for 1½ years means one full year's compounding plus a half-year simple-interest-style addition on the resulting amount: Amount after 1 year = 12000 × 1·2 = 14400; for the remaining half year, add half of the annual interest on 14400: 14400 + (0·5 × 0·20 × 14400) = 14400 + 1440 = 15840. The key insight tested: for a fractional time period like 1½ years, the "extra" half-year is usually treated as simple interest on the amount at the end of the last completed compounding period.

**Q57.** Value of (a − b) given 5x² − 8y − 20x + 2xy = (x + a)(bx + 2y).
**Correct answer: (3) — −9.**
Expanding (x + a)(bx + 2y) = bx² + 2xy + abx + 2ay. Matching with 5x² + 2xy − 20x − 8y: coefficient of x² gives b = 5; coefficient of y gives 2a = −8, so a = −4; check x-term: abx = (−4)(5)x = −20x ✓, matching. So a − b = −4 − 5 = −9. The reusable technique: expand the factored form fully, then match coefficients of each like term (x², xy, x, y, constant) one at a time.

**Q58.** Value of (5p + q − r) given A + B − C expressed as px² + qx²y² + ry².
**Correct answer: (4) — 8.**
A = x² − 3x²y², B = x²y² − 2x², C = 3y² − 12x²y². So A + B − C = (x² − 3x²y²) + (x²y² − 2x²) − (3y² − 12x²y²) = (x² − 2x²) + (−3x²y² + x²y² + 12x²y²) + (−3y²) = −x² + 10x²y² − 3y². So p = −1, q = 10, r = −3. Then 5p + q − r = −5 + 10 − (−3) = 8. The technique: expand each expression term by term first, then collect like terms carefully by their variable pattern (x², x²y², y²) before matching to p, q, r.

**Q59.** Which numbers are divisible by 2, 3, and 11?
**Correct answer: (3) — A and D.**
8448: even ✓; digit sum 8+4+4+8=24, divisible by 3 ✓; for 11, alternating sum (8−4+4−8)=0, divisible by 11 ✓. 9636: even ✓; digit sum 9+6+3+6=24, divisible by 3 ✓; alternating sum (9−6+3−6)=0, divisible by 11 ✓. **Q60.** The equation whose solution matches [(x+1)−(2x+4)]/(3−5x) = 1/23.
**Correct answer: (3) — 3(2x + 3) = 5(x + 1).**
Simplify the given equation: numerator = x+1−2x−4 = −x−3. So (−x−3)/(3−5x) = 1/23 → 23(−x−3) = 3−5x → −23x−69 = 3−5x → −18x = 72 → x = −4. Testing option 3: 3(2(−4)+3) = 3(−5) = −15; 5(−4+1) = 5(−3) = −15 ✓ — matches. The technique here: solve the given equation for x fully first, then substitute that x into each option to see which one is also satisfied — faster than solving all four options algebraically.

**Q61.** Assertion-Reason on stomach HCl and digestion.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**
The stomach does secrete hydrochloric acid that helps break down food (A), and this is properly explained by R: digestive enzymes such as pepsin need an acidic medium to become active and function effectively — so R correctly explains why the acid is produced in the first place.

**Q62.** Meaning of 'nitrogen fixation'.
**Correct answer: (4) — Conversion of atmospheric nitrogen to a more usable form for living organisms.**
Nitrogen fixation specifically refers to converting inert atmospheric nitrogen gas (N₂), which most organisms cannot use directly, into compounds like ammonia or nitrates that plants can absorb. This is distinct from nitrification (ammonia → nitrate, option 2) and decomposition/mineralisation (option 3), which are separate steps of the nitrogen cycle.

**Q63.** Position of least potential energy in a spring.
**Correct answer: (1) — The spring is neither compressed nor pulled out and is lying on the ground.**
A spring's elastic potential energy depends on how much it is deformed (compressed or stretched) from its natural length — an undeformed spring stores zero elastic PE. Height above the ground affects gravitational PE, not elastic PE, so the only state with minimum (zero) potential energy overall is the undeformed spring at ground level.

**Q64.** Set of cold-blooded (ectothermic) organisms.
**Correct answer: (3) — Snake, Frog, Cockroach.**
Reptiles, amphibians, and insects are all ectothermic (cold-blooded) — their body temperature varies with the environment. Birds and mammals (parrot, dolphin, pigeon, humans, whale) are endothermic (warm-blooded), which is why every other option mixes a warm-blooded animal into the set.

**Q65.** Units measuring the same physical quantity.
**Correct answer: (2) — kg m²/s², Ws.**
kg·m²/s² is the SI unit of energy/work (equivalent to a joule), and a watt-second (Ws) is also a unit of energy (power × time = joule). Nm (newton-metre) is also energy/torque, but it's paired incorrectly with kg m/s² (a unit of force, not energy) in option 1 and 3, so only option 2 correctly pairs two energy units.

**Q66.** Fill in the blanks: zygote/embryo/uterus/foetus sequence.
**Correct answer: (2) — zygote, embryo, uterus, foetus.**
After fertilisation, sperm and egg fuse to form a zygote (W), which divides to form a developing structure called an embryo (X) that embeds in the wall of the uterus (Y); once all body parts are identifiable, this developing structure is called a foetus (Z). Getting the order (zygote → embryo → foetus) right, and correctly placing "uterus" as the site of implantation, is the crux of this question.

**Q67.** Which displacement reaction is not feasible?
**Correct answer: (3) — Zn + CaSO₄.**
This reaction is not feasible because calcium is more reactive than zinc in the reactivity series, so zinc cannot displace calcium from its salt solution. In contrast, Fe can displace Pb (option 1), Al can displace Cu (option 2), and Mg can displace Zn (option 4) — all displacements from a more reactive metal onto a less reactive metal's salt, which is exactly the rule that makes a displacement reaction feasible.

**Q68.** Which graphs are straight lines?
**Correct answer: (1) — A, B and D.**
Distance-time graph of a body at rest is a horizontal straight line (distance constant) — A; velocity-time graph of uniformly accelerated motion is a straight line with constant slope — B; velocity-time graph of a body at rest is a horizontal straight line at zero velocity — D. Distance-time graph of uniformly accelerated motion (C) is a curve (parabola), not a straight line, because distance increases non-linearly with time under constant acceleration.

**Q69.** Floating behaviour based on densities of liquids A<B<C<D.
**Correct answer: (1) — An object that floats in liquid C may or may not float in liquid A.**
An object floats in a liquid if its own density is less than or equal to the liquid's density. Since C is denser than A, an object could have a density between A's and C's — such an object would float in C but sink in A, so it "may or may not" float in A (depends on the object's exact density). This kind of question tests whether you understand that floating is a comparison between two densities, not an absolute property of the object.

**Q70.** Assertion-Reason: is electric current a vector?
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is false, but (R) is true.**
Electric current, despite having an associated direction of flow, does not obey the laws of vector addition (currents at a junction add algebraically, not vectorially, per Kirchhoff's current law), so it is treated as a scalar quantity in physics — making A false. R itself, taken in isolation, is a true general statement (a quantity having both magnitude and direction is typically vector), but current is the well-known exception, which is exactly why this question is a classic conceptual trap.

**Q71.** Cell organelles containing their own DNA.
**Correct answer: (3) — A, B and C** *(Plastids, Mitochondria, and Nucleus contain DNA; the Vacuole does not).*
Mitochondria and plastids (like chloroplasts) contain their own circular DNA, a relic of their evolutionary origin as free-living bacteria absorbed by early eukaryotic cells (the endosymbiotic theory) — this is why they can even divide somewhat independently of the cell. The nucleus, obviously, houses the bulk of a cell's DNA. The vacuole is simply a storage sac with no DNA of its own.

**Q72.** Statements true of all living cells.
**Correct answer: (1) — Only D.**
Every cell has a cell membrane (D) — this is a universal feature of all cells. But not all cells are microscopic (some, like an ostrich egg cell or certain plant fibre cells, are visible to the naked eye — A is false), not all cells have a cell wall (only plant, fungal, and bacterial cells do; animal cells don't — B is false), and not all cells have a nuclear membrane (prokaryotic cells like bacteria lack one — C is false).

**Q73.** Matching waste type to decomposition time.
**Correct answer: (2) — a–v, b–iv, c–i, d–ii, e–iii.**
Plastic (a synthetic polymer) takes the longest of all to break down, since microorganisms cannot easily digest its long-chain structure — matched to "several years" (v). Silk, a natural protein fibre, still persists a long time — about 4 years (iv). Cotton, a natural cellulose fibre, decomposes faster — over 2–5 months (i). A banana peel, being soft organic matter, decomposes fastest of all — within 1–2 weeks (ii). Paper, also cellulose-based but processed, takes a bit longer than a banana peel — 10–30 days (iii). The general principle to retain: natural/biodegradable materials decompose much faster than synthetic ones, and how soft or processed a material is also affects decomposition speed.

**Q74.** Net force on a feather falling at constant velocity.
**Correct answer: (1) — Zero.**
By Newton's first law, an object moving at constant velocity (including "terminal velocity" for a falling feather, where air resistance balances gravity) has zero net force acting on it — the forces of gravity and air resistance exactly cancel out. This is a frequently tested idea: constant velocity always implies zero net force, regardless of how large the individual forces are.

**Q75.** Protons, electrons, neutrons in X³⁺ (atomic mass 27, atomic number 13).
**Correct answer: (4) — 13, 10 and 14.**
Atomic number = number of protons = 13 (unchanged by ionisation). Neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 27 − 13 = 14 (also unchanged). Since X³⁺ has lost 3 electrons, electrons = 13 − 3 = 10. The reusable rule: protons and neutrons never change when an atom becomes an ion — only the electron count changes, decreasing for a cation (positive ion) and increasing for an anion (negative ion).

**Q76.** Plants producing bisexual flowers.
**Correct answer: (2) — Mustard, Rose, Petunia.**
Mustard, rose, and petunia all produce flowers containing both male (stamen) and female (pistil) parts in the same flower — bisexual (or "perfect") flowers. Cucumber, papaya, and corn are all classic examples of plants bearing unisexual flowers (separate male and female flowers, sometimes even on separate plants), which is why every other option mixes in one of these unisexual-flower species.

**Q77.** Incorrect statement about clayey vs. loamy soil.
**Correct answer: option 1 or option 2 accepted (errata) — "Clayey soil is better aerated than loamy soil" and "Clayey soil is more fertile as compared to loamy soil."**
CBSE accepted both options 1 and 2 as valid answers to this question due to a genuine ambiguity in the source statements. Conceptually: clayey soil has small, tightly packed particles, giving it *poor* aeration (not better than loamy soil) — so option 1 is a clearly incorrect (hence correctly identified) statement. Whether clayey soil is "more fertile" than loamy soil is debatable depending on definition of fertility (loam is typically considered the most agriculturally ideal soil), which is why CBSE accepted both as valid "incorrect statements." The reliable facts to retain: loamy soil, being a balanced mix of sand, silt and clay, has the best aeration and water retention balance for plant growth; clayey soil retains water well but drains and aerates poorly.

**Q78.** Substances on a matchbox's rubbing surface.
**Correct answer: (1) — Glass powder and red phosphorus.**
The striking surface of a matchbox is coated with a mixture of red phosphorus and powdered glass (which provides friction); the matchstick head itself contains chemicals like antimony trisulphide and an oxidiser. White phosphorus is not used commercially in matchboxes because it is highly toxic and dangerously reactive (self-igniting in air), which is why it was phased out in favour of red phosphorus.

**Q79.** Glands A, B receiving orders from gland C.
**Correct answer: (2) — Thyroid, Adrenal glands, Pituitary.**
The pituitary gland is often called the "master gland" because it releases hormones that stimulate other endocrine glands, including the thyroid and adrenal glands, to secrete their own hormones. This hierarchical control (pituitary → other glands) is the key concept — recognise that the pituitary is always the "commanding" gland (C) in such a triad, unlike testes/ovaries, which are controlled by the pituitary too but were not the pairing tested here.

**Q80.** Why the pinhole camera image is real.
**Correct answer: (2) — Light travels in a straight line.**
A pinhole camera forms an image because light rays from an object travel in straight lines through the tiny hole and land inverted on the screen, crossing paths at the pinhole. This rectilinear propagation of light is the basic principle explaining pinhole imaging — not diffraction, refraction, or the general nature of light as energy (which are all true statements about light but don't explain *this specific* phenomenon).

**Q81.** Identify the open-ended question.
**Correct answer: (3) — What would happen if humans could digest cellulose?**
An open-ended question invites imaginative, exploratory, and varied reasoning with no single fixed answer — a hypothetical "what if" question is the clearest example here. The other three questions ("why," "what is it made of," "name five organisms") each have a single, factual, retrievable correct answer, making them closed questions.

**Q82.** Current curricular integration practices.
**Correct answer: (1) — B and C.**
Physics, Chemistry, and Biology are indeed taught in an integrated manner as a single subject called "Science" at the secondary stage (B is accurate current practice). Science and Social Science are also genuinely combined as "Environmental Studies" (EVS) at the primary stage — EVS is explicitly designed to blend the natural and social environment into one subject (C is accurate). Statement A is false: Science and Mathematics are not taught combined as "Science and Technology" in elementary classes — they remain separate subjects throughout school. Remember this pattern: EVS = Science + Social Science (primary), Science = Physics + Chemistry + Biology (secondary) — but Mathematics is never merged into either.

**Q83.** Not a suitable indicator for assessing the boiling-point measurement task.
**Correct answer: (3) — Measure the boiling temperature as 100°C.**
Assuming the boiling point will always come out as exactly 100°C is not a fair or suitable *process* indicator — ordinary tap water may boil at a slightly different temperature due to impurities, altitude, or pressure, and the whole point of the activity is to have students measure and observe, not assume a textbook value. The other three options (checking least count, avoiding wall contact, using a stand) are all genuinely good experimental practices worth assessing.

**Q84.** NOT a valid reason for including socio-scientific issues in the science curriculum.
**Correct answer: (4) — Helpful in appreciating science as a definitive discipline.**
Socio-scientific issues (like GM food) are valuable precisely because they show science as a discipline involving debate, uncertainty, and value judgements — not as a "definitive," settled body of facts. Presenting science as always definitive misrepresents the nature of science and contradicts the very reason such issues are included (to build argumentation and evidence-based reasoning skills, as in options 1–3).

**Q85.** Process skill emphasised by the snail/plant/CO₂ activity.
**Correct answer: (1) — Predicting.**
Asking students which test tube would have the highest CO₂ concentration *before* they actually test it (with an indicator like limewater, in a later step) requires them to use their existing knowledge of respiration and photosynthesis to anticipate an outcome — this is precisely what "predicting" means as a science process skill, distinct from measuring (using instruments) or estimating (approximating a quantity).

**Q86.** NOT correct about 'Extended Learning' activities in textbooks.
**Correct answer: (2) — These form an important basis of students' evaluation.**
Extended Learning activities in NCERT textbooks are meant to enrich understanding and connect learners with the wider world (experts, community, independent inquiry) — they are not intended as formal evaluation tools for grading students. Treating them as an "important basis of evaluation" misrepresents their actual pedagogical purpose, which is enrichment, not assessment.

**Q87.** Not implied in 'Content validity' of the science curriculum.
**Correct answer: all options accepted (Z) — genuine ambiguity/erratum.**
"Content validity" means the curriculum content is accurate, updated, and properly represents the concepts it claims to teach. Whether "trivialising concepts for some learners" (C) counts as a violation of content validity, or a separate issue of pedagogical differentiation, was ambiguous enough that CBSE accepted all four options (A, B, and C, i.e., "A, B and C") as correct, alongside the sole option (B). Conceptually, retain this: content validity is fundamentally about accuracy and currency of subject matter, not about how it is presented to different learners (which relates more to pedagogical or instructional validity).

**Q88.** Most suitable rationale for team teaching.
**Correct answer: (2) — It benefits the students with diverse expertise.**
Team teaching's core strength is combining teachers with different specialisations or strengths so that students gain from a wider range of expertise than one teacher alone could offer. It is not primarily justified by class size (1), easier assessment (3), or participation levels (4) — these may be side benefits, but they are not the defining rationale for why team teaching is done.

**Q89.** Best activity to introduce spherical-mirror image formation.
**Correct answer: (2) — Students are asked to play with shiny, steel spoons and observe their images in it.**
A curved, shiny spoon surface behaves like a concave mirror on one side and a convex mirror on the other, giving students a simple, hands-on, exploratory first encounter with how curved mirrors distort and form images — before any formal laws or ray diagrams are introduced. This activity-first approach (concrete experience before abstract rules) reflects good science pedagogy, whereas jumping straight to laws of reflection or ray-diagram procedures (options 3, 4) skips the exploratory stage.

**Q90.** NOT implied by 'true to the child, true to life, true to the discipline'.
**Correct answer: (3) — Science education should train the students to remain disciplined in school.**
This famous phrase (from the Yashpal Committee/NCF) is about being faithful to the child's developmental stage, connecting science to real-life experience, and staying true to the logical/methodological rigour of science as a discipline — it has nothing to do with student behavioural discipline in the everyday sense of classroom conduct, which is a wordplay trap based on the double meaning of "discipline."

## Part III: Language I (English)

**Q91.** Effect of showing a captioned movie.
**Correct answer: (1) — listening strategies, active viewing, and motivating the students to use authentic language material.**
Captioned authentic video material engages multiple language skills at once — students listen to spoken dialogue while reading captions, actively viewing for meaning, all using real (authentic) language rather than simplified textbook language. This combined benefit is broader than options 2–4, which each capture only a narrower part of the effect.

**Q92.** Assertion-Reason: early childhood verb/noun use and grammar.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**
Young children genuinely tend to use more verbs than nouns early on and often ignore adult grammar rules (A is a documented pattern in early language acquisition). This is properly explained by R: a young child's vocabulary range is still limited, so they lean on a small set of high-utility action words and simple word order rather than a fuller, rule-governed grammar that a wider vocabulary would support — the restricted word stock directly accounts for the simplified, verb-heavy speech pattern described in A.

**Q93.** Child's learning preference — feeling objects while blindfolded.
**Correct answer: (1) — I remember language well when I move.**
Physically manipulating and touching an object while guessing what it is engages kinaesthetic/tactile learning — learning through physical, hands-on movement and action — which is what "I remember well when I move" captures, as opposed to purely visual or auditory preferences.

**Q94.** Strategy matching "If I don't know what something means, I look it up."
**Correct answer: (1) — Consulting reference materials.**
"Looking up" an unfamiliar word or meaning is literally the act of consulting a dictionary or other reference source — a specific, well-defined vocabulary-learning strategy distinct from practising language, feeling confused, or noticing word combinations (collocations).

**Q95.** What a diagnostic test assesses.
**Correct answer: (4) — learners' strong and weak points in language.**
A diagnostic test is specifically designed to identify a learner's specific strengths and weaknesses before or during instruction, so a teacher can plan targeted intervention — this is different from a proficiency test (assessing overall level reached) or a summative/ranking test (comparing achievement across learners).

**Q96.** What a proficiency test always assesses.
**Correct answer: (1) — the level of language that learners have reached.**
Proficiency tests measure a learner's overall command of a language, independent of any specific syllabus or course they may have studied — this is why it assesses the general level reached, not "what has been taught" (that would define an achievement test instead).

**Q97.** Purpose of a pre-reading quiz about 'elephants'.
**Correct answer: (1) — generate interest in a topic.**
A short quiz before reading, on the topic the text will cover, is a classic pre-reading activity used to activate students' existing knowledge and spark curiosity about the topic — priming them to engage more actively with the text that follows, rather than assessing memory or providing peer feedback.

**Q98.** Handling a disruptive student.
**Correct answer: (1) — first talk to the children to offer remedies.**
Good classroom management favours addressing behaviour directly and constructively with the student(s) involved first, rather than immediately escalating to authorities or parents, or ignoring the issue by only attending to well-behaved students — direct, timely dialogue is the first and most respectful step.

**Q99.** Skill reflected by "brainstorm, organise, draft, edit and revise."
**Correct answer: (3) — Writing skill.**
This sequence — brainstorming ideas, organising them, drafting, editing, and revising — is the standard process-writing approach taught in language classrooms, describing how a piece of writing is developed from idea to polished final text.

**Q100.** Assertion-Reason: reading/writing and critical thinking.
**Correct answer: (4) — (A) is true, but (R) is false.**
Reading and writing skills do support critical thinking at the primary level (A is true) — through activities like inferring, comparing, and evaluating texts. However, R inverts the relationship: critical thinking is not a "sub-skill" of reading and writing; if anything, critical thinking is a broader cognitive capacity that reading and writing help develop, so R is false.

**Q101.** How language skills are best learnt.
**Correct answer: (3) — if they are dealt with in an integrated manner.**
Modern language pedagogy favours integrating the four skills (listening, speaking, reading, writing) together in meaningful, communicative tasks, since real-world language use rarely isolates one skill — mechanical drills, single-skill focus, or written-only assessment (options 1, 2, 4) don't reflect how language is actually used or best acquired.

**Q102.** Assessing understanding of narrative skill.
**Correct answer: (1) — share an incident when they were surprised.**
Narrative skill is about the ability to construct and tell a coherent story/account of events — asking students to narrate a personal incident directly requires them to produce a narrative, unlike grammar-identification tasks (2, 3) or comprehension-based reading tasks (4), which test different skills (grammar awareness, reading comprehension) rather than narrative production.

**Q103.** Teacher facilitating reading beyond the prescribed textbook.
**Correct answer: (3) — Extensive reading.**
Extensive reading refers to reading widely, for pleasure and general fluency, across a range of materials beyond the set textbook — as opposed to intensive reading (close, detailed study of a specific short text), shared reading (a specific guided classroom technique), or guided reading (teacher-led, leveled reading in small groups).

**Q104.** NOT relevant while selecting textbooks and resources.
**Correct answer: (4) — Conceptual complexity and density of material.**
When selecting textbooks and resources, the primary considerations are learner-centred: how adaptable the material is to different classroom needs, the learning needs and languages the children already know, and the students' level. "Conceptual complexity and density," taken as a separate, free-standing criterion, is effectively already covered once "level of the students" is considered — treating it as an additional, independent factor is redundant, which is why it is marked as the one not separately relevant here.

**Q105.** Most helpful criteria for monitoring material use.
**Correct answer: (3) — Observation, feedback sessions and written reports.**
Monitoring how learners actually use language materials is best done through direct observation of learners in action, combined with structured feedback and documented reports — a mix of qualitative, ongoing methods. Abstract acronyms like "present, prepare, practice" or vague notions like "usability, repetition, production" don't describe genuine *monitoring* criteria/methods the way observation-feedback-reports does.

**Q106.** Attribute NOT associated with the lily (in the poem).
**Correct answer: (1) — The colour red.**
The poem explicitly associates "rose-red" with the rose and "lily-white" with the lily ("her Juno mien," "pale lily") — red is the rose's colour, not the lily's, in this extract. The lily is instead associated with paleness, statuesque grandeur ("stately"), and loveliness — the other three options.

**Q107.** Assertion-Reason on the poem not declaring the lily queen.
**Correct answer: option accepted with erratum (B) → options 1 and 3 both accepted.**
The poem does leave the question of "undisputed queen" between rose and lily unresolved (Love ultimately asks for both colours combined in the lotus), so it is broadly true that the poets did not declare a single winner (A). The reason offered — that the lily's paleness is why — is only one possible factor among several in the poem's playful indecision, so both option (1) ("A is true, but R is not the only reason") and option (3) ("Both A and R are true") were accepted by CBSE as defensible readings of this literary extract.

**Q108.** Whose dispute echoed in Psyche's garden.
**Correct answer: (4) — the lily and the rose.**
The poem is explicitly a rivalry ("Rivals for that high honor") between the rose and the lily over which flower deserves to be "queen" — the lotus is only introduced at the very end as Flora's compromise solution, not as a party to the original dispute.

**Q109.** Rhyme scheme of lines 1–4.
**Correct answer: (3) — abba.**
Reading the end-words of lines 1–4 ("flower," "queen," "been," "power") shows lines 1 and 4 rhyme (flower/power) while lines 2 and 3 rhyme (queen/been) — an enclosed rhyme pattern, written as abba, where the outer lines rhyme with each other and the inner lines rhyme with each other.

**Q110.** Poetic devices in Line 1 and Line 10.
**Correct answer: (2) — personification, simile.**
Line 1 ("Love came to Flora asking for a flower") personifies the abstract idea of Love as a character who acts and speaks. Line 10 ("stately as the lily in her pride") uses "as" to directly compare a quality to the lily — the hallmark structure of a simile.

**Q111.** Word analogy: delicious : inviting :: strife : ?
**Correct answer: (1) — conflict.**
"Delicious" and "inviting" are near-synonyms (something delicious is inviting to eat/experience); the analogy requires a near-synonym for "strife," and "conflict" is the closest synonym (both mean discord or struggle), unlike melody, adventure, or discussion, which are unrelated in meaning.

The passage explicitly states that "the needs of employers and the economy can change very quickly, and often unexpectedly" — directly supporting this option, while the other choices (career decisions, employer preferences, dentist shortages) are either not stated as the *reason* or are just illustrative examples within the passage, not the general explanation asked for.

In this context, "keeping one's head down" is an idiom meaning to stay focused and work hard through difficulty without giving up — essentially, to face and push through challenges — not literally bowing one's head or avoiding trouble, which would contradict the passage's message of perseverance.

**Q116.** Parts of speech of underlined words in "A majority of people think that their dream job is too hard to achieve."
**Correct answer: (4) — noun, adverb.**
"Majority" functions as a noun (a quantity/group), and "too" functions as an adverb modifying the adjective "hard" (indicating degree). Recognising that "too" modifies an adjective, and therefore is itself an adverb, is the key grammatical distinction tested here.

**Q117.** Meaning of "feel limited by the choices they made."
**Correct answer: (2) — to feel constrained.**
"Feeling limited" means feeling restricted or boxed in by past decisions — "constrained" is the closest synonym, capturing a sense of restriction, as opposed to feeling dejected, limitless (the literal opposite), or confused.

**Q118.** Assertion-Reason: ease of getting a job vs. economic scenario.

The passage argues it is actually hard, not "quite easy," to get a job immediately after graduating in a tough economy (making A incorrect), while it does affirm that the economic scenario regarding job generation is genuinely difficult (making R correct) — consistent with the passage's overall message that persistence, not immediate ease, is required.

**Q119.** Meaning of "learning the trade from the bottom up."
**Correct answer: (1) — from the lowest to the highest in hierarchy.**
This idiom describes starting at the most junior/basic level of a profession and gradually working one's way up through experience — exactly what the passage describes as "climbing up the career ladder" from humble beginnings, not literally trading places or learning "from colleagues" or across departments.

**Q120.** Word analogy: expectedly : unexpectedly :: rejection : ?
**Correct answer: (4) — selection.**
"Expectedly" and "unexpectedly" are opposites in meaning, formed by adding the negating prefix "un-". The analogy therefore asks for the word that is opposite in meaning to "rejection" — and "selection" (being chosen) is the clearest semantic opposite of "rejection" (being turned down) among the options, unlike "dejection" (a near-synonym mood word, not an opposite), "eviction," or "probation," which are unrelated in meaning.

भाषा अध्यापक द्वारा व्यक्तिपरक (individualised) अधिगम योजना बनाया जाना वास्तव में महत्त्वपूर्ण है (A सही), और यह भी सत्य है कि नई भाषाओं के अधिगम में शिक्षार्थियों में विविधता होती है (R सही)। परन्तु R केवल विविधता के अस्तित्व को बताता है, यह सीधे यह नहीं समझाता कि व्यक्तिपरक योजना ही क्यों आवश्यक है — इसलिए यह A की सटीक व्याख्या नहीं बनती।

**प्र.123.** प्रथम भाषा की केन्द्रीय भूमिका वाली विधि।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — व्याकरण-अनुवाद विधि।**
व्याकरण-अनुवाद विधि (Grammar-Translation Method) में नई भाषा को सीखने के लिए लगातार शिक्षार्थी की मातृभाषा/प्रथम भाषा में अनुवाद किया जाता है, इसलिए प्रथम भाषा की भूमिका केन्द्रीय होती है — जबकि प्रत्यक्ष विधि और संप्रेषणात्मक विधि जानबूझकर प्रथम भाषा के प्रयोग से बचती हैं।

**प्र.124.** चेतन का भोजपुरी छोड़कर केवल अंग्रेज़ी बोलना।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — ऋणात्मक द्विभाषावाद (Subtractive Bilingualism)।**
जब कोई व्यक्ति नई दूसरी भाषा सीखते हुए अपनी प्रथम भाषा को पूरी तरह खोता जाता है — जैसा चेतन के साथ हुआ — तो इसे ऋणात्मक द्विभाषावाद कहा जाता है, क्योंकि यहाँ नई भाषा जोड़ी नहीं जा रही बल्कि पुरानी भाषा की जगह ले रही है। यह योगात्मक द्विभाषावाद (जहाँ दोनों भाषाएँ बनी रहती हैं) से बिल्कुल विपरीत स्थिति है।

**प्र.125.** पठन-पूर्व गतिविधि।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — अनुमान लगाने से संबंधी कार्य का प्रयोग।**
पठन-पूर्व गतिविधियाँ शिक्षार्थियों को पाठ पढ़ने से पहले विषयवस्तु के बारे में पूर्वानुमान (prediction) लगाने के लिए प्रेरित करती हैं, जिससे रुचि जागृत होती है और पाठ से जुड़ाव आसान होता है — सारांशीकरण, व्याकरण शिक्षण या भावानुवाद संबंधी कार्य पठन-पश्चात या पठन-मध्य गतिविधियाँ हैं।

**प्र.126.** उपचारात्मक शिक्षण के बारे में सही कथन।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — प्रदर्शन/उपलब्धि सुधार हेतु मदद देना।**
उपचारात्मक (remedial) शिक्षण का उद्देश्य विशिष्ट कठिनाइयों की पहचान कर लक्षित सहायता देकर विद्यार्थी के प्रदर्शन में सुधार लाना है — यह सहानुभूति दिखाने, अतिरिक्त अंक देने या केवल मेधावी बच्चों पर ध्यान देने से भिन्न, एक व्यवस्थित शैक्षणिक हस्तक्षेप है।

**प्र.127.** अभिकथन-तर्क: अंग्रेज़ी सह-राजकीय भाषा।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — (A) सही है, परन्तु (R) ग़लत है।**
भारत के संविधान के अनुसार अंग्रेज़ी वास्तव में एक सह-राजकीय भाषा है (A सही), परन्तु भारतीय विद्यालयों में अंग्रेज़ी को सामान्यतः द्वितीय भाषा के रूप में पढ़ाया जाता है, विदेशी भाषा के रूप में नहीं (R ग़लत) — यह अंतर पहचानना इस प्रश्न की कुंजी है।

**प्र.128.** भाषा की विशेषता नहीं है।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — भाषा स्थिर है।**
भाषा गतिशील (dynamic) होती है — समय के साथ नए शब्द जुड़ते हैं, प्रयोग बदलते हैं, अर्थ विकसित होते हैं — इसलिए "भाषा स्थिर है" कहना ग़लत है। भाषा का सांकेतिक, सामाजिक और व्यवस्थित होना उसकी वास्तविक और स्वीकृत विशेषताएँ हैं।

**प्र.129.** अभिकथन-तर्क: पूर्व ज्ञान का संकेत देना।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — (A) और (R) दोनों सही हैं तथा (R), (A) की सही व्याख्या है।**
विद्यार्थियों को पहले के ज्ञान की याद दिलाना वास्तव में महत्त्वपूर्ण है (A सही), और इसका सटीक कारण भी सही है: पूर्व ज्ञान का प्रत्यास्मरण नई भाषा को पहले से जानी गई भाषा से जोड़ने में सीधे मदद करता है (R सही और उचित व्याख्या) — यह भाषा-शिक्षण में "known to unknown" के सिद्धांत को दर्शाता है।

**प्र.130.** ऊर्जा-बचत लीफ़लेट कार्य किस विशेषता को पोषित करेगा।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — सकारात्मक आदेशसूचक (Positive Imperatives)।**
ऊर्जा बचाने के सुझाव देने वाला लीफ़लेट स्वाभाविक रूप से आदेशसूचक वाक्यों ("बिजली बंद करें", "पंखा बंद रखें" जैसे) का प्रयोग करेगा, क्योंकि इसका उद्देश्य पाठक को कोई कार्य करने हेतु प्रेरित करना है — यह प्रश्न-स्वरूप, तुलनात्मक रूप या भूतकाल से संबंधित नहीं है।

**प्र.131.** व्यापार संबंधी सम्प्रेषण सिखाने का उद्देश्य किस विधा से मेल खाता है।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — पत्र (Letter)।**
ग्राहकों के साथ विशिष्ट व्यापारिक सम्प्रेषण सिखाने का उद्देश्य पत्र-लेखन (जैसे व्यावसायिक/औपचारिक पत्र) की विधा से सबसे अधिक मेल खाता है, क्योंकि पत्र ही वह विधा है जिसमें प्रत्यक्ष रूप से किसी को संबोधित कर व्यापारिक बात कही जाती है — रिपोर्ट, वर्णन और आलेख की अपनी अलग संरचना और उद्देश्य होते हैं।

**प्र.132.** वाद्य यंत्रों पर चर्चा गतिविधि किस संज्ञानात्मक कौशल से मेल खाती है।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — विश्लेषण करना।**
विद्यार्थियों से यह पूछना कि विभिन्न संगीत समारोहों में अलग-अलग वाद्य यंत्रों का प्रयोग क्यों किया जाता है, उन्हें कारणों को तोड़कर समझने और तुलना करने के लिए प्रेरित करता है — यह विश्लेषणात्मक चिंतन (Analysing) का उदाहरण है, न कि केवल सृजन, तर्क देने या कल्पना का।

**प्र.133.** एकल भाषी वक्ता का औपचारिक से अनौपचारिक स्थिति में जाना।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — कोड परिवर्तन (Code Switching, रजिस्टर के अर्थ में)।**
जब एक ही भाषा के भीतर वक्ता औपचारिक शैली से अनौपचारिक शैली में (शैली/रजिस्टर परिवर्तन) जाता है, तो इसे यहाँ कोड परिवर्तन के रूप में वर्गीकृत किया गया है, जो कोड मिश्रण (दो भाषाओं को एक वाक्य में मिलाना) से भिन्न है — यहाँ भाषा वही रहती है, केवल औपचारिकता का स्तर बदलता है।

**प्र.134.** बच्चों की अनौपचारिक अंतःक्रियाएँ किस स्वरूप पर आधारित होती हैं।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — भाषा सामाजीकरण (Language Socialization)।**
बच्चे अपने समुदाय में भाषा के सामाजिक प्रयोग के माध्यम से ही मित्रता और संबंध बनाना सीखते हैं — भाषा सामाजीकरण की यह अवधारणा बताती है कि भाषा-अधिगम और सामाजिक अंतःक्रिया साथ-साथ चलते हैं, न कि केवल अनुकूलन, आत्मसात्करण या सांस्कृतिकरण जैसी अलग-थलग प्रक्रियाओं के माध्यम से।

**प्र.135.** व्याकरण, शब्दावली के प्रयोग के संबंध में क्या दृष्टि देता है।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — शब्दकोशीय बिन्दु को अर्थपूर्ण और सम्प्रेषणात्मक अभिव्यक्ति में जोड़ पाना।**
व्याकरण का व्यावहारिक उद्देश्य व्यक्तिगत शब्दों (शब्दकोशीय बिन्दुओं) को सार्थक और संप्रेषणीय वाक्यों/अभिव्यक्तियों में जोड़ने का ढाँचा देना है — यही वह पुल है जो शब्दावली को उपयोगी संप्रेषण में बदलता है।

**प्र.136.** प्राण किस से बहना चाहिए।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — गहराई।**
गद्यांश स्पष्ट रूप से कहता है कि दिन भर हमारा प्राण "गहरे से" बहना चाहिए — अर्थात गहराई ही वह गुण है जिस पर बल दिया गया है, न कि गति की तीव्रता या धीमापन अकेले।

**प्र.137.** ऊर्जावान, शक्तिवान और शांत स्वभाव के लिए क्या आवश्यक है।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — प्राण वायु का धीमा और गहराई से बहना।**
गद्यांश के अनुसार जब हम प्राण को धीमा और गहरा भरना शुरू करते हैं, तभी हम ऊर्जावान, प्राणवान, शक्तिवान और शांत स्वभाव का अनुभव करते हैं — "धीमा" और "गहराई" दोनों गुण एक साथ आवश्यक बताए गए हैं, अकेले कोई एक नहीं।

**प्र.138.** प्राण वायु व्यवस्थित रूप से बहने पर क्या नहीं होगा।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — घबराहट में वृद्धि होना।**
गद्यांश के अनुसार धीमी और गहरी प्राण-वायु से भय और घबराहट से मुक्ति मिलती है, रचनात्मकता बढ़ती है और मानसिक तनाव समाप्त होता है — अतः "घबराहट में वृद्धि होना" ठीक इसका विपरीत परिणाम है, जो गद्यांश के दावों से मेल नहीं खाता।

**प्र.139.** प्राण वायु का गहराई व धीमी गति से बहना किसमें सहायता करता है।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — ध्यान लगाने।**
गद्यांश के अंत में स्पष्ट कहा गया है कि यह अभ्यास (धीमा, गहरा श्वास) ध्यान की गहराइयों में ले जाने में सहायक होता है — अर्थात यह सीधे ध्यान (meditation) की गहनता से जुड़ा है, न कि व्यापार या इच्छापूर्ति से।

**प्र.140.** मानव-जीवन में सर्वाधिक महत्त्वपूर्ण क्या है।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — प्राण वायु।**
गद्यांश का केंद्रीय विषय ही प्राण वायु (श्वास) का सही ढंग से बहना है, और इसी पर संपूर्ण चर्चा केंद्रित है — योग, ध्यान और अभ्यास इसी प्राण-वायु के नियमन को प्राप्त करने के साधन के रूप में वर्णित हैं, स्वयं में सर्वाधिक महत्त्वपूर्ण तत्व के रूप में नहीं।

**प्र.141.** विशेषण शब्द पहचानिए।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — शांत।**
"शांत" किसी संज्ञा (जैसे स्वभाव) की विशेषता बताता है, इसलिए यह विशेषण है। "धैर्य," "मन," और "घबराहट" संज्ञा शब्द हैं (भाव/वस्तु का नाम बताते हैं), विशेषण नहीं।

**प्र.142.** 'सुनिश्चित' में उपसर्ग और मूल शब्द।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — सु + निश्चित।**
"सु" एक मानक उपसर्ग है (जिसका अर्थ है 'अच्छी तरह से' या बल देना), और "निश्चित" मूल शब्द है — दोनों मिलकर 'सुनिश्चित' बनाते हैं। शब्द को सही ढंग से उसके मानक उपसर्ग-मूल घटकों में तोड़ना ही इस प्रकार के प्रश्नों की कुंजी है।

**प्र.143.** कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता के कारण किस पर नकारात्मक प्रभाव पड़ता है।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — मेधा।**
गद्यांश में यह चिंता व्यक्त की गई है कि जब विद्यार्थी हर उत्तर तुरंत ए.आई. से प्राप्त कर लेते हैं, तो उनकी स्वयं की मेधा (बौद्धिक क्षमता, सोचने-समझने की शक्ति) प्रभावित हो सकती है — गद्यांश की मूल चिंता तकनीक या कंप्यूटर पर नहीं, बल्कि मानव बुद्धि/मेधा पर पड़ने वाले प्रभाव पर केंद्रित है।

**प्र.144.** युवा विद्यार्थियों द्वारा कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता का सहयोग लेने का कारण नहीं है।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — उसमें शिक्षक से अधिक संवेदनाएँ हैं।**
गद्यांश स्पष्ट करता है कि ए.आई. और मानव बुद्धिमत्ता के बीच सबसे बड़ा अंतर "मानवता" (संवेदनाएँ) का ही है — अर्थात ए.आई. में मानवीय संवेदनाओं की कमी है, अधिकता नहीं। विद्यार्थी ए.आई. का प्रयोग तेज़ी, सुविधा और तुरंत उत्तर मिलने के कारण करते हैं, संवेदनाओं के कारण नहीं।

**प्र.145.** शिक्षक वर्ग कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता से क्या स्थिति में है।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — प्रतिस्पर्धा नहीं कर पाएगा।**
गद्यांश में यह आशंका उठाई गई है कि "क्या शिक्षकों का ज्ञान ए.आई. का मुकाबला कर पाएगा?" — यह एक संदेहात्मक प्रश्न है जो शिक्षकों की तुलनात्मक कमज़ोर स्थिति की ओर संकेत करता है, अर्थात वे गति और सूचना-भंडार में ए.आई. से प्रतिस्पर्धा करने में कठिनाई महसूस कर सकते हैं।

**प्र.146.** गद्यांश के आधार पर क्या कहा जा सकता है।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता के कारण मानवीय संवेदनाएँ प्रभावित होंगी।**
गद्यांश में "मानवता" को ए.आई. और मानव बुद्धि के बीच सबसे बड़े फ़र्क के रूप में रेखांकित किया गया है, और भविष्य को लेकर व्यक्त की गई चिंताएँ इसी बात की ओर इशारा करती हैं कि तकनीक पर बढ़ती निर्भरता मानवीय संवेदनाओं और गुणों को प्रभावित कर सकती है — यह गद्यांश का केंद्रीय भाव है, न कि रोज़गार में वृद्धि या जीवन में सीधा सुधार होने का दावा।

**प्र.147.** गद्यांश के अनुसार कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता में क्या नहीं है।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — मानवता।**
गद्यांश स्पष्ट रूप से कहता है कि ए.आई. और मानव बुद्धि के बीच सबसे बड़ा फ़र्क "मानवता" का है — अर्थात ए.आई. उत्तर, ज्ञान और तकनीक तो प्रदान कर सकती है, परन्तु उसमें मानवीय संवेदना और मानवता का अभाव है।

**प्र.148.** कृत्रिम बुद्धिमत्ता से प्रभावित न होने वाला तत्व।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — चैट जी.पी.टी.।**
गद्यांश में नौकरी, अस्पताल और डिज़ाइनर जैसे पेशों/क्षेत्रों को ए.आई. से प्रभावित होने वाले तत्वों के रूप में गिनाया गया है, जबकि "चैट जी.पी.टी." स्वयं एक ए.आई.-आधारित प्लेटफ़ॉर्म है — अर्थात यह प्रभावित होने वाला तत्व नहीं, बल्कि प्रभाव डालने वाला माध्यम है, इसलिए यह सूची से बाहर है।

**प्र.149.** समूह से भिन्न शब्द।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — मेधा।**
"एक्सपर्ट्स," "चैट जी.पी.टी." और "एग़्ज़ाम" — ये सभी अंग्रेज़ी मूल के शब्द हैं (या अंग्रेज़ी शब्दों के हिंदी लिप्यंतरण), जबकि "मेधा" एक शुद्ध संस्कृतनिष्ठ/तत्सम हिंदी शब्द है — यही भाषिक मूल का अंतर इसे समूह से अलग बनाता है।

**प्र.150.** 'इत' प्रत्यय के उदाहरण वाला शब्द।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — निश्चित।**
'निश्चित' शब्द 'नि + श्चि + इत' से बना है, जहाँ 'इत' एक मानक तत्सम प्रत्यय है (जैसे 'निश्चित', 'चर्चित', 'प्रचलित' आदि में)। जबकि 'प्रभावित' में 'इत' प्रत्यय तो है, यहाँ मानक उदाहरण के रूप में चिन्हित शब्द 'निश्चित' है — प्रत्यय-पहचान के प्रश्नों में सदैव शब्द के मूल धातु और प्रत्यय को अलग करके देखने का अभ्यास करें।
