# July 2024 — Answers and Explanations

## Part I: Child Development and Pedagogy

**Q1.** How progressive education perceives children.
**Correct answer: (3) — Knowledgeable and meaning-makers**
Progressive education, drawing on constructivist thought, sees children as active sense-makers who bring prior knowledge and construct meaning from experience, not as blank slates waiting to be filled. This is why the wording "innocent," "compliant," or "vulnerable" is rejected — those framings treat children as passive recipients rather than agents of their own learning.

**Q2.** When feedback is most effective.
**Correct answer: (2) — It occurs during as well as after the learning activity**
Effective feedback is timely and continuous — it helps a learner adjust while the task is still in progress, not just after it is over. Feedback that only ranks students, focuses solely on deficits, or arrives once a year at exam time cannot guide ongoing improvement.

**Q3.** Assertion-Reason on gender expectations in adolescence.
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true but (R) is false**
It is true that adolescents face intensified pressure to conform to masculine/feminine norms, but gender roles are not biologically determined — they are socially and culturally constructed. Confusing "sex" (biological) with "gender" (social) is a very common misconception this question is testing.

**Q4.** Source of Sunita's exceptional musical ability.
**Correct answer: (3) — heredity and environment**
Sunita has musically accomplished parents (a hereditary/genetic contribution) and also receives dedicated voice training (an environmental/experiential contribution) — her ability reflects the interaction of both, illustrating the nature-nurture interplay rather than either factor alone.

**Q5.** Meaning of "sensitive periods" in development.
**Correct answer: (3) — environmental factors**
Sensitive periods are windows during which specific environmental inputs and experiences have an especially strong influence on a particular aspect of development; missing that input during the window makes later development harder, though not always impossible.

**Q6.** Teacher practice that builds critical thinking.
**Correct answer: (3) — give questions that involve inferences and explanations**
Critical thinking grows when students must reason beyond recall — inferring, explaining, and justifying — rather than answering single-response factual questions. Discouraging questions or avoiding analytical demands does the opposite of building this skill.

**Q7.** Intelligence being promoted by classifying and analysing objects in the environment.
**Correct answer: (3) — Naturalistic Intelligence**
Gardner's naturalistic intelligence involves recognising, categorising, and understanding patterns in the natural world — exactly the skill invoked when a teacher asks students to classify objects/organisms and examine their own classification schemes.

**Q8.** Effect of mastery goals on motivation and view of ability.
**Correct answer: (2) — enhanced intrinsic motivation and belief in the incremental view of ability**
Mastery goals focus a learner on understanding and improving rather than on outperforming others, which nurtures intrinsic motivation and the incremental (growth) belief that ability can be developed through effort — the opposite of the fixed "entity" view.

**Q9.** Best way for Rama to help a struggling student, per Vygotsky.
**Correct answer: (4) — Work through the problem with the student, providing guidance and feedback as needed while involving other group members**
This is scaffolding within the Zone of Proximal Development: the teacher provides just enough support so the student can accomplish what she cannot yet do alone, gradually reducing help as competence grows — simply giving the answer or removing the student from the challenge would bypass this productive struggle.

**Q10.** Assertion-Reason on debates/collaboration and knowledge production.
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true but (R) is false**
Encouraging debate and collaboration is sound pedagogy (A is true), but knowledge production is an active, social process — group engagement supports rather than hinders meaningful learning, so the reason's claim is false.

**Q11.** Term for change and stability in emotions, personality, and social relationships together.
**Correct answer: (3) — Psychosocial development**
Psychosocial development is the umbrella term covering how emotional life, sense of self (personality), and social relationships evolve together over time, as in Erikson's stage theory — it is broader than emotional or personality development taken alone.

**Q12.** Features of culturally responsive teaching.
**Correct answer: (3) — (i), (ii), (iii) — Intentional, Adaptive, Differentiated**
Culturally responsive teaching is deliberately planned (intentional), flexible to student backgrounds (adaptive), and tailored to diverse needs (differentiated) — it is explicitly the opposite of "standardized," so (iv) is excluded.

**Q13.** Assertion-Reason on assessing only finished products and sequential learning.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are false**
Modern pedagogy values assessing the process of learning, not just finished outcomes, and learning is not rigidly linear/hierarchical for all learners — both claims misrepresent how learning actually works.

**Q14.** Vygotsky's view of the relationship between language and thought.
**Correct answer: (4) — Language and thought are complex interrelated processes of development**
For Vygotsky, language and thought begin somewhat separately in infancy but merge and mutually shape each other as the child develops — neither is simply a one-way product of the other, and they are not independent.

**Q15.** Correctly matched assessment terms.
**Correct answer: (2) — Norm-referenced assessment: compares performance to that of peers**
Norm-referenced assessment ranks a student's performance relative to a peer group, unlike criterion-referenced assessment (measured against a fixed standard) — the other three pairings in this question mismatch the terms with their actual definitions.

**Q16.** What hinders participation of visually impaired students.
**Correct answer: (2) — printed textbooks and notes**
Standard printed text is inaccessible to students with visual impairment, whereas electronic/audio transcriptions, raised-line drawings, and tactile models are precisely the adaptive materials that support their participation.

**Q17.** Kohlberg's method for studying moral development.
**Correct answer: (2) — Posing moral dilemmas to children and analyzing responses**
Kohlberg's classic method presented children with hypothetical moral dilemmas (like the Heinz dilemma) and analysed the reasoning behind their answers, not just the answer itself, to identify stages of moral reasoning.

**Q18.** What constructivist teaching leads to.
**Correct answer: (1) — discovery**
Constructivism holds that learners actively build understanding through exploration and experience, which is discovery learning — the opposite of rote or passively receptive learning.

**Q19.** How to teach reading to students with dyslexia.
**Correct answer: (1) — Through systematic phonological training**
Dyslexia involves difficulty processing phonemes (sound-symbol relationships), so structured, systematic phonics/phonological instruction is the evidence-based intervention — piling on harder or longer texts would only increase frustration.

**Q20.** Implication of Piaget's theory of cognitive development.
**Correct answer: (3) — Curriculum should be designed keeping children's age-group and stage in mind**
Piaget showed that children's thinking changes qualitatively across distinct stages, so curriculum and teaching methods must match the learner's current stage rather than assume one-size-fits-all instruction based on rewards/punishments or outcome alone.

**Q21.** Strategy that fosters metacognitive skills.
**Correct answer: (2) — Encouraging students to reflect on their own learning and thinking processes**
Metacognition is "thinking about one's own thinking" — it is built by explicitly prompting students to reflect on how they learn and solve problems, not by grading, spoon-feeding notes, or assigning rote memorisation.

**Q22.** Correct statement about Autism.
**Correct answer: (2) — Autism is a neuro-developmental disorder**
Autism is a lifelong neuro-developmental condition affecting brain development and functioning; it is not caused solely by environment, does not universally include a special "exceptional skill," and is not "cured" — these are common myths the question is testing against.

**Q23.** Key commonality between Piaget and Vygotsky.
**Correct answer: (3) — Involvement and engagement of children in their own learning**
Despite their differences (Piaget emphasised individual stage-based construction, Vygotsky emphasised social/cultural mediation), both agreed that children are active participants who construct knowledge through engagement, not passive recipients.

**Q24.** Assertion-Reason on respecting individual differences vs. avoiding collaboration.
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true but (R) is false**
Respecting individual differences and adapting teaching is good practice (A true), but avoiding collaboration and group work for diverse learners is poor advice — collaborative structures typically help diverse learners, so R is false.

**Q25.** Learning strategy where children teach others what they learned.
**Correct answer: (4) — Reciprocal teaching**
Reciprocal teaching is a structured strategy where students take turns leading discussion and explaining content to peers, reinforcing their own understanding by teaching it — distinct from rote or one-way expository teaching.

**Q26.** Assertion-Reason on emotional display rules in Asian cultures.
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true but (R) is false**
Cultural display rules genuinely shape which emotions (like anger vs. shame) are encouraged or discouraged (A true), but emotions are not irrelevant to learning — they are deeply tied to motivation, memory, and engagement, so R is false.

**Q27.** How Piaget said children differ from adults.
**Correct answer: (3) — Children's thinking is qualitatively different from that of adults**
Piaget's central insight was that children do not just know "less" than adults (a quantitative difference); at each stage they reason in fundamentally different ways (e.g., pre-operational vs. formal operational thought) — a qualitative difference.

**Q28.** Question that promotes analytical thinking.
**Correct answer: (4) — What are the various causes of increase of population in India?**
This question requires identifying multiple causes and reasoning about relationships between factors, unlike the other options which only demand recall of a single fact or figure.

**Q29.** What inclusive classrooms are about.
**Correct answer: (3) — Are meant to offer differentiated instructions as per individual differences**
Inclusion means adapting instruction to the varied needs, paces, and abilities of all learners in one classroom — it does not assume identical learning rates, is not a separate program only "for special children," and does not aim to erase differences.

**Q30.** Assertion-Reason on experimentation and counter-examples for misconceptions.
**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A)**
Giving children chances to experiment and compare examples/non-examples (A) works precisely because presenting counter-examples is an effective, constructive way to challenge and correct misconceptions (R directly explains A).

## Part II: Mathematics and Science

**Q31.** Area of quadrilateral ABCD given perimeter and two right angles.
**Correct answer: (1) — 660**
With ∠A = ∠B = 90°, AB is perpendicular to both AD and BC, making ABCD a right trapezium. AB = 120 − 48 − 17 − 40 = 15 cm; dropping a perpendicular from D onto BC creates a right triangle with legs 15 and (48−40)=8 and hypotenuse CD=17 (since 8²+15²=17², confirming AB=15). Area = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height = ½ × (40+48) × 15 = 660 cm². The general lesson: whenever two right angles sit on the same side, treat that side as the trapezium's height.

**Q32.** Least appropriate statement about mathematical proofs.
**Correct answer: (4) — Proofs are built on intuitive knowledge and not reasoning**
Mathematical proof is fundamentally about logical reasoning and deduction, not mere intuition — proofs explain *why* a result is true, reveal structural connections, and let students validate their own thinking, which is the opposite of what this option claims.

**Q33.** Value of (7y+5) in a parallelogram with given diagonal segments.
**Correct answer: (3) — 26**
In any parallelogram, diagonals bisect each other, so OP = OR and OQ = OS. From OP=OR: x+7=20 → x=13. From OQ=OS: x+y=16 → 13+y=16 → y=3. So 7y+5 = 21+5 = 26. Remembering "diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other" instantly turns this into simple linear equations.

**Q34.** Evaluating a mixed arithmetic expression using BODMAS.
**Correct answer: (2) — 5**
Following order of operations: 52×3÷4 = 156÷4 = 39; √441÷7×3 = 21÷7×3 = 3×3 = 9; and 32÷8×12 = 4×12 = 48. So the expression becomes 39 + 9 + 5 − 48 = 53 − 48 = 5. The key skill is doing division/multiplication left-to-right in the order they appear, not multiplication before division blindly.

**Q35.** Equation to find Varun's present age x.
**Correct answer: (1) — ⅚(x−5) − ⅗(x−8) = 6**
If Varun's present age is x, Arun's is x+3. Eight years ago, Varun was (x−8) and Arun was (x+3−8) = (x−5). The condition "⅚ of Arun's age exceeded ⅗ of Varun's age by 6" translates directly to ⅚(x−5) − ⅗(x−8) = 6. The trick is carefully assigning "8 years ago" to each person's *current* age expression before applying the fractions.

**Q36.** Domain of learning dealing with attitudes and values.
**Correct answer: (3) — Affective domain**
In Bloom's taxonomy, the affective domain covers attitudes, values, interests, and emotional responses, distinct from the cognitive domain (knowledge/thinking) and psychomotor domain (physical skills).

**Q37.** Effect on cylinder volume when radius is halved and height increases by 3 cm.
**Correct answer: (4) — one-third the original volume**
Original volume = π(6)²(9) = 324π. New radius = 3, new height = 12, so new volume = π(3)²(12) = 108π. Since 108π is exactly one-third of 324π, the new volume is one-third the original — a reminder that radius affects volume quadratically, so halving it shrinks volume far more than the height increase can compensate.

**Q38.** Ascending order of Van Hiele levels of geometric thinking.
**Correct answer: (2) — (d), (b), (a), (c)**
Van Hiele's theory orders geometric thinking from recognising shapes holistically (d: identification as they are), to grouping shapes by shared features (b: classification), to naming specific properties (a: identification of properties), and finally to reasoning about relationships between those properties (c) — each level builds on the reasoning ability of the one before it.

**Q39.** Difference between Mathematics and English marks from a pie chart.
**Correct answer: (1) — 60**
Total marks (540) correspond to 360°, so each degree represents 540/360 = 1.5 marks. The angle difference between Mathematics (90°) and English (50°) is 40°, giving a marks difference of 40 × 1.5 = 60. This "marks per degree" conversion is the standard technique for any pie-chart marks question.

**Q40.** Topic NOT included under Number System at upper primary, per NCERT.
**Correct answer: (1) — Complex Numbers**
Complex numbers are introduced only at the senior secondary level; fractions, exponents/powers, and square/cube roots are all part of the upper primary number system curriculum as per NCERT.

**Q41.** Statement NOT correct about rubrics.
**Correct answer: (4) — Rubrics make evaluation more subjective**
Rubrics are designed to do the opposite — by spelling out a fixed scale and clear descriptors for each performance level, they make evaluation more objective and consistent, not more subjective.

**Q42.** Value of (2F+3E−4x) using Euler's formula.
**Correct answer: (2) — 19**
Euler's formula for polyhedra states V − E + F = 2. With F=7, E=15: x = V = 2 − 7 + 15 = 10. So 2F+3E−4x = 14+45−40 = 19. Euler's formula is the go-to tool whenever a question gives any two of faces/edges/vertices and asks for the third.

**Q43.** Value of (p+q) from a quadratic factorisation.
**Correct answer: (4) — −5**
Let u = 2x+5y. Then u² − 5u − 14 factors as (u−7)(u+2), since −7 and 2 multiply to −14 and add to −5. So p and q are −7 and 2 (in either order), giving p+q = −5. This is the same "find two numbers whose product and sum match the constant and coefficient" technique used in any quadratic factorisation.

**Q44.** Value of (4x+6°) from co-interior angles.
**Correct answer: (1) — 154°**
When a transversal cuts two parallel lines, interior angles on the same side are co-interior (supplementary), summing to 180°: (2x+15)+(3x−20)=180 → 5x−5=180 → x=37. So 4x+6 = 148+6 = 154°. Recognising "co-interior angles are supplementary" (not equal) is the crucial rule tested here.

**Q45.** Sum of the largest and smallest of four given fractions.
**Correct answer: (4) — 2 39/40**
Converting to decimals for comparison: 6/7≈0.86, 2⅗=2.6, ⅜=0.375, and 2 8/15≈2.53. The largest is 2⅗ (13/5) and the smallest is ⅜ (3/8). Their sum: 13/5 + 3/8 = 104/40 + 15/40 = 119/40 = 2 39/40. Converting mixed numbers to a common form before comparing avoids errors when fractions "look" similar in size.

**Q46.** What does NOT capture the meaning of assessment.
**Correct answer: (2) — It helps to label the students**
Genuine assessment is meant to gather information, diagnose learning needs, and support growth — using it to permanently "label" students (e.g., as slow/bright) is a misuse of assessment, not one of its intended purposes.

**Q47.** NOT an indicator of creativity in Mathematics.
**Correct answer: (1) — Students use convergent thinking in different contexts**
Mathematical creativity is marked by divergent thinking — flexibility, multiple strategies, and posing new questions — whereas convergent thinking (narrowing down to one correct, expected answer) is the opposite trait and does not indicate creativity.

**Q48.** Value of 3x+2 where x is the least number to make 955 a perfect square.
**Correct answer: (1) — 20**
The nearest perfect square above 955 is 31² = 961, so x = 961−955 = 6. Then 3x+2 = 18+2 = 20. Finding the nearest square roots just above/below the target number is the standard approach for such "least number to be added/subtracted" problems.

**Q49.** Natural numbers between 1 and 500 divisible by 3, 5, and 7.
**Correct answer: (2) — 4**
A number divisible by 3, 5, and 7 must be divisible by their LCM, which is 105. Multiples of 105 up to 500 are 105, 210, 315, and 420 — that's 4 numbers (525 exceeds 500).

**Q50.** Least important process in mathematics per NCF 2005.
**Correct answer: (3) — Finding short-cuts in mathematics**
NCF 2005 emphasises problem solving, understanding when/how to apply a technique, and generalising formulas as core mathematical processes; hunting for shortcuts is a surface-level skill, not a foundational process the framework prioritises.

**Q51.** Value of (3x+25°) where M is equidistant from all three triangle vertices.
**Correct answer: (2) — 85°**
Since AM=BM=CM, M is the circumcentre of triangle ABC and, because M lies on side AB itself, AB must be the diameter of the circumcircle — meaning angle C = 90°. So x + 70° + 90° = 180° → x = 20°, and 3x+25° = 60°+25° = 85°. The key insight (M on the hypotenuse, equidistant from all vertices) signals a right angle at C — a classic circle-geometry shortcut.

**Q52.** Simple interest at 10% for 2½ years, given compound interest data.
**Correct answer: (3) — ₹675**
From CI: A = P(1.1)² = 3267, so 1.21P = 3267 → P = ₹2,700. Simple interest for 2.5 years at 10%: SI = P×R×T/100 = 2700×10×2.5/100 = ₹675. Always extract the principal from the compound-interest condition first, then apply the simple-interest formula separately — the two use different growth rules.

**Q53.** Value of angle ADC where CD bisects angle ACB.
**Correct answer: (4) — 100°**
Angle C = 180°−50°−70° = 60°, so the bisector splits it into two 30° angles, meaning angle ACD = 30°. In triangle ACD, angle ADC = 180°−50°−30° = 100°. Whenever a bisector is given, immediately halve the relevant angle before applying the triangle angle-sum rule.

**Q54.** Difference between mean and range of the given marks data.
**Correct answer: (3) — 5.5**
Sum of the twelve values = 366, so mean = 366/12 = 30.5. Range = max−min = 42−17 = 25. Difference = 30.5−25 = 5.5. This question rewards careful, error-free addition over any special formula.

**Q55.** Trader's overall profit on 12 fans.
**Correct answer: (2) — ₹3,000**
Cost per fan = 24000/12 = ₹2,000. Marked price = 125% of 2000 = ₹2,500. Selling price after 10% discount = 2500×0.9 = ₹2,250. Profit per fan = 2250−2000 = ₹250, so total profit = 250×12 = ₹3,000. Working "per unit" first, then scaling up, avoids arithmetic slips in multi-step profit/loss problems.

**Q56.** Simplifying [(a−b+c)² − (a−b−c)²] ÷ (b−a).
**Correct answer: (3) — −4c**
Using the identity X²−Y² = (X+Y)(X−Y) with X=(a−b+c), Y=(a−b−c): X+Y = 2(a−b) and X−Y = 2c, so the numerator is 4c(a−b). Dividing by (b−a) = −(a−b) gives 4c(a−b)/−(a−b) = −4c. Spotting the difference-of-squares pattern instantly avoids expanding both squared brackets term by term.

**Q57.** Perimeter of a right triangle given area and one leg.
**Correct answer: (1) — 80**
Area = ½ × 30 × (other leg) = 240 → other leg = 16 cm. Hypotenuse = √(30²+16²) = √1156 = 34 cm. Perimeter = 30+16+34 = 80 cm. Recognising 16-30-34 as a scaled-up Pythagorean triple (8-15-17 × 2) makes the hypotenuse calculation quick.

**Q58.** Teaching method where learners measure and tabulate angles to find the angle-sum result themselves.
**Correct answer: (1) — Discovery method**
The teacher does not tell students the rule; instead they gather data through hands-on measurement and arrive at the generalisation themselves — this student-led, experience-first approach defines the discovery method, as opposed to a teacher simply stating (deductive) or explaining (teacher-centred) the result.

**Q59.** Statement that is NOT true.
**Correct answer: (3) — The expression (x²+10x+25) cannot be simplified further**
This is false because x²+10x+25 is a perfect square trinomial that factors neatly as (x+5)² — so it *can* be simplified further, making this the (incorrect) statement the question asks you to identify.

**Q60.** Sum of LCM and HCF given a 3:7 ratio and LCM=630.
**Correct answer: (4) — 660**
Let the numbers be 3k and 7k; since 3 and 7 share no common factor, LCM = 21k = 630 → k = 30, which is also the HCF. So LCM+HCF = 630+30 = 660. Whenever two numbers are given as a ratio in lowest terms, their HCF is exactly the ratio's common multiplier k.

**Q61.** Classifying statements as observation (O) or inference (I).
**Correct answer: (3) — O − C; I − A, B, D**
"Lime water turns milky on passing CO2" (C) is a direct, specific observation from an experiment. "Metal oxides are basic" (A), "air expands on heating" (B), and "NaOH and HCl neutralise each other" (D) are all generalised conclusions drawn from patterns across many observations — i.e., inferences. The distinction to remember: an observation is a single recorded event; an inference is the general rule built from repeated observations.

**Q62.** NOT a feature of alternate conceptions in science.
**Correct answer: (4) — Ideas do not demonstrate cause and effect reasoning**
Alternate (mis)conceptions are usually stable, resistant to change, and built from everyday observable experience — but they typically *do* involve the child's own cause-and-effect reasoning (even if that reasoning is scientifically incorrect), so claiming they lack cause-effect logic is the false statement here.

**Q63.** Assertion-Reason on adding acid to water.
**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A)**
Acid should always be added to water, never the reverse, because dilution of concentrated acid releases a large amount of heat (it is exothermic) — adding water to acid can cause sudden boiling and dangerous spattering, so the heat-release reason directly explains the safety rule.

**Q64.** Assertion-Reason on breathing rate in aquatic vs. land animals.
**Correct answer: (4) — Both (A) and (R) are false**
In reality, aquatic animals breathe *faster* than land animals, not slower, because water holds far less dissolved oxygen than air — so they must process larger volumes of water to extract enough oxygen. Both the assertion and its reasoning misstate the actual biology.

**Q65.** Assertion-Reason on curved ceilings in concert halls.
**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A)**
Concert and cinema hall ceilings are curved so that sound waves striking them get reflected and scattered in many directions rather than bouncing back sharply as an echo — this even distribution of reflected sound is exactly why curved ceilings improve acoustics.

**Q66.** True statements about scientific investigations.
**Correct answer: (2) — (b) and (d)**
Social context genuinely can shape how conclusions from an investigation are interpreted, and there is no single fixed "scientific method" that all investigations must follow. It is false, however, that results are unaffected by procedure, or that data and evidence are identical — data must be interpreted to become evidence.

**Q67.** Assertion-Reason on tungsten bulb filaments.
**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true and (R) is false**
Tungsten is indeed used for bulb filaments (A true) because of its very high melting point, but the reasoning is wrong — tungsten actually has relatively high resistance (which is precisely why it heats up and glows when current passes through it), not low resistance.

**Q68.** Water flow from holes punched at different heights in a bottle.
**Correct answer: (4) — (a) and (d): water from the lowest hole comes out with greatest force and falls at maximum distance**
Water pressure increases with depth, so the hole nearest the bottom experiences the greatest pressure from the water column above it, giving it the greatest exit force/velocity and, in this classic classroom demonstration, the farthest landing distance — a direct, hands-on way to teach that liquid pressure depends on depth.

**Q69.** Nature of the human circulatory system.
**Correct answer: (4) — closed, double circulation**
Human blood always remains within vessels (closed circulation), and it passes through the heart twice per complete cycle — once to the lungs (pulmonary circuit) and once to the rest of the body (systemic circuit) — making it double circulation.

**Q70.** Best strategies to identify students' alternate conceptions about plant/animal adaptations.
**Correct answer: options (2) and (3) both accepted — Portfolio/group discussion/diagnostic questionnaire, and Diagnostic questionnaire/interview/drawings**
CBSE accepted both option sets as correct (erratum code covering options 2 and 3), since diagnostic questionnaires, interviews, drawings, portfolios, and group discussion are all valid, complementary tools for surfacing what students already believe about a topic before teaching it — the common thread across the accepted options is that each elicits the student's own thinking rather than just testing recall.

**Q71.** NOT caused by excessive fertilizer and pesticide use.
**Correct answer: (1) — Soil erosion**
Excess fertilizer/pesticide runoff causes eutrophication (nutrient overload in water bodies), disrupted nitrification, and biomagnification (toxin build-up up the food chain) — but soil erosion is primarily a physical process driven by factors like deforestation, water/wind action, and poor land management, not chemical overuse.

**Q72.** What an odometer measures.
**Correct answer: (1) — distance travelled by the car**
An odometer is a cumulative distance counter; it is the speedometer, not the odometer, that shows instantaneous speed, and neither instrument directly measures average speed or acceleration.

**Q73.** True statement about scientific laws.
**Correct answer: (1) — They describe relationships between observable phenomena**
A scientific law is a concise statement of a consistently observed relationship between variables (like Boyle's law relating pressure and volume) — it describes *what* happens, while a theory explains *why* it happens; laws can, in principle, be refined as evidence accumulates.

**Q74.** Truth of statements on self and cross pollination in bisexual flowers.
**Correct answer: (2) — (a) is false and (b) is true**
Self-pollination is not restricted only to bisexual flowers — it can also occur between different flowers on the same monoecious plant that bears separate male and female (unisexual) flowers, so statement (a) overstates the condition. Bisexual flowers, however, genuinely can undergo both self- and cross-pollination, so (b) is correct.

**Q75.** Activity best suited to develop inductive reasoning.
**Correct answer: (2) — asking students to measure angle of reflection for various cases of angle of incidence in a plane mirror**
Inductive reasoning means arriving at a general rule from specific observed instances — by measuring many individual cases first, students discover the law of reflection themselves, rather than being told the law and asked to verify or apply it (which would be deductive).

**Q76.** Type of twins from two sperms fusing with two eggs.
**Correct answer: (2) — fraternal**
When two separate eggs are fertilised by two separate sperms, each zygote has its own distinct genetic combination, producing fraternal (dizygotic) twins — genetically no more alike than regular siblings — unlike identical twins, who arise from a single fertilised egg splitting in two.

**Q77.** True statements about rainwater harvesting.
**Correct answer: (3) — (a), (b), (d)**
Rainwater harvesting recharges groundwater, reduces local flooding by capturing runoff, and provides water usable for irrigation — it solves drainage problems rather than creating them, which is why (c) is excluded.

**Q78.** Benefits of discussing socio-scientific issues in class.
**Correct answer: (2) — (a), (b), and (c)**
Debating real-world science-related issues builds students' argumentation skills, exposes them to multiple perspectives, and can inspire citizen-science participation; appreciating "society's role in the development of science" (d) is a related but distinct benefit not included in the accepted combination here.

**Q79.** Activity illustrating "science as inquiry."
**Correct answer: (2) — Explore factors which affect the rate of germination in gram seeds**
Science as inquiry means posing a question, designing a way to test variables, and drawing conclusions from evidence — exploring what affects germination rate requires exactly this investigative process, unlike simply classifying, listing, or observing existing categories.

**Q80.** What gender-responsive pedagogy in science involves.
**Correct answer: (3) — (a) and (b)**
Gender-responsive teaching uses gender-sensitive language and actively helps students examine and question stereotypes; deliberately forming gender-based groups works against inclusive, responsive pedagogy rather than supporting it, so it is excluded.

**Q81.** Truth of statements on photosynthesis and respiration as thermal reactions.
**Correct answer: (1) — Both S1 and S2 are true**
Photosynthesis absorbs light energy to build glucose, making it endothermic, while respiration breaks down glucose and releases energy, making it exothermic — these are two of the most fundamental energy-transfer facts in school biology.

**Q82.** Colour changes of indicators in acids and bases.
**Correct answer: (1) — reddish brown, green, colourless, orange**
Turmeric turns reddish-brown in a base (A); China rose turns green in a base (B); phenolphthalein stays colourless in an acid (C); methyl orange turns orange/red in an acid (D). Memorising this indicator-colour table is essential groundwork for the acids-bases-salts chapter.

**Q83.** What flame colour depends on.
**Correct answer: (3) — (a) and (b)**
Flame colour depends on the chemical nature of the burning substance (different elements/compounds burn with characteristic colours) and on oxygen supply (a well-oxygenated flame burns blue/hotter, an oxygen-starved flame burns yellow/sooty) — ambient surrounding temperature is not a determining factor.

**Q84.** Factors responsible for generation and flow of winds.
**Correct answer: (3) — (a) and (b)**
Winds arise from uneven heating of the Earth's surface and uneven heating of the oceans, which create pressure differences that air moves to equalise — Earth-Sun distance and gravity are essentially constant and don't drive day-to-day wind patterns.

**Q85.** Chemical formula of a compound from elements with atomic numbers 13 and 17.
**Correct answer: (2) — AB₃**
Atomic number 13 is aluminium, which loses 3 electrons to form Al³⁺; atomic number 17 is chlorine, which gains 1 electron to form Cl⁻. Balancing charges needs three Cl⁻ ions per Al³⁺ ion, giving the formula AlCl₃, i.e., of the form AB₃.

**Q86.** Statement NOT true about heat conduction.
**Correct answer: (3) — Conduction does not take place in gases**
Conduction does occur in gases too, just far less efficiently than in solids, since gas molecules are widely spaced and collide less frequently — this is why gases are generally poor conductors, not non-conductors.

**Q87.** Comparing densities of liquids A and B from floating behaviour.
**Correct answer: (3) — Density of liquid B is greater than density of liquid A**
An object needs to displace less of its own volume to float in a denser liquid. In liquid B, only ¼ of the object is submerged (¾ stays outside), meaning very little volume was needed to displace an equal weight of liquid — indicating liquid B is denser than liquid A, where ¾ of the object had to submerge to float.

**Q88.** Incorrect observation about potassium reacting with water.
**Correct answer: (2) — It is an endothermic reaction**
Potassium reacts violently and explosively with cold water, releasing (not absorbing) a large amount of heat — enough to ignite the liberated hydrogen gas with a pop sound — so calling this reaction endothermic is the incorrect statement.

**Q89.** Purposes of divergent questions in a science classroom.
**Correct answer: (1) — (a), (b), (d), and (e)**
Open-ended, divergent questions allow multiple valid responses, which helps a teacher promote critical thinking, notice differences in how individual students reason (including spotting advanced/gifted thinkers), and uncover students' alternate conceptions — this is different from convergent questions, which target one expected "correct" answer.

**Q90.** Identifying mirror types from image characteristics.
**Correct answer: (3) — A−Plane mirror, B−Concave, C−Concave, D−Convex**
A virtual, erect, same-size image always comes from a plane mirror (A). A real, inverted, same-size image forms when an object is at the centre of curvature of a concave mirror (B). A real, inverted, enlarged image forms when an object is between the focus and centre of curvature of a concave mirror (C). A virtual, erect, diminished image is the signature of a convex mirror (D) — matching image type and size to mirror type this way is the fastest route to such questions.

**Q108.** Term for intonation, tone, stress, and rhythm.

**Q112.** School of thought viewing language learning as habit formation.
**Correct answer: (2) — Behaviourist**
Behaviourist theory (associated with Skinner) holds that language, like other behaviours, is learned through repetition, reinforcement, and habit formation — in contrast to cognitivist or interactionist views that emphasise internal mental processes or social interaction.

**Q113.** Term for the time and place of a story.

**Q116.** NOT an important factor in planning a listening lesson.

**Q118.** Skills known as receptive.

Receptive skills involve taking in and processing language input (through ears or eyes) — listening and reading — as opposed to productive skills (speaking and writing), which involve generating language output.

**Q119.** What listening involves.

Effective listening is an active, meaning-making process — recognising sound patterns and inferring meaning from them — rather than mechanically decoding sounds "as they are," which describes a shallower, bottom-up-only view of listening.

**Q120.** Matching language teaching methods to their features.

**Q138.** प्रतिदिन काम में आने वाली सामग्री का शिक्षण में प्रयोग।
**सही उत्तर: (1) और (3) दोनों स्वीकृत — वास्तविक वस्तुएँ (रीयलिया) / शिक्षण सामग्री**
उत्तर कुंजी में इस प्रश्न पर एक त्रुटि-सुधार कोड लागू है जिसके अनुसार विकल्प 1 और 3 दोनों को सही माना गया। तकनीकी रूप से सटीक शब्द 'रीयलिया' है — रोज़मर्रा की असली वस्तुओं (जैसे टिकट, मेनू कार्ड) का शिक्षण में प्रयोग भाषा को वास्तविक जीवन से जोड़ता है, इसीलिए 'शिक्षण सामग्री' को भी स्वीकार्य माना गया।

**Q139.** त्रिभाषा सूत्र किसे समुन्नत करने के लिए कहता है।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — मातृभाषा आधारित बहुभाषावाद**
भारत की भाषा नीति (त्रिभाषा सूत्र और NEP) मातृभाषा को आधार बनाकर बहुभाषिक दक्षता विकसित करने पर बल देती है, न कि किसी एक भाषा या द्विभाषिक शिक्षा पर — यह बच्चे की अपनी भाषा से सीखना शुरू करने के सिद्धांत पर टिका है।

**Q140.** टॉप-डाउन (शीर्ष अधोमुखी) प्रक्रिया के बारे में क्या सही नहीं है।
**सही उत्तर: (4) — सुनने वाले को प्रत्येक शब्द का अर्थ सुनने की आवश्यकता है**
टॉप-डाउन प्रक्रिया में श्रोता पूर्व ज्ञान और अनुमान के आधार पर अर्थ ग्रहण करता है, प्रत्येक शब्द को अलग-अलग डिकोड करने की आवश्यकता नहीं होती — शब्द-दर-शब्द अर्थ ग्रहण करना तो बॉटम-अप प्रक्रिया की विशेषता है, इसलिए यही कथन गलत है।

**Q141.** अन्तर्राष्ट्रीय मातृभाषा दिवस कब मनाया जाता है।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — इक्कीस फरवरी**
यूनेस्को द्वारा हर वर्ष 21 फरवरी को अंतर्राष्ट्रीय मातृभाषा दिवस मनाया जाता है, ताकि भाषाई और सांस्कृतिक विविधता तथा बहुभाषावाद के प्रति जागरूकता बढ़ाई जा सके।

**Q142.** मित्र-सहयोगी को लिखे जाने वाले ईमेल का प्रकार।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — अनौपचारिक**
जब प्राप्तकर्ता व्यक्तिगत रूप से परिचित और मित्र भी हो, तो पत्र-व्यवहार की भाषा और शैली अनौपचारिक (informal) होती है, भले ही वह कार्यस्थल का सहयोगी क्यों न हो — औपचारिकता का स्तर संबंध की प्रकृति से तय होता है, पद से नहीं।

**Q143.** समूची शिक्षण-अधिगम प्रक्रिया के दौरान स्पष्टता हेतु किया जाने वाला आकलन।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — अधिगम के लिए आकलन**
'अधिगम के लिए आकलन' (Assessment for Learning) शिक्षण प्रक्रिया के दौरान निरंतर किया जाता है ताकि शिक्षक और विद्यार्थी दोनों को यह स्पष्ट हो सके कि अधिगम किस दिशा में जा रहा है, जबकि 'अधिगम का आकलन' अंत में परिणाम मापने पर केंद्रित होता है।

**Q144.** ऑडियो सुनाकर वर्कशीट भरवाने की गतिविधि का उद्देश्य।
**सही उत्तर: (2) और (3) दोनों स्वीकृत — विद्यार्थियों को कार्य में संलग्न किया जाए / विचार बिन्दुओं को सुनकर विश्लेषण किया जाए**
इस प्रश्न पर भी उत्तर कुंजी में त्रुटि-सुधार कोड लागू है जिसमें विकल्प 2 और 3 दोनों स्वीकृत हैं। सुनने के बाद कार्यपत्रक भरना विद्यार्थियों को सक्रिय रूप से कार्य में जोड़ता है और साथ ही उन्हें सुनी गई सामग्री के विचार-बिंदुओं का विश्लेषण करने पर भी प्रेरित करता है — केवल कंठस्थ करने या निष्क्रिय श्रवण पर आधारित उद्देश्य यहाँ लागू नहीं होता।

**Q145.** भाषा अधिगम में गलतियों के बारे में क्या सही नहीं है।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — गलतियाँ भाषा अधिगम प्रक्रिया में अवरोध पैदा करती हैं**
आधुनिक भाषा-शिक्षण दृष्टिकोण गलतियों को सीखने की प्रक्रिया का स्वाभाविक और सहायक हिस्सा मानता है, अवरोध नहीं — गलतियाँ बताती हैं कि शिक्षार्थी सक्रिय रूप से भाषा के नियमों को समझने और आज़माने की कोशिश कर रहा है, इसलिए इन्हें बाधा कहना गलत है।

**Q146.** भाषा शिक्षा में रचनात्मकता किसे कहते हैं।
**सही उत्तर: (1) — खुले अन्त वाले कक्षायी प्रदत्त कार्य जिनकी भिन्न-भिन्न व्याख्याएँ हो सकती हैं और भिन्न-भिन्न उत्तर निकल सकते हैं**
रचनात्मकता का मूल भाव यह है कि कार्य का कोई एक निश्चित सही उत्तर न हो, बल्कि विद्यार्थी अपनी समझ और कल्पना के अनुसार अलग-अलग वैध उत्तर या व्याख्याएँ प्रस्तुत कर सकें — यही खुले अंत वाले (open-ended) कार्यों की पहचान है।

**Q147.** समृद्ध भाषायी परिवेश और गलतियों को अधिगम का साक्ष्य मानने की अनुशंसा किसके द्वारा की गई है।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — संज्ञानात्मकवादी**
यह दृष्टिकोण संज्ञानात्मकवादी (कॉग्निटिविस्ट) विचारधारा से जुड़ा है, जो भाषा अर्जन को शिक्षार्थी की सक्रिय मानसिक प्रक्रिया मानती है — गलतियाँ यहाँ शिक्षार्थी द्वारा भाषा-नियमों की परिकल्पना बनाने और परखने (hypothesis testing) का प्रमाण मानी जाती हैं, न कि किसी दोष का संकेत।

**Q148.** पठन वैकल्य और लेखन वैकल्य से संबंधित कथनों की सत्यता।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — (a) और (b) दोनों सही हैं**
'डिस्लेक्सिया' (पठन वैकल्य) पढ़ने से संबंधित विकार है और 'डिसग्राफिया' (लेखन वैकल्य) लिखने से संबंधित विकार है — दोनों नाम अपने-अपने विकार के प्रकार से सीधे मेल खाते हैं, इन्हें आपस में बदल देना ही सामान्य भ्रम है जिससे यह प्रश्न बचाता है।

**Q149.** रीता द्वारा दृश्य उपकरण और भाषा खेल के प्रयोग से किस सिद्धांत का अनुपालन।
**सही उत्तर: (3) — अभिप्रेरणा और रुचि का सिद्धान्त**
दृश्य साधन और भाषा-खेल कक्षा को रोचक और सहभागितापूर्ण बनाते हैं, जिससे विद्यार्थियों की स्वाभाविक रुचि जागृत होती है और वे प्रेरित होकर सीखते हैं — यही अभिप्रेरणा और रुचि के सिद्धांत का प्रयोग है, न कि केवल अभ्यास-आदत निर्माण का।

**Q150.** पशु पात्रों वाली और नैतिक मूल्य उभारने वाली लघुकथा।
**सही उत्तर: (2) — जंतुकथा (फेबल)**
जंतुकथा (फेबल) एक लघु कथा-विधा है जिसमें पशु-पक्षी मुख्य पात्र होते हैं और कथा के अंत में सामान्यतः कोई नैतिक शिक्षा या मूल्य निहित होता है — पंचतंत्र की कहानियाँ इसका सुपरिचित उदाहरण हैं।
