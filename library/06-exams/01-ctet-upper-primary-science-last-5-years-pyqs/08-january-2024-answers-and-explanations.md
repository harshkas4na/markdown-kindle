# January 2024 — Answers and Explanations

## Part I: Child Development and Pedagogy

**Q1.** Vygotsky and the teacher's role.

**Correct answer: (2) — To guide and support the student's learning through scaffolding.**

Vygotsky saw the teacher (or any "more knowledgeable other") as a guide who provides temporary, adjustable support — scaffolding — inside the child's zone of proximal development, then withdraws it as competence grows. Option (1) is closer to a transmission model, and options (3) and (4) both wrongly deny the teacher any active supporting role, which contradicts Vygotsky's social-constructivist view that learning is mediated through guided interaction.

**Q2.** Which statement about adolescence is incorrect.

**Correct answer: (4) — Children across different cultures usher in and experience adolescence in a similar manner.**

Adolescence is now understood as a socially and culturally constructed stage, not a biologically uniform experience — its length, markers, and meaning vary widely across cultures and historical periods. Options (1)–(3) are standard, accurate descriptions of adolescence (social construction, onset near puberty, multidimensional transition), which is why (4) is the one that is *not* correct.

**Q3.** Sujata and the cash-prize dance competition.

**Correct answer: (2) — Extrinsically motivated.**

Sujata's engagement is driven by an external reward (cash) rather than the inherent enjoyment of dancing, which is the textbook definition of extrinsic motivation. Intrinsic motivation (1) would mean she dances for the sheer pleasure of it; goal-orientation labels like "mastery-oriented" (3) or "failure-avoiding" (4) describe *why* someone approaches a task in terms of competence goals, not the source (external vs internal) of the drive, so they don't fit this scenario.

**Q4.** Addressing individual differences among learners.

**Correct answer: (3) — Consider differences as an obvious feature of human diversity.**

Modern inclusive pedagogy treats variation among learners as natural and expected, not as a problem to be fixed or ignored. Framing differences as "deficits" (1), being indifferent (2), or forcing uniformity (4) all work against differentiated, learner-centred teaching, whereas viewing diversity as normal is the foundation for planning differentiated instruction.

**Q5.** Piaget's view of knowledge.

**Correct answer: (1) — Constructed by the child rather than being passed passively from the environment.**

Piaget is the founder of cognitive constructivism: children actively build their own understanding through processes of assimilation and accommodation as they interact with the world. Option (3) describes Vygotsky's social/co-constructed view, not Piaget's individually-driven one, and options (2) and (4) reflect nativist and behaviourist positions respectively, both of which Piaget rejected.

**Q6.** Assessment in a progressive classroom.

**Correct answer: (1) — Dynamic.**

Progressive education favours ongoing, formative, process-oriented assessment that evolves with the learner's growth — described as "dynamic" — rather than a single fixed summative judgment (3) or a rigid, one-size-fits-all normative comparison (4). "Objective" (2) refers to scoring neutrality, not the progressive philosophy's emphasis on continuous, contextual assessment.

**Q7.** The process needed for meaning-making and storing information in memory.

**Correct answer: (1) — Attention.**

Before information can be encoded and stored in memory, a learner must first attend to it — attention is the gateway process that determines what gets processed for meaning. Decay (2) and forgetting (3) are about losing information, and retrieval (4) is about pulling information back out, so none of these fit "receiving and making meaning of" new input.

**Q8.** The question that requires analysis.

**Correct answer: (3) — How does the author use symbolism in "To Kill a Mockingbird"?**

Asking *how* a literary device works requires breaking the text into components and examining relationships — this is analysis in Bloom's taxonomy. The other three questions (plot, theme, protagonist) can be answered by simple recall or comprehension of stated facts, not by analytical reasoning.

**Q9.**

**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**

Culturally responsive teaching is built on teacher reflectiveness, and that reflectiveness is precisely what drives teachers to examine their own biases and stereotypes — so the reason directly explains the assertion. Both statements are well-established principles in inclusive, culturally responsive pedagogy.

**Q10.** Gilligan's critique of Kohlberg.

**Correct answer: (1) — Ethics of care and the feminist perspective.**

Carol Gilligan argued Kohlberg's stage model, based on studies of boys, privileged an abstract "justice" orientation and undervalued the "ethics of care" orientation more commonly expressed by girls and women — a foundational feminist critique in developmental psychology. The other options don't capture the substantive gendered-care argument that made her critique influential.

**Q11.**

**Correct answer: (2) — Both (A) and (R) are true but (R) is not the correct explanation of (A).**

Both statements are individually true: real-life, concrete experiences do give children multiple pathways to learn, and development genuinely is orderly and sequential. But the orderliness of development is not *why* concrete experience creates multiple learning pathways — that benefit comes from experiential, multisensory engagement, not from developmental sequencing, so (R) doesn't explain (A).

**Q12.** Dyslexia.

**Correct answer: (2) — neurological, link sounds with symbols.**

Dyslexia is a neurologically-based learning difficulty that specifically affects a person's ability to connect sounds (phonemes) with their written symbols (graphemes), affecting decoding and fluent reading. It is not emotional, physiological in the general sense, or sensory (like a vision or motor-coordination problem) — pinning down this precise neurological, phonological definition is what distinguishes dyslexia from other conditions on a CTET paper.

**Q13.** Meaningful learning: connecting concepts to the real world.

**Correct answer: (4) — Let the students research a current issue in small groups and present their findings to the class.**

Meaningful learning requires learners to actively construct connections between new concepts and real-world contexts — inquiry-based group research and presentation does exactly that. Memorization (1), MCQ worksheets (2), and stimulus-response conditioning (3) are all passive or rote approaches that do not build the kind of connected understanding the question describes.

**Q14.** Kohlberg's stage where reasoning revolves around ethics of law.

**Correct answer: (2) — Conventional morality.**

In Kohlberg's conventional level (stages 3 and 4), moral reasoning is organized around maintaining social order, rules, and law — "law and order" thinking is the signature of stage 4 within this level. Pre-conventional morality (1) is driven by punishment/reward, and "heteronomous"/"autonomous" morality (3, 4) are Piaget's terms, not Kohlberg's stage labels.

**Q15.** What does NOT exemplify an inclusive classroom.

**Correct answer: (4) — Encouraging competitive ethos.**

Inclusive classrooms emphasise collaboration, accommodation, and valuing diverse perspectives — a strongly competitive ethos works against inclusion because it can marginalise learners who need more time or different kinds of support. The other three options (diverse perspectives, reasonable accommodation, differentiated instruction) are all core inclusive practices.

**Q16.** Work-in-progress portfolios for formative assessment.

**Correct answer: (4) — (a), (b), (d).**

Portfolios are valued for formative assessment because they actively involve students in assessing their own work, reveal individual progress over time, and provide concrete evidence of learning — statements (a), (b), and (d). Statement (c), focusing mainly on the final product, is precisely what a "work-in-progress" portfolio does *not* do; its whole point is capturing process, not just the end product.

**Q17.** Mastery goal orientation.

**Correct answer: (2) — Focus on improving one's skills and understanding of a task.**

A mastery orientation is about self-referenced growth — getting better at the task itself — rather than about beating others (1), chasing grades/external rewards (3), or avoiding failure (4), all of which describe performance or avoidance orientations instead.

**Q18.** The teacher's role in progressive education.

**Correct answer: (2) — The teacher provides diverse materials for children to experiment and provides scaffolding as and when required.**

Progressive education positions the teacher as a facilitator who arranges rich materials and experiences for exploration while stepping in with support exactly when needed — neither an all-controlling authority (1, 3) nor a completely hands-off bystander (4).

**Q19.** Critical questions about gender representation in textbooks and media.

**Correct answer: (1) — Questioning gender stereotypes.**

Asking learners to examine how much and in what ways genders are represented is a critical pedagogy technique aimed at making stereotypical assumptions visible so they can be questioned and challenged — the opposite of reinforcing identity (3) or discouraging flexibility (4).

**Q20.** Piaget: using symbols for symbols (e.g., letting X stand for an unknown).

**Correct answer: (3) — formal operational stage; algebra and calculus.**

The capacity for abstract, symbolic reasoning about symbols themselves (algebraic variables) is a hallmark of Piaget's formal operational stage (roughly from age 11 onward), which is what makes algebra and calculus accessible. Conservation and seriation (the correct pairing for options 2 and 4) are concrete operational achievements that appear earlier and involve concrete quantities, not abstract symbol manipulation.

**Q21.** Alok's symptoms (awkward head position, squinting, red/swollen eyes, confusing letters).

**Correct answer: (1) — Visual problems.**

These are classic behavioural indicators of an undiagnosed vision problem in a child — straining posture, eye rubbing, redness, and letter confusion (often mistaken for a reading disorder) frequently trace back to uncorrected visual difficulties. They don't match the profiles for autism spectrum disorder or communication/language-comprehension disorders, which involve different clusters of behaviours.

**Q22.**

**Correct answer: (3) — (A) is true but (R) is false.**

It is true that inclusion requires adapting goals, strategies, and assessment (A). But inclusion does not aim to "eliminate all individual differences" — it aims to accommodate and value differences, not erase them, so the reason as stated misrepresents the philosophy of inclusion and is false.

**Q23.** Vygotsky and the zone of proximal development.

**Correct answer: (1) — more knowledgeable others; within.**

Vygotsky's ZPD is defined as the range of tasks a child can perform with the help of a more knowledgeable other (MKO) but not yet alone — the assistance operates *within* that zone, not outside it, which is why "more knowledgeable others; within" is the accurate pairing.

**Q24.** Helping a highly distractible student stay focused.

**Correct answer: (3) — Giving a timer to the student for self-regulation and time-management.**

Tools like timers build the student's own self-regulation skills in a supportive, non-punitive way — an evidence-based classroom accommodation for attention difficulties. Repeating the same tasks (1), simply commanding more attention (2), or resorting to punishment (4) do not teach the underlying self-management skill the student needs.

**Q25.**

**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**

Emotional attachment to school and feeling that one "matters" are strongly linked to persistence and achievement precisely because emotions play a foundational role in motivation and learning — so the reason genuinely explains the assertion here.

**Q26.** A core principle of constructivist learning theory.

**Correct answer: (3) — Students construct their own knowledge through active engagement with their environment.**

This is the defining claim of constructivism (Piagetian and social varieties alike): learners actively build understanding rather than passively absorbing transmitted facts. Options (1), (2), and (4) all describe transmission/behaviourist models that constructivism explicitly rejects.

**Q27.**

**Correct answer: (2) — Both (A) and (R) are true but (R) is not the correct explanation of (A).**

The assertion describes the principle of scaffolding — support inversely proportional to a child's current ability — which is a true and well-established teaching practice. The reason, that IQ cannot accurately measure intelligence, is also a true and widely accepted critique, but it is not what explains *why* scaffolding should fade as competence grows; that logic comes from the ZPD, not from a critique of IQ testing.

**Q28.** Characteristics of the process of socialization.

**Correct answer: (3) — (b), (c), (d).**

Socialization is complex, multidimensional, and expressed differently across cultures — but it is not a simple linear process; it unfolds through overlapping, sometimes non-sequential influences across a person's life, so (a) is excluded and (b), (c), (d) remain.

**Q29.**

**Correct answer: (1) — Both (A) and (R) are true and (R) is the correct explanation of (A).**

There genuinely is no sharp, objectively defined boundary marking entry into middle childhood or adolescence, and this is precisely *because* development is a continuous process rather than a series of discrete jumps — so the reason directly accounts for the assertion.

**Q30.** Influencing academic self-concept.

**Correct answer: (3) — Rewarding autonomy and initiative in students.**

Academic self-concept grows when students are encouraged to take initiative and act independently and are positively reinforced for it. Low expectations (whether for all students or "special" students, options 1 and 4) and punishing autonomy (2) all suppress the sense of competence that underlies a healthy self-concept.

## Part II: Mathematics and Science

**Q31.** Diagonal BD of parallelogram ABCD.

**Correct answer: (3) — 22°.**

Since AB ∥ DC with transversal BD, alternate angles give ∠ABD = ∠CDB, so 7y = 28° and y = 4. Since BC ∥ AD with transversal BD, alternate angles give ∠CBD = ∠ADB, so 12x = 60° and x = 5. Then 2x + 3y = 10 + 12 = 22°. The key idea to remember: in any parallelogram, a diagonal always creates two pairs of equal alternate angles with the parallel sides.

**Q32.** Properties of rational numbers.

**Correct answer: (1) — (a) and (c).**

Every integer is a rational number (it can be written as itself over 1), so (a) is true. Every fraction (with an integer numerator and non-zero integer denominator) is by definition rational, so (c) is true. Statement (b) is false — 0 is rational because it can be written as 0/1 — so it's excluded, leaving only (a) and (c).

**Q33.** Factorising p³x + p²(x − y) − p(y + z) − z.

**Correct answer: (3) — p²x − py − z.**

Grouping terms: p³x + p²x − p²y − py − pz − z = p²x(p+1) − py(p+1) − z(p+1) = (p+1)(p²x − py − z). The common factor (p+1) pulls out cleanly, leaving p²x − py − z as the other factor — a good reminder that grouping-and-common-factor is the go-to technique whenever four terms don't share one obvious common factor.

**Q34.** Median shift after replacing two values.

**Correct answer: (3) — 3.**

Sorting the original ten values gives 3, 7, 8, 15, 17, 21, 23, 24, 29, 30; the median (average of the 5th and 6th values) is (17+21)/2 = 19 = x. After replacing 3→33 and 8→18, the sorted data becomes 7, 15, 17, 18, 21, 23, 24, 29, 30, 33; the new median is (21+23)/2 = 22 = y. So y − x = 3. This tests whether students know that for even-sized data sets, only the two middle values matter for the median — changing values outside that middle pair doesn't affect it unless it shifts the ordering.

**Q35.** Three numbers in ratio 2:3:4, sum of cubes 33957.

**Correct answer: (2) — 63.**

Let the numbers be 2k, 3k, 4k. Their cubes sum to 8k³ + 27k³ + 64k³ = 99k³ = 33957, so k³ = 343, giving k = 7. The sum of the numbers is (2+3+4)k = 9 × 7 = 63. The trick is recognising that ratios scale cubes by the cube of the ratio, so the whole problem reduces to one cube-root calculation.

**Q36.** Vertices, edges, faces of a polyhedron.

**Correct answer: (2) — 9.**

Euler's formula for polyhedra states V − E + F = 2. With V = 10 and E = 15: 10 − 15 + F = 2, so F = 7. Then 3x − 12 = 3(7) − 12 = 9. Euler's formula is a foundational upper-primary geometry fact worth memorising cold, since it appears in many disguised forms.

**Q37.** Evaluating x/y = (−1/3)⁻³ ÷ (2/3)⁻⁴.

**Correct answer: (4) — −48/265.**

(−1/3)⁻³ = (−3)³ = −27, and (2/3)⁻⁴ = (3/2)⁴ = 81/16. So x/y = −27 ÷ 81/16 = −27 × 16/81 = −16/3, meaning y/x = −3/16. Then x/y + y/x = −16/3 − 3/16 = −256/48 − 9/48 = −265/48, and its reciprocal is −48/265. The key rule being tested is that a negative exponent flips the fraction and, crucially, that (negative base)^(odd power) stays negative.

**Q38.** Who coined the term 'Schema'.

**Correct answer: (2) — Piaget.**

Jean Piaget used "schema" to describe the mental structures or frameworks through which children organize and interpret experiences, refined via assimilation and accommodation — a core piece of his cognitive-development vocabulary distinct from Vygotsky's, Bruner's, or Van Hiele's terminology.

**Q39.** x = 1.011 + 10.11 − 12.101 + 0.1011; what must be added to reach 1.1?

**Correct answer: (1) — 1.9789.**

Computing step by step: 1.011 + 10.11 = 11.121; 11.121 − 12.101 = −0.98; −0.98 + 0.1011 = −0.8789. To reach 1.1 from −0.8789 requires adding 1.1 − (−0.8789) = 1.9789. This is really a decimal-arithmetic accuracy check — align decimal places carefully and track the sign through each step.

**Q40.** Ram knows definitions but can't classify actual numbers.

**Correct answer: (3) — Ram has good memory but lacks conceptual understanding.**

Being able to recite a definition is rote/verbal memory, while being able to correctly sort actual numbers into odd/even/prime/composite requires genuine conceptual grasp of what those definitions mean in practice. The gap between the two is a textbook sign that a learner has memorised words without internalising the underlying concept — a distinction every mathematics teacher needs to watch for in oral assessment.

**Q41.** Shopkeeper's marked price and profit percent.

**Correct answer: (3) — 23%.**

Selling at ₹324 after a 28% discount means 0.72 × MP = 324, so MP = ₹450. At an 18% discount, SP = 0.82 × 450 = ₹369. Profit = 369 − 300 = ₹69, and profit% = (69/300) × 100 = 23%. The core skill is recovering the marked price first from one discount scenario before applying the second discount.

**Q42.** Product of two rationals is −40/3; one is −5/2; where does the reciprocal of the other lie?

**Correct answer: (2) — between 9/50 and 1/5.**

The other number = (−40/3) ÷ (−5/2) = (−40/3) × (−2/5) = 16/3. Its reciprocal is 3/16 = 0.1875. Since 9/50 = 0.18 and 1/5 = 0.2, and 0.18 < 0.1875 < 0.2, the reciprocal falls in that interval. Converting all the fractions to decimals is the fastest way to locate a value within a range of fraction options.

**Q43.** Minimum common distance for steps of 70, 85, 95 cm.

**Correct answer: (2) — Concept of LCM.**

Finding the smallest distance that each person can cover in a whole number of their own steps means finding the smallest number that all three step-lengths divide into exactly — that is exactly the definition of the Least Common Multiple. HCF would instead find the largest measure that divides all three, which answers a different kind of "greatest common" question, not "smallest common distance."

**Q44.** Student's percentage-decrease error.

**Correct answer: (1) — Student is able to understand the concept of percentage but is not able to understand percentage of which quantity is to be found.**

The student correctly set up a percentage calculation but divided the new population by the original when the decrease-percentage formula requires dividing the *decrease* (28,000 − 26,500 = 1,500) by the original population, then multiplying by 100. This shows she understands "percentage" as a concept but hasn't identified the correct base quantity for this specific percentage — a very common conceptual slip when percentage-of-change problems are introduced.

**Q45.** Supplementary angles differing by 20°.

**Correct answer: (4) — 190°.**

If the two supplementary angles are p and p + 20, then p + (p + 20) = 180, giving p = 80. So 3p − 50 = 3(80) − 50 = 240 − 50 = 190°. Remembering that "supplementary" means the pair sums to 180° (as opposed to 90° for complementary) is the fact this item is really checking.

**Q46.** Inverse variation: x = 3.5 gives y = 2.4; find y when x = 5.6.

**Correct answer: (2) — 1.5.**

For inverse variation, xy stays constant: 3.5 × 2.4 = 8.4. When x = 5.6, y = 8.4 / 5.6 = 1.5. The one rule to remember is that inverse variation keeps the *product* constant, unlike direct variation, which keeps the *ratio* constant.

**Q47.** LTRs suited for visually challenged students in a mathematics classroom.

**Correct answer: (3) — (b) and (c).**

Taylor's abacus is a tactile counting device designed specifically for blind and visually impaired learners, and computers (paired with screen-reading/text-to-speech software) make mathematical content accessible non-visually — both are established accessible resources. Geogebra and spreadsheets are highly visual tools and are not the most appropriate choices for visually challenged learners without significant additional adaptation.

**Q48.** Sports-integrated pedagogy per NEP 2020.

**Correct answer: (3) — (a) and (d).**

NEP 2020 promotes sports-integrated learning as a way to make teaching-learning joyful (a) and explicitly encourages using indigenous/traditional games alongside popular sports (d) to teach subjects including mathematics. Statements (b) and (c), dismissing sports as unusable or too time-consuming, run directly against the spirit and letter of this NEP recommendation.

**Q49.** Cylinder: curved surface 4400 cm², base circumference 110 cm; find volume in m³.

**Correct answer: (2) — 0.0385.**

From circumference 2πr = 110, r = 110 × 7/(2×22) = 17.5 cm. From curved surface 2πrh = 4400, h = 4400/110 = 40 cm. Volume = πr²h = (22/7) × 17.5² × 40 = 38,500 cm³ = 0.0385 m³. The key technique is extracting r first from the circumference before using it in either the curved-surface or volume formula.

**Q50.** Cost of painting the inner surface of an open wooden box.

**Correct answer: (2) — ₹839.10.**

The box's inner dimensions shrink by twice the wood thickness (3 cm) on each of length and breadth, but only once on height (since it's open at the top): inner length = 148 − 6 = 142 cm, inner breadth = 116 − 6 = 110 cm, inner height = 83 − 3 = 80 cm. The inner surface to paint is the four inner walls plus the inner base: 2h(l+b) + lb = 2(80)(142+110) + (142×110) = 40,320 + 15,620 = 55,940 cm² ≈ 5.594 m². At ₹150/m², cost ≈ ₹839.10. The trap here is remembering that thickness reduces the *height* dimension only once (top is open) while it reduces length and breadth on both sides.

**Q51.** Congruence of triangles ABC and DEF.

**Correct answer: (4) — ∆ABC ≅ ∆FED, by AAS.**

Matching angles: ∠B = 90° = ∠E, ∠A = 40° = ∠F, so the third angles ∠C = ∠D = 50° automatically. The given equal side, BC = DE = 8 cm, sits opposite ∠A and ∠F respectively — so the correspondence is A↔F, B↔E, C↔D, i.e. ∆ABC ≅ ∆FED, proven by Angle-Angle-Side (AAS) since the equal side is not the one included between the two known angles.

**Q52.** Three consecutive integers, weighted sum 99.

**Correct answer: (3) — 20.**

Let the integers be n−1, n, n+1. Multiplying by 3, 5, 2 respectively: 3(n−1) + 5n + 2(n+1) = 99 → 10n − 1 = 99 → n = 10. So the integers are 9, 10, 11, and the sum of the first and third is 9 + 11 = 20.

**Q53.** A teacher contrasting heights of athletes with hourly train-boarding data.

**Correct answer: (4) — Teach the use of histograms for data representation.**

Continuous, grouped numerical data like a range of heights is exactly the kind of data histograms are designed to represent (frequency of values falling into class intervals), which is the pedagogical point the teacher is illustrating by pairing it with a second, similarly continuous dataset (passenger counts across time intervals).

**Q54.** Divisibility of 9471x9y2 by 72.

**Correct answer: (3) — x = 9 and y = 5.**

72 = 8 × 9, so the number must be divisible by both 8 (check the last three digits) and 9 (check the digit sum). For x=9, y=5: the last three digits are 952, divisible by 8, but the digit sum is 9+4+7+1+9+9+5+2 = 46, which is *not* divisible by 9 — so this combination fails and the statement is not true. **Q55.** Why mathematics is "a way of thinking."

**Correct answer: (3) — (a) and (c).**

Mathematics functions as a way of thinking when students engage in proof and pattern-examination (a) and apply appropriate strategies to genuinely new problems (c) — both involve active reasoning. Simply reproducing memorised formulae and symbols (b) is rote reproduction, not thinking, so it's excluded.

**Q56.** Pie chart angle for apples (44, 56, 42, 30, 38).

**Correct answer: (3) — 96°.**

The total is 44+56+42+30+38 = 210. The angle for apples is (56/210) × 360° = 96°. The general rule: each category's pie-slice angle equals (that category's value ÷ total) × 360°.

**Q57.** Trapezium area equal to rectangle area (sides in ratio 3:2).

**Correct answer: (3) — 60.**

Trapezium area = ½ × (11+25) × 12 = ½ × 36 × 12 = 216 cm². If rectangle sides are 3x and 2x, then 6x² = 216, so x² = 36, x = 6 — giving sides 18 cm and 12 cm. Perimeter = 2(18+12) = 60 cm.

**Q58.** Sum of coefficients of x² and x in (A+B+C−D).

**Correct answer: (3) — 1.**

Adding/subtracting term by term: x² terms give −2+3−4−1 = −4; x terms give 12−8−(−1) = 5; so A+B+C−D = −4x² + 5x + 31. The coefficients of x² and x are −4 and 5, summing to 1. Careful sign-tracking while combining like terms is the whole game in this kind of algebra question.

**Q59.** Not a dimension of mathematics-learning assessment.

**Correct answer: (3) — Disposition towards mathematics.**

Per the framework this exam draws on, mathematics-learning assessment is organised around dimensions like communication, patterns/procedures, and mathematical reasoning — cognitive and procedural aspects of doing mathematics. "Disposition" describes an affective attitude toward the subject rather than a directly assessed learning dimension in this framework, which is why it's flagged as the odd one out here.

**Q60.** Which configuration makes ∆PQR an obtuse scalene triangle (∠P=55°, QR=18 cm)?

**Correct answer: (2) — ∠R = 15° and PR > 18 cm.**

If ∠R = 15°, then ∠Q = 180 − 55 − 15 = 110°, which is obtuse — and since ∠Q is the largest angle, the side opposite it (PR) must be the largest side, consistent with "PR > 18 cm." All three angles (55°, 15°, 110°) are different, making the triangle scalene as well. The underlying rule to remember: in any triangle, the largest side always lies opposite the largest angle.

**Q61.** Sound-producing parts of musical instruments (strings, membrane, air column).

**Correct answer: (3) — guitar, tabla, shehnai.**

A guitar produces sound via stretched strings, a tabla via a stretched membrane, and a shehnai via a vibrating air column inside a tube — this is basic acoustics linking instrument families (chordophones, membranophones, aerophones) to their sound-producing mechanism.

**Q62.** Set of plant micronutrients.

Micronutrients are needed by plants in very small (trace) quantities and include zinc, iron, manganese, boron, copper, molybdenum, and chlorine. Potassium (option 1) is a macronutrient needed in much larger quantities, which is why that option is wrong despite containing two genuine micronutrients.

**Q63.**

Stars do appear to move east to west across the night sky, and this apparent motion is a direct consequence of the Earth's actual rotation from west to east — an observer on a rotating Earth sees the sky appear to move in the opposite direction, so the reason genuinely explains the assertion.

**Q64.** True statements about rusting of iron.

**Correct answer: (4) — (a), (b), (c).**

Rusting is a genuine chemical reaction between iron and oxygen (in the presence of moisture) (a), it releases a small amount of heat, making it exothermic (b), and it readily occurs at room temperature (c). It is not reversible (d) — once rust (hydrated iron oxide) forms, it cannot simply revert back to iron and oxygen, which is why (d) is excluded.

**Q65.** Matching substances to chemical names.

**Correct answer: (1) — (a)-(iv), (b)-(vi), (c)-(iii), (d)-(v), (e)-(i), (f)-(ii).**

**Q66.** Fact vs. law.

**Correct answer: (1) — Only (a).**

A fact is indeed an observation repeatedly verified as true — that's a correct definition. A scientific law, however, is a generalized statement or rule describing a pattern (often derived from many facts), not itself "a theory" — a theory is a broader explanatory framework, which is a distinct concept from a law, so statement (b) conflates two different terms and is false.

**Q67.** Observation vs. inference with the electroscope.

**Correct answer: (1) — (a) is an observation while (b) and (c) are inferences.**

Seeing the electroscope's leaves move apart (a) is something directly observed with the senses. Claiming both leaves "acquire similar charge" (b) or that "negative charges pass on to the electroscope" (c) both require inferring an unobservable cause (charge transfer) from the observed effect (leaf divergence) — these go beyond direct sensory observation and are therefore inferences.

**Q68.** Gases responsible for acid rain.

**Correct answer: (4) — (d) and (e).**

Sulphur dioxide and nitric oxide (nitrogen oxides), largely from burning fossil fuels, dissolve in atmospheric moisture to form sulphuric and nitric acids — the chemistry behind acid rain. Nitrogen, oxygen, and ozone are not the acid-rain-forming pollutants referred to here.

**Q69.** NOT indicative of the hypothesising skill.

**Correct answer: (3) — Using the senses to gather information.**

Using the senses to gather information describes the *observing* skill, not hypothesising. Hypothesising involves formulating enquiry-driving questions, proposing explanations grounded in a principle, and identifying variables — all forward-looking, explanatory acts distinct from the sensory data-gathering of observation.

**Q70.** Non-metals and reactions with water/acids.

**Correct answer: (3) — Both (a) and (b).**

Non-metals generally do not react with water, and they also generally do not react with acids to liberate hydrogen (unlike reactive metals) — both statements reflect standard patterns of non-metal reactivity taught at the upper-primary level.

**Q71.** Ball thrown upward: which quantities stay the same going up and coming down?

**Correct answer: Both options (1) Acceleration and (2) Force of gravity were accepted by the official key.**

Throughout the ball's flight, the only force acting (ignoring air resistance) is gravity, giving a constant downward acceleration g — this doesn't change direction or magnitude whether the ball is rising or falling. Displacement (3) changes continuously and work done by gravity (4) is positive on the way down but negative on the way up, so neither of those stays the same.

**Q72.** Kinetic energy from momentum p and mass m.

**Correct answer: (3) — p²/2m.**

Since p = mv, v = p/m. Kinetic energy = ½mv² = ½m(p/m)² = p²/(2m). This is a standard derived-formula relationship worth memorising directly: KE = p²/2m.

**Q73.** Best strategy to introduce 'chemical reactions' at upper primary.

**Correct answer: (4) — Hold a discussion on chemical reactions which students are likely to have observed in their lives.**

Starting from learners' own everyday observations (cooking, rusting, burning) builds new concepts on familiar experience — a constructivist entry point appropriate for this age group. Explaining valence electrons (1) or writing symbolic equations (2) are too abstract for the upper primary stage, and merely defining-and-exemplifying (3) skips the experiential grounding that makes the concept meaningful.

**Q74.** Animals showing external fertilization.

**Correct answer: (4) — Frogs, fish, starfish.**

All three release their gametes into the external environment (usually water), where fertilization occurs outside the body. Hens, lizards, and butterflies (appearing in options 1–3) undergo internal fertilization, which is why any set containing them is wrong.

**Q75.** Poorly constructed matching test item.

Having a slightly unequal number of entries in the two columns is actually a *recommended* test-construction practice (it reduces the chance of guessing correctly by elimination on the last item), so it is not a valid reason to call the item poorly constructed. The genuine flaws here are that plausible distractors are limited, matches are easily guessable, and the entries are internally inconsistent — those (options 1, 3, 4) are legitimate criticisms.

**Q76.**

**Correct answer: (1) — (A) and (R) are both correct and (R) is the correct explanation for (A).**

Raisins swelling in plain water is a genuine example of osmosis (water moving into the cells from a region of lower solute concentration outside to higher solute concentration inside), and this happens precisely because plant cell membranes are selectively permeable, allowing water but not all solutes to pass — so the reason correctly explains the assertion.

**Q77.** Best strategy to introduce 'deficiency diseases' at upper primary.

**Correct answer: (2) — Arrange for a doctor's prescription pertaining to a deficiency disease and hold a discussion around it.**

Using a real, concrete artefact (a prescription) as a discussion anchor makes an abstract topic tangible and discussion-driven, matching how upper-primary science teaching is meant to connect content to real life. Defining terms first (1) or simply listing nutrients and functions (3) are drier, more didactic entry points, and discussing diseases generally (4) doesn't focus specifically on deficiency.

**Q78.** Explaining 'science promotes scepticism.'

**Correct answer: (1) — Scientists judge the validity of a claim based on objective empirical evidence.**

Scientific scepticism means withholding acceptance of any claim until it is supported by objective evidence — a demand for verification, not blind trust or blind distrust. It is distinct from "not trusting other scientists" (3), which describes cynicism rather than the evidence-based scepticism that defines the scientific method.

**Q79.** Forces on a stationary box on a table.

**Correct answer: (3) — A pair of balanced forces acts on it.**

The box's weight (mg = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N downward) is exactly balanced by the table's normal reaction force (5 N upward) — this balanced pair is why the box stays at rest, per Newton's first law, rather than there being "no force" (which would ignore gravity entirely) or an unbalanced force (which would cause acceleration).

**Q80.** X sees around corners, based on principle Y.

**Correct answer: (3) — Periscope, reflection.**

A periscope uses two parallel mirrors (or prisms) angled at 45° to reflect light around obstacles, letting the user see around corners or over obstructions — this is a direct application of the law of reflection, not refraction or dispersion.

**Q81.** X and Y, thick black road-metalling liquids.

**Correct answer: (1) — Bitumen, Coal Tar.**

Bitumen is a petroleum-derived residue used in road surfacing, while coal tar is a similar thick black liquid derived from the destructive distillation of coal — the two are commonly confused but come from different raw materials, which is exactly what this question tests.

**Q82.** Not desirable in a classroom demonstration.

**Correct answer: (2) — Emphasise beforehand the linkages that students are required to make.**

Telling students the conclusions/linkages in advance defeats the purpose of a demonstration, which is meant to let students observe and reason toward those connections themselves. Rehearsing beforehand (1), supplementing with explanation (3), and following up with discussion (4) are all sound demonstration practices that support (rather than pre-empt) student reasoning.

**Q83.** Correct statement about fertilisers.

**Correct answer: (1) — Fertilisers are richer in specific nutrients as compared to manure.**

Chemical fertilisers are manufactured to deliver concentrated amounts of specific nutrients (like nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium), unlike manure, which is nutrient-dilute but rich in organic matter. Fertilisers do not add humus, do not particularly improve water-holding capacity, and do not increase beneficial soil microbes — those benefits belong to organic manure instead.

**Q84.** Matching plants to their mode of asexual reproduction.

**Correct answer: (1) — (a)-(v), (b)-(i), (c)-(ii), (d)-(iii).**

Rose is commonly propagated vegetatively (through cuttings), yeast reproduces by budding, ferns reproduce via spore formation, and Spirogyra reproduces by fragmentation of its filaments — each is a standard example used in NCERT-level biology to illustrate a distinct mode of asexual reproduction.

**Q85.** Bacterial plant diseases.

**Correct answer: (2) — Only (b).**

Citrus canker is caused by a bacterium (Xanthomonas). Rust of wheat is a fungal disease, and yellow vein mosaic of bhindi (okra) is caused by a virus transmitted by whiteflies — neither is bacterial, which is why only (b) qualifies.

**Q86.** Max–min resistance from three 1/3 Ω resistors.

**Correct answer: (2) — 8/9 Ω.**

Maximum resistance (all in series) = 1/3 + 1/3 + 1/3 = 1 Ω. Minimum resistance (all in parallel): 1/R = 3/(1/3) = 9, so R = 1/9 Ω. The difference is 1 − 1/9 = 8/9 Ω. Series always gives the maximum combined resistance and parallel always gives the minimum — a rule worth internalising rather than re-deriving each time.

**Q87.** Skills used when comparing a boat, fish, and aeroplane's shape and movement.

**Correct answer: (2) — (b) and (c).**

Students first *observe* the shapes and movement patterns of the three objects, then use *inductive reasoning* to generalise a pattern (e.g., streamlined shapes suit movement through a fluid medium) from these specific instances — classifying and measuring are not the primary skills this particular activity calls for.

**Q88.** What seed dispersal does NOT help with.

**Correct answer: (1) — Over crowding.**

As worded in the original item, seed dispersal is credited with helping plants avoid competition, invade new habitats, and (per the official key) is *not* credited with helping in "over crowding" — dispersal exists precisely to spread seeds away from the parent plant and reduce crowding, so listing "over crowding" as a benefit (without the word "avoid") is the mismatched, excluded option; producing new genetic varieties is a separate process (sexual reproduction/mutation), unrelated to the mechanism of seed dispersal itself.

**Q89.** Why Newton's theory of gravitation is regarded as excellent.

**Correct answer: (1) — It explains a larger number of observations by making fewer assumptions.**

A hallmark of a strong scientific theory is explanatory power combined with parsimony — accounting for a wide range of phenomena (falling objects, planetary orbits, tides) using a small, simple set of assumptions, rather than being judged "excellent" merely for its age, mathematical form, or the amount of experimentation behind it.

**Q90.** Rayon: synthetic and free of natural raw material?

**Correct answer: (2) — S1 is true and S2 is false.**

Rayon is classified as a man-made (synthetic/regenerated) fibre, so S1 is true. However, it is manufactured from cellulose sourced from wood pulp — a natural raw material — so S2, which claims no natural raw material is used, is false. This distinguishes rayon (semi-synthetic, natural-source-based) from fully synthetic fibres like nylon, which are made entirely from petrochemicals.

When a variation in language is tied to a specific social group or community within the *same geographic area* (rather than a different region), it is termed a social dialect (sociolect), distinguishing it from a regional dialect, which varies by place rather than community.

BICS refers to the conversational, contextually-supported language used in everyday social interaction, which develops faster than the more abstract, decontextualised academic language (CALP) needed for content-area learning.

**Q109.** Discussing a text from the lens of power and justice.

**Correct answer: (1) — Critical literacy skills.**

Reading texts specifically to uncover underlying power structures, justice, and ideology is the defining goal of critical literacy — teaching learners to read *against* as well as *with* the text.

**Q110.** Procedural knowledge in grammar teaching.

**Correct answer: (1) — Knowing how to do the grammatical item.**

Procedural knowledge is "knowing how" — the ability to actually use a grammatical structure in real communication — as distinct from declarative knowledge, which is "knowing that" (facts and rules *about* the item, described in option 2).

**Q111.** Pair activity: reading and writing sentences from a ten-sentence story.

**Q112.** NOT true of Sign Language.

**Q113.** True statement about the three-language formula.

The three-language formula prescribes starting schooling with the child's mother tongue, home language, or regional language as the first language, then progressively adding two more — not English or Hindi by default, which rules out the other options.

**Q114.** Who remedial teaching is for.

**Correct answer: (3) — Learners and teachers to understand and modify/improve their teaching/learning strategies.**

Remedial teaching is a two-way diagnostic-and-adjustment process — it's not just about "fixing" learners, but about both learners and teachers understanding gaps and adapting their strategies together, which is broader than either option (1) or (2) alone.

**Q115.** Teacher + four students, rotating roles, dialogue on text content improving comprehension.

**Correct answer: (2) — Reciprocal Teaching.**

Reciprocal teaching is a well-known comprehension strategy in which small groups take turns performing specific roles (predicting, questioning, clarifying, summarising) around a shared text, generating dialogue that scaffolds comprehension within the ZPD.

**Q116.** Telling a story in the languages learners know, then in the classroom's common language.

**Correct answer: (1) — Translanguaging.**

Deliberately drawing on learners' full linguistic repertoires — moving between whichever languages they know before consolidating in a shared classroom language — is the pedagogical practice known as translanguaging.

**Q117.** Reading a newspaper item for specific information.

**Correct answer: (3) — Scanning.**

Scanning is reading quickly to locate specific pieces of information (like a fact or name), as opposed to skimming, which is reading quickly to get a general overview of the whole text.

**Q118.** 'Interlingual identification.'

**Correct answer: (1) — Learner's judgment that something in the native language and something in the target language are similar.**

This term describes the learner's perception of similarity between a first-language and target-language feature, which can lead to positive transfer or, when the perceived similarity is false, to errors — but the definition itself is about perceived *similarity*, not interference or differing ability.

**Q119.** Shreya's Punjabi, English, and Tamil.

**Correct answer: (1) — Her Punjabi and Tamil come under language acquisition and English comes under language learning.**

Punjabi (her mother tongue) and Tamil (picked up informally from her neighbourhood through natural exposure) were acquired subconsciously through use — the hallmark of language acquisition — while English, studied formally as a school subject, was consciously learned, matching the classic acquisition-vs-learning distinction.

**Q120.** NOT a purpose of extensive reading.

**Correct answer: (3) — Reading for learning grammar.**

Extensive reading is meant for pleasure, appreciation, and building general fluency/extrapolation skills through large volumes of enjoyable reading — it is not designed as a vehicle for the focused, analytic study of grammar rules, which belongs to intensive reading or direct grammar instruction instead.

गद्यांश के अनुसार "योग में इस प्राण को बल देने के लिए अनेक अभ्यास हैं" — अर्थात् प्राण-शक्ति बढ़ाने के अभ्यास योग के अंतर्गत आते हैं।

**Q132.** प्राण वायु शरीर के हर अंग में कैसे बहती है।

**सही उत्तर: (1) — व्यवस्थित।**

गद्यांश के अनुसार अच्छे स्वास्थ्य के लिए प्राण को हर अंग की ओर "संतुलित और व्यवस्थित होकर" बहना चाहिए — असंतुलन/अव्यवस्था ही रोग का कारण बताई गई है।

**Q133.** रोग का कारण।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — प्राण वायु का अंग विशेष में असंतुलन।**

गद्यांश स्पष्ट करता है कि "बीमारी और कुछ भी नहीं, बल्कि प्राण का उस अंग विशेष में असंतुलित हो जाना ही है" — अर्थात् रोग किसी विशेष अंग में प्राण के असंतुलन से उत्पन्न होता है, न कि प्राण वायु के अधिक बढ़ने या शारीरिक शक्ति घटने से।

**Q134.** अच्छे स्वास्थ्य का संबंध किससे है।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — प्राण वायु।**

गद्यांश के अनुसार स्वास्थ्य का अर्थ ही है प्राण वायु का शरीर के सभी अंगों में समुचित प्रवाह — इसलिए अच्छा स्वास्थ्य सीधे प्राण वायु की स्थिति से जुड़ा है।

**Q135.** 'बुद्धि' + 'इक' प्रत्यय।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — बौद्धिक।**

संस्कृत की वृद्धि संधि नियम के अनुसार 'बुद्धि' में 'इक' प्रत्यय जोड़ने पर स्वर परिवर्तन (उ→औ) होकर शब्द 'बौद्धिक' बनता है — यह एक मानक तद्धित प्रत्यय-निर्माण का उदाहरण है।

**Q136.** ड्रिल और भाषा के औपचारिक गुणधर्म में निपुणता पर बल देने वाली विधि।

**सही उत्तर: (1) — श्रव्य भाषावाद (ऑडियोलिंग्वलिज़्म)।**

ऑडियोलिंग्वल विधि की पहचान ही सघन मौखिक ड्रिल और वाक्य-प्रतिरूपों (structural patterns) की यांत्रिक निपुणता पर बल देना है, जबकि व्याकरण-अनुवाद विधि नियम और अनुवाद पर केंद्रित होती है, अनुदेशात्मक ड्रिल पर नहीं — इसलिए यहाँ सही उत्तर ऑडियोलिंग्वलिज़्म है।

**Q137.** एल्म की चार भाषाओं का प्रयोग—स्थिति के अनुसार बदलना।

**सही उत्तर: (1) — परभाषिक (ट्रांसलिंग्युअल) योग्यता।**

एल्म श्रोता/संदर्भ के अनुसार अपने पूरे बहुभाषिक भंडार में से उपयुक्त भाषाओं का चयन करता है (घर में चारों, स्कूल में तीन) — यह क्षमता कि वक्ता संदर्भानुसार अपने भाषिक संसाधनों को लचीले ढंग से प्रयोग करे, परभाषिक (ट्रांसलिंग्युअल) योग्यता कहलाती है।

**Q138.** भाषा अधिगम के बारे में सही कथन।

**सही उत्तर: (3) — सहजात/प्रथम भाषा का ज्ञान द्वितीय भाषा के अधिगम को समर्थित करता है।**

आधुनिक भाषा-अधिगम सिद्धांत मानता है कि प्रथम भाषा का ज्ञान (जैसे अवधारणात्मक व व्याकरणिक समझ) सकारात्मक स्थानांतरण (positive transfer) के माध्यम से द्वितीय भाषा सीखने में सहायक होता है, बाधक नहीं — यह पुरानी "हस्तक्षेप" वाली धारणा से आगे की समझ है।

**Q139.** चम्मच, प्लेट, डिब्बे आदि वस्तुएँ कक्षा में लाना — भाषा शिक्षण के संदर्भ में इन्हें क्या कहेंगे।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — वास्तविक वस्तुएँ (रीयलिया)।**

वास्तविक जीवन की मूर्त वस्तुओं को कक्षा में लाकर शब्दावली और भाषा सिखाने के लिए प्रयोग करना 'रीयलिया' (Realia) कहलाता है — यह भाषा शिक्षण में एक मान्य दृश्य-स्पर्शीय सहायक सामग्री की श्रेणी है।

**Q140.** घोषणात्मक ज्ञान क्या है।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — व्याकरणिक तत्वों के बारे में ज्ञान।**

घोषणात्मक ज्ञान (Declarative knowledge) का अर्थ है किसी नियम/तत्व के "बारे में जानना" (जानना कि क्या है), जबकि प्रक्रियात्मक ज्ञान का अर्थ है उस तत्व का व्यावहारिक प्रयोग "कैसे करना है" यह जानना — दोनों में यही मूल अंतर है।

**Q141.** विषय के विपरीत दृष्टिकोण से परिचय कराने की प्रक्रिया किसका विकास करती है।

**सही उत्तर: (3) — समालोचनात्मक साक्षरता।**

किसी विषय के विरुद्ध या वैकल्पिक दृष्टिकोण से परिचित कराना विद्यार्थियों को पाठ को आलोचनात्मक ढंग से पढ़ने, प्रश्न उठाने और गहराई से समझने के लिए प्रेरित करता है — यही समालोचनात्मक साक्षरता (Critical Literacy) का मूल उद्देश्य है।

**Q142.** 'परभाषा व्यवहार' (ट्रांसलैंग्युजिंग) क्या है।

**सही उत्तर: (3) — एक कक्षा में एक से अधिक भाषाओं को संसाधन के रूप में इस्तेमाल करने की शिक्षण शास्त्रीय प्रक्रिया है।**

ट्रांसलैंग्युजिंग का अर्थ है शिक्षार्थियों की समस्त भाषिक क्षमताओं को शिक्षण-अधिगम में संसाधन के रूप में सहर्ष स्वीकार करना और उपयोग में लाना, न कि केवल एक भाषा से दूसरी में अनुवाद करना या भाषा-नीति संबंधी निर्णय।

**Q143.** भाषा को अच्छी तरह लिखना सीखने की प्रक्रियाएँ (सही क्रम)।

**सही उत्तर: (4) — मानस मंथन – मुख्य बिन्दु नोट करना – रूपरेखा तैयार करना – प्रारूप लिखना – संपादन – अन्तिम स्वरूप देना।**

लेखन-प्रक्रिया का मानक क्रम है: विचार-मंथन से मुख्य बिंदु एकत्र करना, फिर उनकी रूपरेखा बनाना, उसके आधार पर प्रारूप (ड्राफ्ट) लिखना, फिर संपादन करना और अंत में अंतिम स्वरूप देना — यह क्रम प्रक्रिया-आधारित लेखन शिक्षण का आधार है।

**Q144.** व्याख्यान से मुख्य बिंदु समझना और लिखना — कौशल।

**सही उत्तर: (1) — अध्ययन कौशल।**

व्याख्यान सुनते हुए मुख्य विचार पहचानना, नोट्स बनाना और सार ग्रहण करना 'अध्ययन कौशल' (Study Skill) की श्रेणी में आता है, जो केवल श्रवण-कौशल से आगे बढ़कर सूचना के संगठन और चयन की क्षमता को शामिल करता है।

**Q145.** पत्र-लेखन प्रश्न किस प्रारूप (टाइपोलॉजी) का है।

**सही उत्तर: (3) — पाठ का विस्तार करने वाला प्रश्न (एक्स्ट्रापोलेटिव)।**

पढ़ी गई कहानी के आधार पर एक नई, संबंधित किंतु पाठ से आगे की स्थिति (पत्र लिखना) रचने के लिए कहना पाठ की सीमाओं से आगे बढ़कर विस्तार करने को प्रेरित करता है — यही एक्स्ट्रापोलेटिव प्रश्न की पहचान है, केवल भावनात्मक अभिव्यक्ति (Expressive) से थोड़ा भिन्न इसलिए कि यह पाठ-आधारित नई रचना की माँग करता है।

**Q146.** पाँच वाक्य दो बार पढ़ना और समूह में पुनर्लेखन करना — गतिविधि का नाम।

**आधिकारिक उत्तर कुंजी में विकल्प (2) अनुच्छेद श्रुतलेख और (3) सामूहिक श्रुतलेख — दोनों को स्वीकार किया गया।**

यह गतिविधि 'डिक्टोग्लॉस' तकनीक है, जिसमें छात्र सुने गए पाठ को शब्दशः नहीं बल्कि अर्थ के करीब पुनर्रचित करते हैं — चूँकि यह कार्य समूह में सामूहिक रूप से किया जाता है और एक अनुच्छेद पर आधारित है, इसलिए दोनों नाम ("अनुच्छेद श्रुतलेख" और "सामूहिक श्रुतलेख") को उचित माना गया।

**Q147.** अकादमिक भाषा या विषयों के लिए आवश्यक भाषा-निपुणता।

**सही उत्तर: (4) — संज्ञानात्मक अकादमिक भाषा निपुणता (CALP)।**

CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency) विशेष रूप से पाठ्यक्रम-विषयों को समझने और उन पर चर्चा करने के लिए आवश्यक अमूर्त, विषय-विशिष्ट भाषा-दक्षता को दर्शाता है, जबकि BICS दैनिक सामाजिक बातचीत की भाषा-दक्षता है।

**Q148.** भाषा शिक्षा पर NEP 2020 के संदर्भ में गलत कथन।

**सही उत्तर: (1) — त्रिभाषा सूत्र के अन्तर्गत शास्त्रीय भाषा का अध्ययन।**

त्रिभाषा सूत्र की मूल संरचना मातृभाषा/घर की भाषा, एक अन्य भारतीय भाषा और अंग्रेजी पर आधारित है; शास्त्रीय भाषाओं (जैसे संस्कृत, तमिल) का अध्ययन NEP 2020 में एक अलग, वैकल्पिक अनुशंसा के रूप में आता है, न कि त्रिभाषा सूत्र के मूल ढाँचे के अंतर्गत — इसलिए यह कथन गलत है।

**Q149.** त्रिभाषा सूत्र के संबंध में सही कथन।

**सही उत्तर: (4) — सभी बच्चे अपनी विद्यालयी शिक्षा मातृभाषा/घर की भाषा में शुरू करते हैं और दसवीं पूरी करने तक कम से कम दो और भाषाएँ सीखते हैं।**

त्रिभाषा सूत्र (और NEP 2020 का समर्थन) स्पष्ट रूप से मातृभाषा/घर की भाषा में शिक्षा प्रारंभ करने की अनुशंसा करता है, अंग्रेजी माध्यम, राज्य भाषा-मात्र, या हिंदी-मात्र से आरंभ करने की नहीं — इसके बाद क्रमशः दो और भाषाएँ जोड़ी जाती हैं।

**Q150.** अधिगम प्रतिफल (Learning Outcomes) शिक्षार्थियों को क्या समर्थ बनाते हैं।

**सही उत्तर: (2) — दक्षताओं को मापन के संदर्भ में प्राप्त करना।**

अधिगम प्रतिफल विशिष्ट, मापन-योग्य दक्षताओं (competencies) के रूप में परिभाषित किए जाते हैं जिन्हें शिक्षार्थी से प्राप्त करने की अपेक्षा की जाती है — यह केवल व्याकरणिक ज्ञान या भाषिक तथ्यों तक सीमित संकीर्ण परिभाषा से व्यापक है।
