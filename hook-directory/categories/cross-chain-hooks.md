# Cross-Chain Hooks

**150 hook projects.**

## The problem, explained simply

Liquidity for the same asset (say, USDC) is scattered across dozens of separate blockchains and rollups — Ethereum mainnet, Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, Unichain, and more. Each chain has its own Uniswap pools with their own separate liquidity. This is called **liquidity fragmentation**, and it creates real problems:

- A trader on chain A can't directly use liquidity sitting on chain B — the two AMMs don't know about each other.
- Moving assets between chains ("bridging") is slow, often takes minutes, and has historically been one of the most exploited categories of smart contract in all of crypto (bridge hacks have cost billions of dollars).
- Because liquidity is split into many small pools instead of one deep pool, prices are less efficient, spreads are wider, and it's easier for the price of the "same" asset to diverge slightly between chains, which is itself an arbitrage/MEV opportunity, not a feature.

Unichain (an Ethereum L2 built by the Uniswap team, referenced heavily throughout this dataset) is itself partly a response to this — it's designed to eventually enable faster, more native cross-chain liquidity movement.

## Why so many teams attack this

Cross-chain is baked into the infrastructure this cohort was building against — Chainlink CCIP (Cross-Chain Interoperability Protocol) and Unichain were both frequently promoted/sponsored integrations across UHI cohorts, so a large share of submissions specifically target "how do I make swaps/orders/liquidity work across more than one chain" as a way to use those sponsor technologies.

## Common approaches seen in this dataset

1. **Cross-chain limit orders / order redemption** — place an order on one chain, have it executed and settled on another, using a messaging layer like Chainlink CCIP to carry the instruction and proof across chains.
2. **Unichain-native hooks** — hooks specifically designed around Unichain's fast-finality properties to make cross-chain-ish behavior (e.g. instant redemption, tight cross-chain arbitrage) more practical than on general-purpose L1/L2s.
3. **Omni-liquidity / liquidity aggregation across pools and chains** — dynamically allocate a single logical liquidity position across multiple chains to minimize slippage and maximize fee capture wherever demand currently is.
4. **Cross-chain arbitrage/bridging toolkits** — instead of hiding cross-chain price differences, some hooks explicitly build tools to find and execute on them (turning fragmentation into a feature for the operator, though this is really just professionalized arbitrage).
5. **LST/LRT cross-chain unification** — liquid staking and restaking tokens (see `staking-restaking.md`) are especially fragmented since almost every L2 has its own wrapped version; several hooks specifically try to unify liquidity for these.

## Prior art / reference material

- Chainlink CCIP (Cross-Chain Interoperability Protocol) documentation — the standard messaging layer most cross-chain hooks in this dataset build on.
- Unichain documentation (Uniswap Labs) — the L2 whose fast-finality and native cross-chain design several hooks are built specifically for.
- General cross-chain bridge security research (e.g. post-mortems of major bridge hacks like Wormhole, Ronin) — the implicit risk backdrop that motivates "safer" cross-chain settlement designs.
